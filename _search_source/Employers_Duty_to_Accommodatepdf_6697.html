Employer’s Duty to Accommodate Duty to Accommodate • There must be a ‘disability’ – ‘Disability’ means any previous or existing mental or physical disability and includes disfigurement and previous dependence on alcohol or a drug. – Ordinarily requires some severity, permanence and persistence Duty to Accommodate • Some examples: – Drug and alcohol dependency – HIV positive/AIDS – Depression – Physical symptoms of stress – Chronic fatigue syndrome – Diabetes – Excess weight Duty to Accommodate • Duty to inquire: – Duty to enquire trigger where employer knowns or ought to know, employee may need accommodation – Express request not required – Employer must be alive to signals (reason for developing and attendance management policy) Duty to Accommodate • Requires a thorough assessment of the workplace and the employee capabilities • May require expert opinion or advice • Reasonable accommodation is required – not perfect accommodation Duty to Accommodate • Duty to accommodate only to point of undue hardship: – Financial cost of accommodation – Impact on other employees’ rights/morale – Disruption of the collective agreement – Size and nature of the operation – Interchangeability of workforce – Bundling – Impact on safety – on accommodated employee and others Duty to Accommodate • Hydro Quebec, 2008 SCC 43: – Employee had physical and mental problems – Had missed 960 days of work over seven and a half years (1994-2001) Duty to Accommodate • Hydro Quebec: – Standard of proving undue hardship: • In case of chronic absenteeism, where employer shows, despite accommodation measures, employee unable to attend for reasonable foreseeable future, undue hardship established. Duty to Accommodate • Hydro-Quebec: – No duty to change working conditions in a ‘fundamental way’ – No need to ‘excessively hamper the business’ – Doesn’t ‘completely alter the essence of the contract of employment’
