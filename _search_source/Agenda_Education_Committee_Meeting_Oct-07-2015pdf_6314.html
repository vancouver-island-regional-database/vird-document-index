Page 6 of 51 7.1 Focus Schools Initiative: A Year In Review The following report provides a high-level summary review of the 2014-2015 Focus Schools Initiative within NLPS. The district is in its third year of implementation of its Response to Intervention (RtI) framework and is seeking to create a sustainable and scalable tiered system of supports for all students in the area of reading. An important aspect of this RtI framework is to increase the use of evidence-based practices within regular instruction. When a significant population of students is at risk it is important to focus on improving the intensity of instruction to improve outcomes. The successful implementation of the district’s RtI framework is key to this Focus School Initiative. Since the implementation of the district’s Response to Intervention (RtI) framework in 2011, principals have been asked to maintain their managerial role while at the same time, become instructional leaders, change agents, capacity builders and facilitators in order to lead the implementation of RtI in their schools. Within NLPS, like most districts, there are schools that experience more challenges and vulnerabilities than others. These challenges often present as social and/or economic challenges. In NLPS we have a variety of socio-economic areas. However, we have a distinct group of schools that experience greater than average challenges, in this regard. In addition, many of these schools also have a higher than average Aboriginal student population. We know that currently Aboriginal students neither participate nor succeed at the same rate as their non-Aboriginal counterparts, in school. The history of colonization in Canada and in particular, residential schools continues to have a huge impact on Canada’s Aboriginal people. Colonization sought to forcibly assimilate Aboriginal people into ‘mainstream’ society. This forced assimilation has left a country of people disconnected from family, culture, language and pride in who they are as Aboriginal people. These aspects of disconnection affect the functioning and/or success in every aspect of society. We also know that reading rates are a critical determinant in school success as well as a clear indicator for life chances. Reading has an impact on every part of Canadian life. Our democracy, our economy, and the quality of our daily lives are all enhanced by reading well and critically. Literacy has become an essential part of the fabric of modern societies, a thread that links all aspects of life and living in our contemporary world. Its reach is extensive and complex, influencing how fully and effectively a person is able to engage in the social and economic life of his or her community. 2014-2015 Focus Schools Report, Department of Learning Services 1 Page 7 of 51 7.1 CCL – Canadian Council on Learning http://www.cclcca.ca/CCL/Topic/Literacy/WhatisLiteracy.html#why Provincial assessment and report card data from 2013 indicate that the percent of students underperforming in grade 4 reading in each of these schools ranged from 70% - 33%. Needless to say, these outcomes have resulted in a sense of urgency to develop and maintain effective reading instruction at these schools. NLPS Department of Learning Services (DLS) considered these factors and reviewed our own academic data. The data identified a number of schools that struggle with the factors mentioned above. DLS wanted to support these schools in an explicit, effective way that directly impacted student learning. Vulnerability factors considered, five schools were invited to participate in this initiative. Why we embarked on the Focus Schools project? The Department of Learning Services brought the Focus Schools together to participate in a planning session around the development of a primary level (K-3), early intervention focus school initiative aimed to provide ‘21st Century teaching’ models that are centred around a belief that all students can learn at high levels – including those schools within our district that have a higher population of vulnerable students. Principals at the Focus Schools agreed to the initiative, short term as a measure to increase the capacity of the educators in these schools and assure that all students K – 3 are confident successful readers. Focus Schools Vision: In collaboration with five school communities, utilizing the districts’ RTI/PLC framework, we will work to significantly impact student achievement in the Kindergarten – Grade 3. 1 Who participated & what were the project goals? 1 Project Team Adopted Vision Spring 2014 2014-2015 Focus Schools Report, Department of Learning Services 2 Page 8 of 51 7.1 Five schools participated in the project: Bayview Elementary, Fairview Elementary, Georgia Avenue Elementary, Brechin Elementary and North Oyster Elementary. The criteria to be considered a ‘focus school’ participant included those schools that hold a higher vulnerability index and a higher Aboriginal student population. A Focus Schools project team was identified, comprising of school staff (principals, itinerate staff, teachers, Supervisor of Instruction and Director of Instruction) who worked together to develop the following project Goals2: 1. Develop and expand current school level initiatives that are evidenced-based 2. Establish a clearly defined RTI process (Tiers 1,2 & 3) for reading and communicate those practices to all district schools 3. Promote principles of self-regulated learning to positively impact all areas of student learning 4. Increase the achievement of Aboriginal students; close the discrepancy of achievement and prevent the overrepresentation of Aboriginal students in special needs categories To support the project’s goal number 4 pertaining to Aboriginal students, the project team identified that all good teaching and reading needs to be the same – what needs to be different is the knowledge of history, perspective, and creating a sense of belonging in schools. In support of this goal, the team identified a specialized Aboriginal PLC that would meet regularly to discuss specifically Aboriginal student data throughout the 20142015 year. How the Project was implemented What was occurring in the district in a parallel process was the creation of a reading assessment, which eventually evolved into creating the NLPS Reading Assessment – a formative assessment. Although we were looking for summative data to make informed decisions at the district level, what evolved out of this assessment process was a formative assessment that was far more useful to teachers in classrooms. The district made a commitment to not collect reading data at the district level however, Focus Schools principals worked together around the collection and analysis of their own specific data for the purposes of the Focus Schools project. Principals agreed that the focus school model is meant to be a shorter term measure to increase the capacity of the educators in these schools and assure that all students k – 3 are confident successful readers. The Focus Schools Initiative is focused on increasing the capacity of the educators onsite. Any extra supports put in place were to be viewed as temporary and as capacity 2 Focus Schools Adopted Goals Fall 2014 2014-2015 Focus Schools Report, Department of Learning Services 3 Page 9 of 51 7.1 increased, student achievement increased, these supports would eventually be discontinued. The Focus Schools priority scope3 included:      RTI: Tangible/Living Modeling differentiation Reading and SRL: Limiting our focus Sustainable practises Transparency The Focus Schools were allotted extra supports to fulfil the project vision.  Firstly, all principals were ensured 1.0 FTE of administration time.  Itinerant staff FTE was increased and aligned to exclusively concentrate efforts on the focus schools, thus allowing for in-depth collaboration, consistency and networking across the 5 Focus Schools.  Each school was given a .5 reading teacher whose purpose was to provide support in tiers 1, 2 & 3 in the area of reading with an overarching goal of continuously increasing the capacity of the educators in the building.  Another support allotted.5 district support through Aboriginal Education and Self-Regulated Learning.  Financial support allocated for educational resources. Regular meetings were held throughout the 2014/15 school year. Larger meetings with all involved staff were held, smaller meetings with principals were held, individual meetings with principals were held, regular meetings with the reading teachers were held, and monthly Aboriginal PLC meetings were held. Meetings were always focused around What’s working, What needs tweaking, and What’s next? What is the data telling us so far? The goals/focus’ of the initiative will remain the same for the 2015/16 school year: K-3 reading, SRL and Aboriginal student achievement. District supports will remain the same, with the exception of the 0.5 FTE – District Aboriginal Education Enhancement Coordinator/District Coordinator of Self-Regulated Learning. These types of support will still be available, however no specific FTE will be allotted to this initiative. We found that these resources were under-utilized, therefore we have pulled this 0.5FTE mandate out of the initiative During the project team year-end debrief the team decided to amend goal #3: Promote principles of self-regulated learning to positively impact all areas of student learning to better reflect the key components of SRL. The goal has been amended as follows: Promote the principles of SRL by: integrating social/emotional learning throughout the day; promoting meta-cognition through planning, self-monitoring and reflecting on process. The team wants to ensure SRL becomes more tangible with a stronger emphasis placed on promoting the principals of SRL, to positively impact all areas of student learning. Building from a legacy of learning and practice from the early years sector, the following “Key Service Features” characterize the priorities of a BC Early Years Centre. BC Early Years Centres offer a menu of early years programs and services through a single point of access, delivered in a manner that is integrated and efficient for families with young children. Establishing and maintaining a BC Early Years Centre requires shared responsibility between government, public systems and communities. Involvement of various partners at all levels such as health authorities, school districts, MCFD regional offices, community agencies, and local governments will support successful outcomes for young children and their families. The PRIMARY purpose of the swim program is to improve and/or maintain the physical, mental, and emotional functioning of our students with physical disabilities and mobility issues. Can a student without a physical disability take part in the Swim Program?
