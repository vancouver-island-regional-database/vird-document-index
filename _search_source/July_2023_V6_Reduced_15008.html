SIGNS WERE PLACED ALONG THE BEACH 04 National Indigenous PEOPLES DAY CELEBRATION Indigenous communities across the nation come together to commemorate National Indigenous Peoples Day. which featured traditional prayers and speeches from community leaders. Vibrant colors, rhythmic drumbeats, and an atmosphere filled with pride and unity marked the National Indigenous Peoples Day celebration held Wednesday, June 21 in Ladysmith. “We are coming together as a community to build relationships and form bonds that will carry us through the challenge of our time. As a generation, we work through the challenges of our past but embrace a bright future together. We are here today to share in fun, food and celebration of culture and traditions so that we can learn more about each other and embrace the path that is in front of us.” -Mayor Stone The event, organized by Stz’uminus First Nation, took place at Transfer Beach and drew a diverse crowd of both Indigenous and non-Indigenous attendees. The festivities began early in the morning, as participants and onlookers gathered for the official opening ceremony, 05 Throughout the day, the event offered a wide array of activities, performances, and educational opportunities, allowing attendees to engage with and appreciate the Indigenous culture. Artisans and crafters proudly displayed their creations, including intricate beadwork, traditional carving, and exquisite handmade jewelry. Visitors had the chance to purchase these unique items while conversing with the artists, gaining insight into the artistic traditions passed down through generations. Traditional songs and dancing were at the heart of the celebration, with performances by the Cowichan Tzinquaw Dancers and the Stz’uminus 06 Young Wolves captivating the audience. The rhythmic beat of the drums resonated through the venue as traditional songs were sung, and dancers adorned in stunning regalia gracefully performed ancestral dances. These expressions of Indigenous culture provided a powerful reminder of the resilience and strength of Indigenous communities throughout history. This event offered valuable opportunities for cross-cultural dialogue and a chance to challenge misconceptions and stereotypes. One of the highlights of the day was the traditional feast, where participants gathered “This is a day of celebration for us. We are recognizing our history and our culture. Also as leaders in this community (Stz’uminus council & Town of Ladysmith council), we are going to continue working together to build a future for our community.” -Chief John Elliott to enjoy a variety of Indigenous foods such as sockeye salmon and fried bread. The flavors and aromas delighted the taste buds of all who attended. This communal meal fostered a sense of unity and shared appreciation for Indigenous culinary traditions. Throughout the performance, attendees were encouraged to actively participate and join the songs and dances. The National Indigenous Peoples Day celebration in Ladysmith not only honored our rich cultural heritage but also provided an opportunity for healing and reconciliation. By bringing together people from diverse backgrounds, the event served 07 as a platform to foster understanding, respect, and appreciation for the Indigenous peoples’ contributions to the fabric of our nation. As the sun set on this memorable day, the spirit of unity and pride lingered in the hearts of all who attended. The National Indigenous Peoples Day celebration not only reminded us of the challenges faced by Indigenous communities but also highlighted our resilience, strength, and enduring cultural legacy. It served as a call to action for all Canadians to continue supporting and celebrating the rich tapestry of Indigenous cultures that make our country truly unique. 08 Organizers & Sponsors Our largest event to date went smoothly and a lot of planning went on behind the scenes to make this event possible. Celebrating the Arts: Our amazing art program has flourished this year, and we are thrilled to celebrate the exceptional talent of our students. Thriving Physical Education: Our physical education program has seen remarkable growth and success. From swimming and rock climbing to exciting canoeing adventures, our students have embraced these activities with enthusiasm and determination. Community Partnerships: We would like to express our heartfelt gratitude to the numerous community members who have supported our programs this year. While there are too many incredible individuals to name, their contributions have been invaluable in creating a nurturing and enriching environment for our students. These collaborations have opened doors to new learning opportunities and enriched the educational experience for our students. 23 Cohort Models: This year, we introduced cohort models based on the wisdom and guidance of our community elders. By grouping our grades 8 to 12 students into elective cohorts, we have created an exceptional learning environment that promotes collaboration, creativity, and exploration. Additionally, we created JK/K and two 6/7 cohorts, which not only offered optimal learning opportunities but also provided a glimpse into more cultural or traditional methods of education that have stood the test of time. We are delighted with the success of these models and the positive impact they have had on our students’ educational journey. This year, we witnessed a record number of buses heading out each week for field trips, allowing our students to learn directly from the land. Whether building community trails, clam digging, tending to gardens, hiking, fishing, canoeing, swimming, or rock climbing, our students have been actively engaged in their learning experiences. They have explored beaches, visited farms, and interacted with guests from other communities, fostering a deep connection with nature and their surroundings. CANADA LEARNING BOND Employment and Social Development Canada (ESDC) provides an additional incentive of up to $2,000 to help low-income families start saving early for their child’s education after high school (post-secondary education). The Canada Learning Bond (CLB) money will be deposited directly into the child’s RESP. The CLB is available for eligible children from low-income families born in 2004 or later and provides an initial payment of $500 for the first year the child is eligible, plus $100 for each additional year of eligibility, up to age 15, for a maximum of $2,000. Personal contributions are not required to receive the CLB. To help cover the cost of opening an RESP, ESDC will pay $25 into the RESP to which the initial CLB of $500 is deposited in recognition of a one-time incidental expense that may be associated with opening the RESP account. Children who are in care of a public primary caregiver for whom a special allowance under the Children’s Special Allowance Act is paid, are also entitled to the CLB. The beneficiary must be under the age of 21 at the time of application. If the beneficiary does not pursue post-secondary education, the CLB is returned to the government.
