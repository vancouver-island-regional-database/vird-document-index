Comprehensive School Health in Nanaimo-Ladysmith Public Schools By Jacquie Poulin What is Comprehensive School Health? ▪ recognizes that healthy young people learn better and achieve more; ▪ understands that schools can directly influence students' health and behaviours; ▪ encourages healthy lifestyle choices, and promotes students' health and well-being; ▪ incorporates health into all aspects of school and learning, acknowledging that health is more than just the absence of illness; ▪ links health and education issues and systems; and, needs the participation and support of families and the community at large. The 4 Pillars of Comprehensive School Health Social and physical environment - For example, healthy schools do not tolerate harassment or bullying, and pay close attention to ensuring school facilities and activities are safe. Teaching and learning - This includes both formal instruction, and informal learning, such as having teachers model healthy behaviours for students. Partnerships and services - These link the school to the broader community, enhancing the range of supports and opportunities available to students, parents, educators and others. Healthy school policy - This can encompass everything from guidelines for food sales to rules regarding conduct to frameworks for engaging community partners. The Benefits of Comprehensive School Health Research shows that a CSH approach can help schools achieve:  better learning outcomes for students;  better health and well-being for students, educators and staff;  more effective learning and teaching; and,  a more cooperative and connected school environment. Extended Benefits of CSH The benefits also extend beyond the school and its students. Because CSH involves community partners and service providers who, in many cases, provide support for families and others, using the CSH approach can also encourage: ▪ increased feelings of support from school; ▪ improved behavior and healthy choices at home and in the community; ▪ enhanced resources and opportunities; ▪ development of unique networks of services and programs for all; and, ▪ increased understanding of connections between curriculum and real life. It is an opportunity to enhance knowledge and skills in physical literacy for our primary classes. A physical literacy teacher mentor was hired to work with five elementary schools in the district to support teachers in the delivery of fundamental movement skills (FMS). Mentorship Criteria for Spring 2013 ▪ Focus will be on primary grades, K – 3 or K – 4 depending on size of school ▪ 10 week program commitment, physical literacy teacher mentor will be at each school for one day per week over the 8 weeks (then in January will do follow up with multiple schools) ▪ School needs to be willing to adjust gym schedules to allow for the primary classes to have gym on the one day that the teacher mentor is at the school ▪ The teacher mentor will collaborate, model, and team teach with the classroom teacher. The ten weeks will see a transition from classroom teacher being the observer/participant to co-teacher to class lead by the end of the ten weeks. ▪ The overall focus of the ten weeks will be on building Fundamental Movement Skills in a games based format. These skills could include run, jump, throw, balance, co-ordination, and agility. Mentorship Round 2 ▪ PacificSport has been successful in securing the grant once again so we are going for Round 2 ▪ Focus this time will be a five new skills, for an eight week program with a physical literacy mentor teacher ▪ New component this year, an additional four weeks will be available as a follow up for the initial five schools from last spring and the new five schools for this fall ▪ Feedback has been so positive that schools are quickly volunteering to be a part of this project Nutrition Guidelines ▪ The original Guidelines for the Sale of Foods and Beverages in BC Schools came out in 2005, and were revised in 2008 ▪ Majority of our schools were co-operative in becoming compliant with these guidelines ▪ Another revised version is due to come out this fall which will be a good time to remind everyone of the reason and rationale and to support schools in again reviewing their nutrition policies and practices Swim to Survive – Grade 3’s ▪ This is still in very early stages and is not yet finalized but is exciting ▪ City of Nanaimo, in partnership with Nanaimo-Ladysmith Public Schools, are submitting a grant application to the Lifesaving Society to offer the Swim to Survive Program to every Grade 3 student in our district ▪ Would include three ‘dryland’ lessons presented in the classroom by the teacher and three ‘pool lessons’ provided by certified lifeguards ▪ This is not a swim lesson program, but rather a “swim survival” program ▪ This is a barrier free program – no cost for any student to participate;
