NANAIMO LADYSMITH PUBLIC SCHOOLS EDUCATION COMMITTEE PUBLIC MEETING INFORMATION SHEET DATE: TO: FROM: SUBJECT: February 7, 2024 Education Committee Mark Walsh, Secretary-Treasurer Bill 40 - Update Background This information sheet is intended to provide a brief update on Bill 40. Discussion Recently, Bill 40 was put into law. Bill 40 makes changes to the School Act that impact a number of areas associated with Indigenous education and the manner in which the District interacts with Rights Holders and community partners. At the outset it should be noted that the impacts cannot be fully understood at this time as regulations that outline important aspects of the legislation are not yet in place. Attached to this information sheet is the document released by the government associated with the changes. Please note that “first nation” is specifically not capitalized in the legislation. Overall, we do not anticipate major impacts on our District associated with the changes. Generally, the changes are related to three areas: 1. School catchments and first nations; 2. Indigenous Education Councils (“IECs”) and Funding decisions; and 3. Local Education Agreements (“LEA”). School Catchments The legislation provides that a first nation is able to designate a school for students living on reserve to attend. Please note that we are seeking clarification of the impact, if any, for first nations students from local nations living off reserve. It is unlikely that this change will have a significant impact on our District. The District strives to place first nation students in their chosen school to support student outcomes. Further, in the case of SFN, there are currently 4 separate reserves spanning from Cedar to downtown Nanaimo and another parcel just transferred as reserve land near VIU means that SFN reserve lands will be close to 3 separate schools. https://nanaimonewsnow.com/2024/01/27/snuneymuxw-we-did-it-nearly-200-acres-offormer-camp-nanaimo-land-returned-first-nation/ Given this reality, local first nations may not see either the need or the desire to designate a school as per the legislation. Information Sheet: Bill 40 Update Page 2 However, each first nation could theoretically designate a school far from their geographic location and the District would be required to support such a request. Given the unlikelihood of this, we have not addressed potential bussing and capacity issues. Indigenous Education Councils The changes also provide for specific authority for Rights Holders and representatives of Indigenous groups in the decision making associated with the Indigenous Targeted Funding and the right to provide advice on a variety of topics. An IEC is specifically not a committee of the Board and will set its own processes. However, the most important issue associated with the IEC is the weighting of voting and its terms of reference which will be provided by regulation at a later date. Therefore, it is difficult to predict the impact of the legislation. However, it should be noted that the legislation recognizes the importance of local land based first nations in IECs. Specifically, the IEC shall have the legislative authority to provide advice and or approval on: • • • • • • Providing comprehensive and equitable educational programming and services to Indigenous students Improving Indigenous student achievement Integrating Indigenous world views and perspectives into learning environments and in particularly local first nations Advising on grants provided under the Act in relation to Indigenous students Approving plans, spending and reporting of the Indigenous target Advising in relation to languages and cultures of the local first nations For our District, the Leaders For Learning Table largely already functions in the manner envisioned by the legislation. For instance, the District would not proceed with spending the target without support of the currently constituted LFL table. There is some concern about what the decision makers structure set by legislation will be. Currently, the group operates in a consensus manner and it is likely that this will not change, despite the technical voting requirements. Model Local Education Agreement Finally, the legislation provides that a first nation is entitled to apply the model LEA to the District and that the model LEA is a contract between the parties. As background, a Local Education Agreement is an agreement between a first nation and a district with respect to services provided to first nations students on reserve attending district schools. It outlines information sharing, service provision, consultation etc. In our District we have LEAs with SFN and Stz’uminus First Nation. We do not have an LEA with Snaw-naw-as First Nation. It is completely at the discretion of a first nation as to the whether an LEA will be put into place. This aspect of the legislation should have little impact on the District.
