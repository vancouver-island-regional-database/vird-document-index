Reporting Requirements ..3 Created: April 2018 Preamble Accumulated operating surplus represents the extent to which operating revenues from all previous years exceeds operating expenditures from all previous years. Conversely, when operating expenditures from all previous years exceeds operating revenues from all previous years, an accumulated operating de?cit results. When an accumulated deficit occurs.it means future revenues are needed to pay for past expenditures. School Districts in BC are not permitted to budget for or incur expenditures that result in an accumulated operating deficit. An accumulated operating surplus allows a School District to budget for expenditures in excess of revenues in a given year and also serves to reduce financial risk that can result from financial forecasting uncertainty and unforeseen circumstances. While the BCSTA supports the development of a policy, we have taken exception to the term “accumulated "operating surplus" to describe what can more accurately be described as a district's “accumulated operating reserve”. However, given that the term “accumulated operating surplus” has been defined in Public Sector Accounting Standards, which school districts are obligated to follow in the preparation of financial statements, the ministry has taken the position that the term must be used. The Ministry if requiring the policy to be approved by Boards by September 30, 2018. The attached draft policy template was developed by the Ministry of Education and has been provided to your management teams. The purpose of the draft policy is to provide you with information that will assist in the development, management and ongoing oversight of your school district's accumulated operating surplus policy. It is prudent financial management to set aside a minimum level of accumulated operating surplus to help mitigate current and future risks to school districts. Operating surpluses provide school districts with the ability to plan and sustain services over time and to provide one-time services that would not othen/vise be affordable within the current budget. Most financial problems facing school districts tend to be multiyear problems and not one time. It is important to recognize that operating surpluses are one-time funds—once used, they are not replaced except by purposeful action of the district. Therefore, while surpluses can be used to carry a district through a year of financial problems until the district can make enough cuts in its budget to address the ongoing financial issues, they cannot sustain ongoing programs and services if no other actions are taken to address significant budget challenges. The draft template provides some ranges for the different categories of accumulated operating surplus. It is important to note that there is no one size fits all approach to determining the optimal levels for the accumulated surpluses given the significant differences in circumstances facing school districts. In setting a minimum level of operating surplus, the goal is to have the resources necessary to meet both planned and unplanned financial obligations, while spending as much of the current year's resources as possible on the current year's programs and students. This is a balancing act that will be unique to each district, with several possible risk factors needing to be considered, including:the overall financial health ofthe district; whetherthe district is facing declining enrollment and is in or moving out of funding protection; the historical usage of operating reserves to balance a district's budget; and reliance on one-time revenue sources like international student fees. POLICY What is most important is to establish clear rationale behind the desired levels and what the intended uses for those funds will be and to periodically assess whether the district is utilizing its resources as planned. Under the policy template, the Secretary Treasurer will be responsible for making recommendations to the Board on any changes to the policy or inter fund transfers if the surplus exceeds board approved levels. This is an important feature that we feel reflects good governance practices. If the operating surplus is below target or exceeds the target, the Board should consider whether this is due to a short-term situation or a longer-term issue which might trigger a broader review of the district's finances or surplus policy. How the level of surplus changes during the year can be a good indicator of the underlying financial health of the school district. Therefore, careful monitoring and reviewing reserves levels should not just be a fiscal year-end procedure. It should be undertaken throughout the year as part of the district's normal budget monitoring and reporting processes. It is important to ensure that the policy continues to be relevant as the district develops or changes its strategy and activities. The policy should also be a part of ongoing budget discussions with stakeholders and be communicated in a clear and transparent manner. The policy should be made publicly available on the school districts website and referenced in public budget materials so that there is a collective understanding of how the surplus policy relates to the district’s strategic goals. If you require additional support in developing your surplus policy, please contact Mike Roberts at mroberts@bcsta.org.
