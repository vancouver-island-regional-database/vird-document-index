This presentation is a reflection of the ideas and concerns of a group of parents from all over the district who have watched the school district’s business for the last 20+ years and continue to do so even after our children have long since graduated. What follows is a little bit of history as well as commentary:   In December 2006 staff presented a report to the Board entitled SD68 Renewal – Focus on Facilities. In that report, it was suggested by staff that “the district should engage in a community conversation in the spring of 2007 to allow the community to comment on the issues facing the district: declining enrolment, capacity utilization and the impact these are having on both operating and capital budgets, - most importantly, on the ability of the district to deliver quality programs to students, with the limited funds we have.” In January 2007, the Board directed staff to fast track the process and in March 2007 the Superintendent of the day submitted a district-wide report entitled Facilities Renewal – Developing a Long-Range Plan – Recommendations for Consideration to the Board. The key premise of the December report remained the same as it does today: “the ability of the district to meet its annual operating needs and to qualify for the capital funding necessary to ensure the best facilities for our students will require a reduction of the capacity of the district – the consolidation and closure of facilities. Without a long-term plan to eliminate the costs associated with the large number of facilities we manage, it will not be possible to sustain the programs and services that are required to provide a high quality education to our students.” The Superintendent’s recommendations at that time interestingly included Secondary Option A – closing Cedar Community Secondary and converting it to K-7, Option B – renovate both NDSS and Woodlands and Option C – close Woodlands and renovate NDSS. As mentioned, that key premise continues to remain true to today and for the foreseeable future. The ongoing underfunding of public education by our provincial government is a fact of life. Regardless of the party in power, we have declining enrollment across the province, nationally and internationally. Until that chain is broken and the government admits that investing in students makes strong financial sense for our society in the long term, this merry-go-round will never cease. Is this stance by the government likely to change in the near future? The answer is a RESOUNDING NO! So what is the sense of barking up that tree? For years now, the government has made it clear that in order for any school district to be considered for facilities funding, they must first address the issue of ongoing declining enrollment, secondly, demonstrate an ability to contribute monetarily to any funding request and more recently, build partnerships to support the project. School District 68 has many aging facilities that are at the end of their life expectancy and are a drain on the district’s finances. We need to start renewing our facilities. These are realities we simply can no longer continue to ignore.   In July 2007 the Facilities Plan recommendations morphed to include several elementary closures and one secondary closure. A fierce consultation process occurred as communities lobbied for their respective schools. In April 2008 the Board made the decision to close NDSS and build a new school at Woodlands. This was the so called $87 million dollar plan which included $22.6 million for Page 53 of 109 3.6     the seismic upgrade of Wellington Secondary which is soon to be completed at a cost of $24.5 million. In November 2008 a newly elected Board chose to step back and review the Facilities Plan decision to build at Woodlands, as well as reviewing a number of educational programs and operational services within our district. This review and planning carried over into a new board in November 2011. That board moved forward with vision and long term planning. They launched a widespread public and stakeholder consultation and developed the first district strategic plan in 20 years. They also identified the importance of and supported the development of our current Enhanced Facilities for Learning Plan. It was guided by a respected outside consultant who remained at arms-length from the influence of staff, special interest groups and stakeholders. His purpose was to gather information and provide an unbiased report to the Board. That report was detailed and comprehensive - it represented extensive feedback from all corners of the community. The board even extended the consultation process, which included developing a community working group to make recommendations to the board, to ensure that the south end community had further input in the closing and consolidation of their neighbourhood schools. The District has withstood challenges to the 2012 consultation process from two communities which was deemed to be fair by both the Ombudsman and our BC courts. So here we are today, once again reviewing the current Facilities Plan. Why? Because there is a special interest group that is understandably upset over the affect that change will have within their community, a change that many others across the district have already faced. No one likes losing their neighbourhood school. It has prompted a further consultation process that is now being questioned by many as being very Cedar focused. When first announced by the board, Your Voice 2015 was slated to review the Enhanced Facilities for Learning Plan and the 2015/16 Budget. However, the review/survey does not address most of the elements outlined in our current plan. Instead, the questions are very arbitrary, leading and focus only on Cedar Secondary and future site development at NDSS. Elements of the three round table consultations were not consistent as questions were added and removed from session to session. Interestingly, two of these sessions were at high schools within Cedar’s catchment area as opposed to a more central location like Woodlands or following the previous consultation process and going to every high school community so a more accurate and less skewed response might have been presented. Instead the message being sent by the board today is one of favouritism for one community, inconsistency and bias in their data collection. There are many of us in the community who feel the current plan is cohesive, comprehensive, as well as visionary. It contains an enhanced educational component and goes further in offering some refreshing new ideas in the form of enrichment and alternate programs. These options go a long way to attracting and retaining students in the public education system. It takes the whole school district into consideration and will move our students into the 21st century. We keep hearing that the Ministry has no money for new schools. The fact is there are monies available for renovation and new construction if certain criteria are met. The first is that the district has a formal Long Term Facilities Plan approved by the Board and submitted to the Capital Branch of the Ministry. This puts the district in the queue for funding if other criteria like reduction of district capacity have been met. Other districts have followed the criteria and have been successful: Sooke, Port Alberni and Revelstoke to name a few. Those districts have demonstrated Page 54 of 109 3.6 stability and consistency in their facilities planning - something this district had attempted to work towards over the last 6 years, a record on its own for Nanaimo/Ladysmith. We are not saying we shouldn’t look for ways to support the barriers or challenges that are appearing for students from actions within the current plan, nor that the plan should not continue to be reviewed and tweaked. Of course it should. For example, the district could look at moving the LSS boundaries by dividing the Cedar area and then provide bussing for students to LSS and John Barsby. This would more equally support choice for families and students within the south end community and offer a choice in secondary programs that many other rural areas in our province do not receive. Bottom line, the current facilities plan addresses the need to reduce excess capacity which has long been an impediment for us in gaining capital funding to address our aging school buildings. It also includes a vision for enhancing programs to support our students to be better prepared for the world they are about to enter. For over 10 years, this district has been fighting and flip flopping on the capacity issue. The data continues to point to declining enrollment being a reality for a long time into the future. The current plan acknowledges that schools function best with a certain critical mass of students and that carefully planned closures and consolidations make for more efficient deployment of resources all of which supports students directly. Unravelling one critical component of the plan in favour of one special interest group will effectively dismantle the entire plan and negatively affect all students in the district. It is imperative that your decisions take into consideration the students of the entire district and be sustainable now and into the future. We MUST continue to move forward and implement the core components of our existing plan.
