Page 13 of 38 TA:CC/COPE491 JULY 31, 2019 Integration of before and after school care for kids up to age 12 is a better model for families • Thousands of potential cost-effective spaces for before- and after-school care exist in every region of B.C. • This would increase available childcare, while delivering a high quality, valueadded system of early childhood education. • Recent changes to the School Act means that school districts can begin implementing before and after school care. • Recent changes to provincial funding models means that school districts can secure fee reduction funding for childcare for kids up to and including age 12. • It aligns with the B.C. government’s objective of integrated childcare within B.C. schools. Page 14 of 38 TA:CC/COPE491 JULY 31, 2019 Benefits of integrated childcare • Improved accessibility and fewer transitions for kids. • Enhanced and informed care that is education-focused. • Coordination of care and learning between before- and after-school and school day programming. • Additional secure and reliable childcare options in every community. • New opportunities to recruit and retain high-quality education assistants and early childhood educators. • More effective delivery of cost-effective, high-quality childcare spaces. • Increased investment in the public school system. Page 15 of 38 TA:CC/COPE491 JULY 31, 2019 Support full implementation of the $10aDay childcare plan • The $10aDay plan provides a roadmap to build a childcare system that provides a safe, quality licenced space for every child whose family wants or needs it. • A key condition for achieving a quality, universal system of childcare is to support the growth of public delivery – a piece that relies on school districts proactively being a part of the conversation. • The $10aDay plan recommends that over time, school boards assume responsibility for delivering new childcare services on and off school sites. • Childcare programs operated by external providers currently located in schools could remain there to ensure no loss of existing childcare spaces. • Beyond that, as much as possible, school boards would deliver childcare services directly. Page 16 of 38 TA:CC/COPE491 JULY 31, 2019 Examples of the integrated school-based childcare • School Districts in the Cowichan Valley, Peace River North, Campbell River, and Nakusp have established integrated before and after school care beyond grade one. • Campbell River has added seven new sites this school year. • Prince George and New Westminster are also moving towards inhouse integrated care programs in partnership with their K-12 CUPE Locals. Government childcare funding to create and operate new childcare spaces. • Funding in place for a childcare coordinator in every school district. Special Education Page 33 of 38 NANAIMO LADYSMITH PUBLIC SCHOOLS BOARD OF EDUCATION PUBLIC MEETING INFORMATION SHEET DATE: TO: FROM: SUBJECT: March 27, 2024 Board of Education Laura Tait, Acting Superintendent Preliminary Strategic Plan Background: As you are aware, in the summer of 2023 the Board embarked on the creation/renewal of the Board’s Strategic Plan. August 2023 – Trustee working session to determine next steps and general priorities October 26, 2023 - November 24, 2023 – Public consultation September 2023 – Trustee working session to continue with prioritizing December 13, 2023 – Draft Strategic Plan Public Consultation feedback provided to the Business Committee October 12, 2023 – Draft Strategic Plan presented to the Business Committee October 25, 2023 – Board passed a motion to put the Draft Strategic Plan Goals and Objectives out for public consultation February 21, 2024 – Trustee working session to refine the draft Strategic Plan Staff reviewed the feedback received from various partner groups within our educational community and organized the feedback into themes which included Mental Health, Enhanced Facilities, Teaching and Learning Approaches, JEDI (Justice, Equity, Diversity, and Inclusion), Morale and Workplace, Indigenous Learning, Gardens and Green Initiatives, Transportation, Wi-Fi/Technology, and Partnerships & Communications. This structured approach enabled staff to condense and categorize the diverse range of perspectives, thus facilitating a clearer understanding of the overarching concerns and opportunities voiced during the consultation process. By aligning our objectives with these identified themes, we ensure that our Strategic Plan is not only comprehensive but also attuned to the needs and aspirations of our NLPS community. Staff and Trustees then engaged in a collaborative working session to refine the draft Strategic Plan. This session involved a comparison between the current draft Board Goals and the consultation themes, inclusive of staff recommendations and rationale. The overarching recommendations from staff emphasized the alignment of the Strategic Plan with the Enhancing Student Learning Report (ESL), a mandatory annual report stipulated by the Ministry of Education and Child Care. To ensure coherence and effectiveness, staff proposed the establishment of clear metrics for the objectives, aligning with the Board Work Plan, ESL Report, and departmental Operational Plans.
