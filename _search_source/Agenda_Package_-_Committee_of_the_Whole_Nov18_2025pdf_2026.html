INTRODUCTION/BACKGROUND: The Town’s city hall is too small to house the Town’s administrative workforce and the condition of the building and size of the property make expansion impractical. Currently, Development Services works out of a Figure 1: Buller Street Site rented office at 132C Roberts Street, for which the Town pays approximately $33,000 (plus GST) annually, which includes utilities and taxes. Rent rates are negotiated every two years. Staff are in the process of negotiating the rate for 2026. According to the Process Four and Urban Systems reports (Attachment A and B respectively), the Town currently needs approximately 18,600 square feet of gross floor area for a city hall and will need approximately 20,000 square feet of gross floor area by 2035. In 2015 the Town purchased the Buller Street site (Figure 1) with the intention of developing a new city hall. In the summer of 2024, in response to potential funding from the Province’s BC Page 17 of 132 Builds Program, the Town initiated an Alternative Approval Process (AAP) seeking electoral approval for the Town’s portion of the “Buller Street Revitalization Project”. Under the proposal, BC Builds would fund the housing component of the project (some of which was intended to be rented at below market rates) and the Town would fund the non-residential component, which was proposed to include a new city hall and additional floor area that City Hall could expand into later, and rented to other tenants in the interim. The AAP process failed, and the project was put on hold. In June of 2025, Council passed resolution CE 2025-048 confirming that a referendum to fund a new city hall would not occur before the 2026 municipal election. Since then, staff have continued investigating options for a city hall, with the aim of providing structure and focus to future consultation. These include: 1. Building on the Buller Street site, as a standalone city hall facility. 2. Building on the Buller Street site as a mixed-use facility containing a city hall and additional non-residential and/or residential development. 3. Leasing or purchasing existing or new office space in the Downtown. 4. Building a new city hall on the Buller Street site, in combination with a new fire hall. 5. Building a new city hall on the current fire hall site, in combination with a new fire hall. 6. Building a new city hall on Town-owned lands on Thicke Road currently used for a sewage treatment facility. 7. Building a new city hall on the public works site as a standalone building or as an addition to the existing building. 8. Locating a new city hall on the Town-owned waterfront lands either as a standalone building or in the Machine Shop. Staff note that options three, five, six seven, and eight could involve selling the Buller Street site and applying the funds to the construction or purchase (but not a lease)1 of a new city hall. Currently the assessed value of the Buller Street properties is $1.2 million. Staff estimate the maximum sale price of these lands would be less than $2 million. Option seven may require relocating existing public works facilities. Options one and two offer multiple tenure options. This would include the more conventional scenario of the Town owning the land and building(s) outright, a “P3” scenario where a developer “buys” 2 the ability to build a commercial real estate development in conjunction with a city hall, or a partnership with another level of government or non-profit wishing to provide residential (e.g. affordable housing) or non-residential facilities (e.g. clinic) on the site. 1 Under section 188(2)(e) of the Community Charter, money from the sale of Buller Street site could only be used for acquiring land, improvements and other assets of a capital nature. 2 This could be a cash payment in exchange for development rights on the site or agreeing to fund some of the cost of a city hall in exchange for development rights. In either case this would involve a legal agreement between the Town and the developer. ANALYSIS: Option 1: Standalone City Hall facility on the Buller Street Site. Staff are recommending retaining this option moving forward. The Buller Street site has more than enough space to accommodate a standalone city hall, making this option one of the most cost effective and simplest choices for new construction. This option would also produce surplus land, which could be used for parking, amenities or additional development (e.g. future expansion of city hall, housing, etc.) Staff note that this option is consistent with OCP policies identifying the Downtown as the “heart of cultural, civic, culinary, economic, and public life in Ladysmith” and would bring substantial short-term and long-term economic and revitalization benefits to the Downtown. This includes improvements to downtown infrastructure (e.g. new sidewalks), initial economic benefits from construction and ongoing benefits from locating a major employer in the Downtown. Currently, City Hall and Development Services employ approximately 25 full-time staff, who typically frequent downtown businesses. Using a conservative daily spending rate of $15-25 per day per employee, this equates to approximately $93,750 to $156,250 in revenue for these businesses. Option 2: Mixed-use Facility on the Buller Street Site Staff recommend retaining this option moving forward. Zoning for the Buller Street site allows a variety of permitted land uses, 100% lot coverage and a height of six storeys. Subsequently, there is ample room to accommodate additional uses on the site, including additional uses that could generate lease revenue for the Town and benefit the community. Although this option is more complex than a standalone city hall on the Buller Street site, it may prove more financially viable. The Town can recover costs and/or generate revenue from the sale or rental of additional non-residential or residential space. In October of 2024, staff were contacted by Island Health to discuss relocating the existing clinic at the Ladysmith Urgent Care & Community Health Centre to an upgraded, more accessible facility. The design for the additional non-residential space in the Buller Street Revitalization Project was ideal for this purpose. However, as noted above, the AAP process to secure funding to build this space failed the preceding summer, and the Town was unable to offer Island Health a tenancy. Island Health then issued an RFP for new space. At time of writing, it appears Island Health has not yet selected a new location, and a clinic and many other compatible community-serving, revenue-generating uses remain an option for this site, as does housing. Staff also note that the economic and revitalization benefits noted for option one apply to this option. However, the initial economic benefits are amplified due to the increased size Page 19 of 132 of the development, as would ongoing economic benefits to Downtown businesses from additional employees and residents in a mixed-use development. The revenue benefits to downtown businesses noted under Option 1, could easily be 5-10 times higher by adding homes and additional employment centres to the project. Option 3: Lease or Purchase Existing or New Office Space in the Downtown. Although there are currently no office facilities in the Downtown that would be suitable for a city hall, staff recommend keeping this option open, as new development projects are expected to emerge that may have sufficient office space for a city hall. This space could be available for purchase (likely as a strata lot) or for rental. Purchase costs would likely be higher than construction cost, but without the risks and complexity of taking on the construction process. In the case of a lease, the lifecycle cost may be less than building or purchasing. For example, financing $15 million over 30 years to build a city hall would cost approximately $888,200 annually (based on a 4% interest rate)—or $26,645,612 in total repayment. Leasing over the same period, at the $20.11 per square foot rate currently paid for the Development Services Offices, at an annual rent increase of 2% would be $12,237,361 for 15,000 square feet of net leasable area, though the Town would not have a saleable asset at the end of a lease. A lease scenario also buys the Town time. For example, the Town could lease for a period of 10 years and use the time to develop a suitable design and secure funding for a new facility on the Buller Street site. The Town can also lease the space it needs today, and expand into new space, or move into a larger space at a later date. Economic benefits of this scenario are similar to Option 1 and 2, with the unique benefit that securing a stable long-term tenant might be the impetus for a developer to commence a major redevelopment project. Option 4: New City Hall and Fire Hall on the Buller Street Site. Staff explored the option of integrating a new city hall and fire hall on the Buller Street site. If combined in the same building, this option has the same financial disadvantages as Option 5. However, this option was also ruled out because securing sufficient access for fire vehicles would be too costly. The Province will not support adding lights to allow fire trucks to stop traffic and make lefthand turns at the Buller/Island Highway intersection. Using 1st Avenue, in its current configuration, as a regular route for fire trucks would slow response times, as fire trucks would have to slow or stop at intersections, drive at slower speeds due to angle parking, and use alternate routes when 1 st Avenue is unavailable (e.g. during Light Up or when blocked by overflow during an accident on the Highway). Though these issues can be remedied, they require substantial infrastructure and operational changes, such as installing roundabouts or traffic lights, removing angle parking and/or changing how special events operate on 1st Avenue. These would add significantly to project costs and impact, likely negating any cost savings by combining the facilities in this location. Page 20 of 132 Staff also note, that a vertical integration--i.e. a city hall above a fire hall would incur the same post disaster construction costs noted under Option 5. For these reasons staff do not recommend exploring this option further. Option 5: Combine New City Hall and New Fire Hall on the Current Fire Hall Site While this option, on its face, appears to be a practical use of Town land and money, further analysis revealed excessive and unnecessary costs to the taxpayer. The existing fire hall site is only 2,270m2 and is oddly shaped. A vertical integration would be needed to fit both facilities in the same location. Under the BC Building Code, fire halls must be built to a “post disaster” standard and anything integrated into them must be designed and constructed to ensure the fire hall can remain fully functional following an earthquake (e.g. an office above a fire hall must not collapse on it, thus preventing use of the fire hall). Staff conferred with the Town’s engineering consultant, building inspector and other building inspectors and, generally speaking, a post-disaster building requires a safety factor of 1.5, whereas other buildings (e.g. a standalone city hall) require a safety factor of 1.0. This means that by simply locating a city hall on top of a fire hall, the safety factor, and thus cost, of building a city hall increases substantially. Assuming a city hall build price of $15,000,000, staff estimate that the premium of building a city hall on top of a fire hall would be between 1.1 to 1.5 or approximately $1,500,000 to $7,500,000 added to the overall cost. This configuration would also likely require tearing down the existing fire hall, which would result in costs to accommodate the Fire Department during construction. Staff also note that when it comes to accommodating the Fire Department’s future needs, less expensive options like renovation, addition, prefabricated solutions or combinations thereof should be explored first. Seismic upgrades appear to have been made to the existing fire hall circa 1995 and, unlike the current city hall site, there is additional room for the Fire Department’s facilities to grow on the current site. Subsequently, cost savings for construction and temporary accommodation from a renovation/expansion scenario could be substantial. Similarly, there are companies that specialize in prefabricated fire halls and related structures that may also offer cost savings but would likely not be an option if a city hall were to be integrated into the design. Essentially, redeveloping the fire hall site as a combined city hall/fire hall comes with two cost premiums: one to build the city hall component to comply with post disaster standards, and one related to the opportunity cost of building a new fire hall instead of a less expensive renovation/addition or prefab construction. These premiums would far exceed the funds the Town could recover from the sale of the Buller Street site, making this option more expensive than building separate city hall and fire hall facilities. Lastly, funding sources for a new fire hall versus those for a city hall are also an important consideration. In 2023, the Province amended the Local Government Act to allow local governments to collect Development Cost Charges (DCC’s) for fire hall construction, expansions and certain renovations. The Town has since budgeted for an amendment to Page 21 of 132 its DCC program, which will take 1-2 years to complete and requires approval from the Province. This process may result in a new fire hall DCC and the ability to borrow from other DCC funds for fire hall construction. Should this occur, new fire hall construction or renovations attributable to population growth can be funded by DCC’s. However, collecting these funds takes time and fast-tracking a fire hall development to piggyback on a new city hall development would increase the amount taxpayers would contribute to the project. Furthermore, grants are often available for fire hall construction or upgrades and the same cannot be said for city hall facilities. Overall, it is likely that more external funding will be available for a fire hall than a city hall or combined city hall/fire hall, and separating the facilities appears to be the best strategy to secure such funding. Staff also note that the fire hall site sits on the same parcel as Public Works, making it a contaminated site under the Environmental Management Act. Building a city hall on this site will face the same challenges described under Option 7. Option 6: New City Hall at the Thicke Road Solid Waste Processing Facility. The Town currently owns a 9,100m2 parcel on Thicke Road which is used for a sewage processing facility. The site sits between the Peerless Road Transfer Station, a utility pole manufacturing facility, Taylor Made Landscaping’s soil and aggregate facility and a cement processing plant. The property was purchased in 2017 and the facility was completed in 2018. The facility covers approximately 30% of the site in a multibuilding configuration that leaves only two buildable sites, each approximately 1,500m 2 in size (by comparison, the existing city hall site is 1,000m2 and the Buller Street site is 2,680m2). The parcel is not serviced by sewer, stormwater, water or natural gas. Thicke Road is only partially paved, has no sidewalks and the site is not directly served by Transit3. Option 7: New City Hall on the Public Works Site The Urban Systems report notes that the potential sale of the Buller Street site could be used to mitigate the cost of pursuing this option. However, further review by staff found that short-term savings (if any) of pursuing this option are likely to be exceeded by longterm costs, opportunity costs and unknown costs. Each of these is explored below. The Public Works site currently hosts bulk fuel storage, vehicle repair and road salt storage facilities, all of which are Schedule 2 uses under the Environmental Management Act. The building permits required to build a city hall on the site will trigger site investigations, an approval freeze and remediation4 under sections 40(1), 40.1(1) of the Environmental Management Act and section 557 of the Local Government Act. Depending on the levels and extent of contamination, site investigations alone can total millions of dollars and physical remediation can reach 10’s of millions of dollars. More importantly, these costs are unpredictable, with the full extent of work (and cost) determined by the results of the remediation work itself. Remediation also takes a long time, often years, as remediation and analysis are often iterative and must occur over an extended period to rule out temporary fluctuations. For a project that requires electoral approval for funding, this can be particularly problematic. The Town must invest in remediation (if funds are available) prior to seeking electoral approval for construction cost, and risk not getting electoral approval, or get electoral approval for all costs, only to need more money due to remediation costs and construction cost escalation following an extended remediation period. It is important to note that the Province can issue a “Release Notice” for “upgrades to a site with ongoing Schedule 2 uses” and this is a common process used for avoiding site freeze and remediation requirements when renovating or expanding existing facilities on contaminated sites. This means the remediation process of expanding or renovating the 4 In this context, “remediation” means either physically decontaminating the site, or proving the site meets provincial requirements through a series of site investigations and provincial approval processes. Page 23 of 132 existing public works or fire hall facilities on the property may not be as onerous as adding a city hall. Site layout is also a major consideration. A city hall requires public access and currently all street frontages on the site are taken up by other buildings. Locating a city hall at the back of the site would require directing the public through the public works yard, which is impractical for safety, security, accessibility and operational reasons. Relocating Public Works from the buildings fronting 6th Avenue to the rear of the property adds additional costs of building new public works buildings and renovating and expanding fronting buildings. Adding additional floors to the public works building appears equally problematic. The existing public works building was built circa 1976, prior to modern day seismic requirements. While a vertical integration of a city hall into the public works building would not require the same post disaster construction as integration into a fire hall, the building design would need to be brought up to modern baseline seismic standards. This would add a sizable premium to the cost of the project (perhaps more than a new fire hall/city hall build.) Similarly, these upgrades (or the renovation itself) would likely require temporary accommodation measures for Public Works, which would also come at a cost. Without detailed structural engineering and remediation work, it is not possible to know exactly what the additional cost of integrating a city hall into the public works site will be. However, given the above, it is reasonable to assume it could easily exceed the sale price of the Buller Street site and, more importantly, has more potential for significant cost overruns. Long-term costs and opportunity costs are also an important consideration for this site. Public works facilities are land intensive, needing ample room for offices, maintenance and repair facilities, storage of large volumes of materials (e.g. road salt, fuel, building materials) and heavy equipment. Ample space is also needed to maneuver and store heavy equipment. Regulations and health and safety considerations also add to layout constraints. For example, the Fire Code requires buildings to be separated by up to 15 meters from above-ground fuel tanks, and health and safety regulations necessitate separating workers and pedestrians from vehicles operating on the site. As a growing community, Ladysmith’s public works needs should also be considered in both current and future contexts—i.e. even if there is enough room on the public works site for a city hall now, there may not be in the future. Currently, there are no Town-owned, serviced, vacant lands that are large and flat enough to accommodate a new public works yard or suffice as a satellite facility, and no plans to purchase any. Land acquisition for public works facilities is expensive, especially over the full lifecycle of land ownership. This includes the purchase price, operational costs of operating from sites that are typically far removed from service areas, and end-of-life remediation costs. This means for the foreseeable future, the existing public works site will need to meet the Town’s needs. Adding a city hall to the site is likely to accelerate the need to acquire (and possibly service and remediate) new land for public works facilities, which over the long term would Page 24 of 132 outweigh any cost savings of consolidating these facilities and selling the Buller Street site. It is also worth exploring this scenario by examining the oft-referenced comparator of North Cowichan’s combined municipal hall and public works site. Firstly, there are substantial size differences between these two sites: Ladysmith’s Public Works site (excluding undevelopable parkland) is 1.9 hectares, and 0.07 hectares is covered by the existing fire hall. This leaves 1.83 hectares of land with approximately 240 meters of road frontage to accommodate city hall and public works facilities. North Cowichan’s land holdings at their municipal hall/public works site total over 7.9 hectares with approximately 800 meters of municipal road frontage with direct connections to the highway. Approximately 3.8 hectares of North Cowichan’s site is currently in use, 1.53 hectares of which is used for public works facilities. The public works and municipal hall facilities are also serviced by separate roads, preventing the above-noted user conflicts. This configuration is not easily achievable at the Town’s public works site. North Cowichan’s facility serves a population that is three times higher than Ladysmith’s (31,990). The Town’s projected population for 2030, 2040 and 2050 is 10,125, 11,345 and 12,712 respectively. Therefore, it is reasonable to assume that Ladysmith’s current public works yard can meet the Town’s needs until 2050, but only if additional space is not taken up by new uses. Option 8: New City Hall on the Waterfront Although on its face this option appears promising, staff are not recommending exploring it at this time for the reasons noted below. Building a standalone or mixed-use building on the Town-owned waterfront lands has similar benefits to doing so on the Buller Street site, however, the opportunity cost is greater. A city hall building would need to be built on land allocated in the Waterfront Area Plan for private development (e.g. the hotel site). As a waterfront site, this land is worth more than the Buller Street site. Subsequently, the net gain of selling the Buller Street site to build on the Waterfront would be less than building on the Buller Street site and selling waterfront lands for private development as anticipated in the Waterfront Area Plan. Currently, there is approximately 7,000 square feet of unused gross floor area in the Machine Shop, and approximately 8,000 square feet of gross floor area that is occupied. Renovations to the occupied space, which are expected to be completed by April 2026, have been paid for with funding from the Investing in Canada’s Infrastructure Program (ICIP) and Island Coastal Economic Trust (ICET). These funding programs have stipulations requiring arts and cultural uses. According to the Process Four Report, the Town currently needs at least 18,600 square feet of gross floor area for a city hall and 20,000 square feet of gross floor area by 2035. This means to move City Hall to the Machine Shop the Town would have to: Page 25 of 132 1. Evict tenants from the occupied space and return ICIP and ICET funding or build a mezzanine above the unoccupied space in the back of the building to meet current space needs; and 2. Evict tenants from the occupied space and return ICIP and ICET funding and build a mezzanine in the back of the building to meet long term space needs. Although the additive cost of evicting tenants and paying back ICET and ICIP funding would likely not push costs beyond that of a new build, this space is allocated for arts and culture uses under the Waterfront Area and Arts and Heritage Hub Plans, and doing so is unlikely to be supported by community stakeholders. Therefore, pursuing any scenario requiring the eviction of existing or prospective tenancies is not supported by staff. The back of the Machine Shop is currently unoccupiable. Therefore, using this space for a city hall would first require bringing the space up to code requirements for occupancy (estimated costs are not available at time of writing) followed by mezzanine construction and other, extensive improvements, including demising walls, wiring, HVAC and accessibility upgrades. Although the cost of this would be much less than a new build and would be an investment in one of the Town’s heritage buildings, it would yield less than 14,000 square feet5 of gross floor area and the Town would quickly outgrow this space (perhaps as early as 2035). Moreover, a city hall is not the intended use of the Machine Shop under the Waterfront Area and Arts and Heritage Hub Plans and the physical changes to the building may not be supported by community stakeholders. The uniqueness, character and intended use of the Machine Shop also warrants considering opportunity cost—i.e. the space may have more value (either monetarily or intrinsically) if it is used for a cultural facility or commercial or institutional use envisioned in the Waterfront Area and Arts and Heritage Hub Plans. Intrinsically, using the Machine Shop for arts and culture, or commercial uses that support arts and culture, will likely contribute more to the vibrancy of the waterfront than a city hall. Monetarily, unused space in the Machine Shop can fetch a premium rent when the Waterfront is fully built out and there are far more grants available to fund renovations for arts, culture and heritage uses than for city halls. In short, the Town will likely be able to fully renovate the Machine Shop with external funding and then generate revenue from the space when the Waterfront Area Plan is fully implemented–if it is not used as a city hall. Subsequently, unless a strong, unified desire from the community to use Town-owned waterfront lands or buildings for a city hall emerges through public engagement, staff see this as an unproductive and needlessly contentious avenue of exploration. 5 Adding a mezzanine would not result in a doubling of the ground floor area, because floor area would be taken up by stairs, elevators, corridors, and structural components of the building that taper inwards where the mezzanine would be. Workplace- a Workplace may be an enclosed office or an open workstation, and includes one or more worksurfaces and may also include a number of ancillary furniture items. Within a workplace, ancillary furniture includes items such as chairs, tables, filing cabinets and bookcases. For planning purposes, these items will not be identified specifically until the design process is initiated. Ancillary- despite the redundancy, the term Ancillary is also used for shared furnishings, equipment, and spaces outside of individual workplaces. These include not only the same kind of furnishings such as filing cabinets and bookcases, et cetera that are found in workplaces, but also include spaces like meeting rooms, storage, and other spaces shared by two or more people. In planning for flexibility and improved functionality, it is important to identify and accommodate shared furniture and equipment separately from workplaces. Comparing area allocations to existing workplaces must take into consideration that bookcases and file cabinets might be more effectively used if kept in a location where others can easily access the material contained. Area allocations are in NASF- Net Assignable Square Feet, which accounts for functional area only and excludes walls and circulation. INTRODUCTION/BACKGROUND: Inter-community business licences (ICBLs) support dynamic local and regional economies. They reduce administrative and cost burdens by allowing eligible businesses to operate in multiple communities with the purchase of a single licence. The terms and conditions of these licences, which includes the fee and licence administration, are set out in a set of common bylaws adopted by each partner community. Currently there are 17 ICBL and IMBL agreements in B.C., covering 104 municipalities. Consumers benefit from increased choice and greater availability as businesses expand services across participating communities. Partners requested to update the bylaw wording to prevent loopholes and ‘shopping around’. Partners within the Vancouver Island ICBL have encountered businesses who had their business licence revoked, then tried to obtain an ICBL from a partnering community only to come back and work in the community that revoked their licence. This creates legal costs and time dedicated to a loophole that could easily be removed with revised wording in the bylaw. With these revisions, businesses within participating communities will only be allowed to obtain an ICBL from the community they are a resident in. Those that are true nonresident businesses will need to go to their closest participating community and those partners that sell perpetual licences will only be able to sell ICBL’s to residents within their community, not to non-residents even if they are the closest community.
