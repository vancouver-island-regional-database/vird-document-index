It is in this context that School District No. 68 (SD 68, Nanaimo Ladysmith Public Schools) sought “. . . an objective evaluation of the District’s Community School Programs . . . to find out if the programs are delivered in a cost-effective manner and if there is any duplication of programs available in the City of Nanaimo and the Regional District of Nanaimo” (Request for Quotation #2522, Community School Program Review). It is important to note that we are not evaluating the way that Community School Coordinators carry out their responsibilities or the schools in which they work. Our task was to address the following questions: • • • What programs or services do community schools provide that (a) are not provided by other schools and (b) are not provided by the City of Nanaimo, the Regional District of Nanaimo or another public agency in the community school’s catchment area? Are the current community school services and programs model effective in promoting overall student success especially for vulnerable students? Does the current community school services and programs model provide value for money in terms of educational benefits? This is a preliminary report produced at the client’s request. Several the components described in the methodology that follows are not yet complete. The full, final report will (a) describe and interpret the analyses about academic performance between community schools and non-community schools, and (b) describe the programs to provide an idea of what we mean by community engagement and social capital building. Methodology To address the goals of the evaluation, Directions is employing the following mixed methods approach: • Scan of community school programs and community programs delivered in SD 68; • Interviews with Community School Coordinators and Principals at community schools; • Questionnaires of non-community school principals, parents of students attending schools in SD 68, and teachers in SD 68; • Questionnaire of agencies and organizations providing programs in community schools and in the community; Directions Evidence and Policy Research Group, LLP Page 8 of 13 Page 3 of 8 School District No. 68 – Community School Program Review • • Comparison of student-level data and Student Learning Survey data from community and noncommunity schools Value for money analysis – a quantitative examination of the benefits that arise from the investment in community schools. Value-for-money refers, in general, to the objective of using resources to maximize the resulting benefits. In a narrow sense, a value-for-money approach may consider simply whether the value of the outcome of a program or initiative exceeds its costs. In a broader sense, and as a tool for decisionmaking, a value-for-money analysis is a means to compare programs to evaluate where the highest returns are to be found. An economic evaluation of a program is concerned with the incremental (marginal) value of the program: what additional benefits are contributed that would not have been realized in the absence of the program? This means that the analysis must consider what happens for a comparable group that is not exposed to or affected by the program. If the results (or the outcomes) for a group that did not participate in an initiative are no different from those of the participating group, the implication must be that the program does not create any incremental benefits, while at the same time incurring incremental costs. In other words, there is no value for money. Our approach to assessing the community schools has been shaped by this general perspective. We identified benefits that might be ascribed to community schools and compared community schools with non-community schools with respect to those benefits. Two broad categories of interdependent benefits were identified. One is academic performance. Academic performance does, of course, refer to performance in the classroom or on standardized tests. However, it also includes changes in student behaviour that would reduce the likelihood of dropping out of school and reduce risky and/or disruptive behaviour (for example, alcohol, substance abuse, improving mental health). The second objective for community schools is enhancing social or community capital. This might be realized as stronger community engagement with the school, or programs that would provide children with opportunities that they would not otherwise have. Our investigations found that the community schools do not improve the academic performance of students on the Foundation Skills Assessment, compared to non-community schools. If academic performance were the sole parameter of benefits expected from community schools, the value-formoney proposition would fail. However, there is a second parameter of benefits: extension of community engagement and creation of social capital. While we have anecdotal evidence about this aspect of community schools, there are no qualitative or quantitative metrics that we can construct to measure this. What we can say is that our analysis does give the Board a clear basis for decision-making. If the Board is of the view that these “social engagement” benefits (described in more detail below) are worth the cost of the program, the value-for-money proposition succeeds. This appraisal was not a planned part of the implementation of community schools in the district, which means that indicators of change over time related directly to the community schools were not systematically collected. Therefore, the review must depend on the data available to us. Given the post-hoc nature of the review, strong claims about the impact of the model would be misleading in the sense that they may imply that the community school model was the cause of any differences we observed. For example, while it may be possible to detect differences in student success relative to the school contexts (community schools or not community schools), it is not possible to make causal statements about whether the differences are caused by the context. In any evaluation it is difficult to assess the contribution or impact of any single program, process, or activity because the programs exist among many of other factors and initiatives that may be influencing outcomes. The Community Schools Concept It is difficult to distinguish between the concept of a community school (the general idea of a community school) and peoples’ perceptions of a community school. Community schools are described as places and as partnerships. The partnership notion sees the school as the locus of relationships between the school and community resources that have been mobilized to serve the students attending the community school, their parents, and others in the community who may not be directly involved in the community school. (see, for example, http://www.communityschools.org/aboutschools/what_is_a_community_school.aspx) The literature devoted to community schools suggests that the mobilization of school and community resources (often including health, social services, child protection, etc.) will improve student academic performance, family engagement, and build social capital in the surrounding community. A related, though not a necessary, condition of community schools is their location in communities that need additional supports or opportunities to enable their inhabitants to flourish. The partnerships between community schools and community organizations are thought to be instrumental in ensuring that children come to school prepared, the parents are engaged in and supportive of the school and its programs, and that community school students have opportunities that students in more affluent communities commonly enjoy (see, for example, https://www.nccs.org/vision-and-mission). Community schools, often described as neighbourhood hubs or centres, build social capital by fostering connectedness between the community and a sense of community ownership of its community school (https://www1.nyc.gov/site/communityschools/index.page) through the promotion of programs and services that extend beyond the hours of school operation. Thus, designating a school as a community school require significant effort and resources beyond the assignment of a coordinator. Directions Evidence and Policy Research Group, LLP Page 10 of 13 Page 5 of 8 School District No. 68 – Community School Program Review The Community School Concept Applied in School District 68 Community schools in SD 68 exhibit some of the characteristics of community schools as they are described above, but there is little coherence to the programs and supports to students, parents, and community members that they provide. Student, family and community needs are said to be considered by the Community School Coordinators, who, by their own admission say that the programs and services provided reflect their interests, their perceptions of what is needed, and the needs expressed to them by staff at the schools. Many programs and services are ‘opportunities’ offered by agencies and organizations outside the school from whom the Community School Coordinators have negotiated reduced fees or designated places for students. Other programs and services offered at the school are ones that the Community School Coordinator has solicited based upon the Coordinators’ knowledge of the surrounding community. Acquiring a bicycle for a student who, in its absence, would not come to school, or providing a jacket or shoes that a student is perceived to need are examples of the resourcefulness of the Coordinators. Organizing and helping to provide food for students or families in need are important dimensions of the Coordinators’ work. Community School Coordinators receive little guidance for the work they do apart from some modest guidance in financial reporting. They do not conduct formal needs assessments and only meet with school staff on an episodic “as needed” basis. SD 68’s community schools have produced tangible benefits for some students and their families, and, in doing so, added to the community capital of the neighbourhoods served by those schools. Had there been discernable differences in student performance and student attitudes, it might have been possible for us to indicate whether SD 68 is receiving value for its investment. We could not detect academic (i.e., FSA) or attitudinal differences (i.e., SLS) between students at community and non-community schools that would be necessary for a value for money calculation on those dimensions. We did note that despite the perception of difference between community and non-community schools, non-community schools attempt to provide similar supports, activities, and programs for students, though not the range that is provided in community schools. In aid of helping the Board of School Trustees of SD 68 to arrive at a decision about the disposition of community schools, we have framed several questions: • Is the Board satisfied with the present state of community schooling in SD 68 and its lack of systematic evidence of impact, such that the Board is willing to continuing funding them as they presently operate? • Is the building community capital benefit of community schools enough that, relative to other Board priorities, community schools should be maintained, and efforts made to more closely align them with Board priorities in order to produce measurable improvement in student outcomes? Recommendations Aligning community schools with Board priorities The gap between current community school practice in SD 68 and the concept of community schooling would be reduced if SD 68 articulated clear, coherent expectations; provided consistent direction to the work of the community school coordinators; and held them accountable for fulfilling the expectations. In its budget criteria for 2019-2020, two priorities set by the Board seem especially pertinent to community schools and the efforts of community school coordinators (Budget Guiding Principles, https://www.sd68.bc.ca/board/2019-20-budget-process/): • • Maximize success/completion rates for our most vulnerable students Maximize support for student learning in literacy and numeracy We believe that, if continued or expanded, these priorities should guide the efforts of community school coordinators and the specific programming and services that are provided to community school students and their families. If community schools are able to improve the success/completion rates for the most vulnerable students and improve student learning in literacy and numeracy, they will help build the social and educational capital in the communities they serve. Balancing autonomy and accountability Among the elements that would benefit from more explicit articulation are the: relationship between community school coordinators and the principals of the schools to which they are assigned, regularly scheduled meetings between them, requirements regarding participation in meetings with parent advisory councils, and provision for continuity during periods of transition. There is no shortage of needs in community or non-community schools. Community school coordinators must have a means of prioritizing their efforts. Formal needs assessments, annual plans, and specific, measurable objectives might be helpful to everyone and beneficial to the recipients of the services and supports. We would not want to eliminate the spontaneity that community school coordinators bring to the role but we think that it should occur within a framework that is aligned to the purposes identified by SD 68. SD 68 should explore the feasibility of organizing recurrent services and supports (sport and recreation activities, food and food security programs, for example) at the district-level so long as delivery and distribution remained at the school level. SD 68 should develop indicators and metrics that are aligned with its priorities for community schools. If, for example, a program or programs is intended to improve student attendance or to diminish disruptive behaviour, then an appropriate indicator and related metric should be used to determine whether the desired outcome has been achieved. Improving coordination and operations The Board should consider establishing a formal coordinator responsible for community schools (education) who should convene regular meetings with the community school coordinators, work with them to forge a community mission and philosophy, and explain the role to board, senior administration, school-based administration and teaching professionals. The coordinator should possess grant writing skills to provide support for this aspect of the community school coordinators’ work. • Develop and apply clear criteria for activities and programs that are organized under the auspices of community school coordinators and those that are simply direct rentals. • The Board should not recover custodial costs for approved community school programs and activities from community school coordinators; such costs should be absorbed by the Board as part of the over-all budgeting for community schools. • Provide bookkeeping support for community school coordinators either from one of their schools or from the central office to (a) reduce the administrative burden on community school coordinators to (b) free up time for their main functions.
