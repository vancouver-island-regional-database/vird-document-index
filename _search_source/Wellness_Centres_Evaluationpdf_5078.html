Evidence Wellness Centre Evaluation Framework Initiative 1.0 Project History and Process For many years, a group of concerned Nanaimo area health professionals, educators and community agencies collaborated with the intent of bringing accessible relevant health services to school‐based youth, particularly those considered high‐risk, and those with mental health challenges. In September 2015, the first secondary school wellness centre was opened at John Barsby Community School in Nanaimo BC, with the goal of providing primary care services (particularly mental health supports) to students. In December 2015, collaborators from the John Barsby Wellness Centre (JBWC) approached the Institute for Health System Transformation and Sustainability (IHSTS) to explore the potential to work together to enhance the success of the wellness service and further the IHSTS mandate. A scoping study was undertaken between January and July of 2016. That study identified the need for Canadian evidence on the effectiveness of school‐based wellness centres, and identified an opportunity to build and test an evaluation framework that identifies population, community1 and service‐level outcomes from school‐based wellness programs that can be used in communities across BC. In fall 2016, the IHSTS Board approved this 2‐year initiative. The goal of the Wellness Centre Evaluation Framework Initiative is to develop and test a practical, evidence‐grounded and utilization‐focused evaluation and performance monitoring framework that:  supports decision making to enhance service delivery and the health and wellbeing of students  links local impacts with population‐level trends on a defined set of wellbeing and educational achievement indicators  provides a provincial resource for evaluation and scaling up of emerging BC school‐based wellness centres. This report is about the development of the wellness centres evaluation framework. Testing of the evaluation framework is planned for 2017/18. 1 In this context, the term community is used to refer to both organizational and broader community interests (e.g. school community, the Aboriginal community). It may be further defined in the forward‐going research process. A literature review conducted on behalf of the Toronto District School Board noted that there is a need for evidencebased research with primary information collection that links health and educational data to assess the impact on student health and education outcomes4. In the United States, primary and secondary school‐based health centres have been delivering a range of primary care, mental health and developmental/behavioural supports since the 1970s or earlier, and are associated with improved student health and educational outcomes5. Most programs are targeted to vulnerable populations, including those without health insurance. Improvements have been noted in indicators such as: fewer school absences, higher educational aspirations, attainment of grade‐level outcomes and higher graduation rates through the improvement of physical and mental health outcomes overall6. The complexity of population need, the varying focus of the programs and the differing outcomes of interest pose challenges in interpretation of evaluation results to understand the overall impact of these programs7. 2.2 Literature on Measuring Youth Wellness There is extensive literature that addresses a broad range of concepts related to youth wellness. These include theoretical frameworks, research studies and learnings (from evaluations of programs and approaches that focus on issues such as healthy growth and development, resiliency, roots of violence, protective factors and risk reduction), developmental assets and self‐determination. Certain factors stand out as critical to healthy development and wellness across a broad range of literature. The national Child Youth Health and Well‐Being Indicators Project 8 identified five core concepts (Physical Health and Well‐Being, Mental and Emotional Health and Well‐Being, Social Relationships, Economic and Material Well‐Being and Cognitive Development) related to child health and wellbeing, and recommended 51 indicators across these domains for inclusion in the Provincial Health Officers Report9. Growing Up in BC (GUIBC)‐2015 provides the most extensive picture of the wellbeing of children and youth in BC, drawing on multiple sources of information12. It includes a particular focus on the challenges and achievements of Aboriginal youth and youth in care. While many wellness and educational achievement outcomes have improved over time, substantial issues remain to be resolved, particularly for Aboriginal youth and youth in care. For example:  The historic inequity between Aboriginal children and other children in BC continues, and Aboriginal children and youth are less likely to have the same opportunities to optimize their health and wellbeing as other BC children and youth.  The majority of children and youth in government care are Aboriginal.  Children and youth in government care tend to have poorer outcomes than those who were never in care.  Youth who have been in care are more likely to have considered or attempted suicide compared to youth who were never in care.  High school progression begins to decline from Grade 10 onwards, with more students failing to progress from Grade 12 to graduation than any other grade progression.  Graduation rates are improving for Aboriginal youth, but still lag behind those of nonAboriginal students.  The majority of youth in care and on a youth agreement were behind their age‐appropriate grade and did not have a high school credential by the time they reached age 19.  Youth fall behind in school because they feel pressured to do well or face several challenges. The report also confirms that comprehensive school health programs are important to promoting healthy eating and active living among children and youth in schools. In addition, children and youth with strong connections to school are less likely to experience a wide range of risky behaviours and negative outcomes. 12 Appendix 1 and 2 of the GUIBC‐2015 report identify the variety of data sources used to compile the report. There is also a shared desire for evidence on the impact of the wellness centre services, and a desire to use this evidence to inform decision making. 15 The Wellness Centre at Belmont serves students from Belmont, Royal Bay and Edward Milne Secondary Schools. The centres are fully integrated into the school structure and a collaborative approach to care is provided by the centre staff along with school staff (counsellors, teachers, child and youth care workers and school administration) and school programs such as those targeted to ensuring students have access to food and programming for Aboriginal students. The activities are framed within a comprehensive school health model. The wellness centre models are evolving organically in response to student needs, the learnings of the staff in responding to these needs, and the potential for service enhancement through partnerships. The Wellness Centres Logic Model was developed through a series of discussions with Advisory Committee members, stakeholders, wellness centre and school staff and students. It has been revised to reflect the dynamic nature of the program development and learnings, and will continue to be revised through the life of the Wellness Centre Evaluation Framework Initiative as appropriate. As such, the Logic Model represents a high‐level snapshot of the current status as of March 31, 2017. The wellness centres evaluation framework was developed based on:  Review of the evidence on youth wellness and secondary school environments  Enhancing the initial program logic model created by wellness centres staff and management  Discussions with wellness centres staff, school staff and students  Discussions with the Barsby and Belmont Leadership Groups and the Project Advisory Committee  Discussion with workshop participants (stakeholders and content experts) The evaluation framework also links local impacts with population‐level trends on a defined set of wellbeing and educational achievement indicators. The evaluation framework is focused on answering a core set of evaluation questions regarding the processes and impacts of the wellness centres, intended for quality improvement purposes (refer to list of questions below). The wellness centres evaluation framework also seeks to align with mediumand long‐term outcomes that have been identified in various strategic reports by Island Health, various departments of the Ministry of Health, First Nations Health Authority and the Ministry of Education.
