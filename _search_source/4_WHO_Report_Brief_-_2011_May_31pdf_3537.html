World Health Organization Categorization of Electromagnetic Fields Issue  The International Agency for Research on Cancer (IARC), part of the World Health Organization (WHO), has classified the radiation emitted by cell phones as “possibly carcinogenic” in a report issued May 31, 2011. Report Summary  Over eight days, a working group of 31 scientists from 14 countries reviewed exposure data, hundreds of studies of cancer in humans and the studies of cancer in experimental animals along with other data. The investigations looked at the possibility of exposure associated to microwaves, transmission of radio, television and wireless telecommunication and personal exposure from cell phones.  They concluded that there was “limited” evidence that wireless phones are linked to brain cancer – meaning that it could be down to chance rather than causation – and “inadequate” proof that mobiles cause other types of cancer.  The WHO classifications include carcinogenic, possibly carcinogenic, and probably not carcinogenic.  By classifying cell phones as “possibly carcinogenic”, the IARC has placed them alongside coffee, nickel and pickled vegetables in a list of more than 900 agents it has analysed.        Possibly Carcinogenic Caffeine Pickled vegetables Frying emission from high-temperature Shift work that involves circadian disruption Carpentry and joinery Nickel Piercing  This decision may put pressure on governments to update their advice to the public on the potential dangers of talking on cell phones for long periods of time. This advice may include suggestions to use a handsfree device like Bluetooth headphone or text when possible.  Research to date about the possible health effects of frequent cell phone use has been inconclusive.  Last year’s IARC study, known as Interphone, reported that making calls for more than half an hour each day over ten years could increase users’ risk of developing gliomas – a type of tumour that starts in the brain or spine – by 40 percent. The study was controversial because it began with people who already had cancer and asked them to recall how often they used their cellphones more than a decade ago.  Following the IARC’s May 31 announcement, experts pointed out that it did not prove mobiles cause cancer, and that there was still little long-term research on the subject: o Prof Malcolm Sperrin, Director of Medical Physics & Clinical Engineering at Royal Berkshire Hospital, said the categorization was justified but added: “It should also be stated that electromagnetic field exposure is not new - witness the regular usage of radio and other waves for many decades with no convincing health detriment at low powers. The social and technological benefits also need to be emphasised.” o Ed Young, head of health information at Cancer Research UK said: “The risk of brain cancer is similar in people who use mobile phones compared to those who don’t, and rate of this cancer have not gone up in recent years despite the dramatic rise in phone use during the 1980s.” BC Hydro’s response to WHO categorization of electromagnetic fields  Radio communications and human exposure to radio frequency dates back more than a 100 years.  BC Hydro recognizes there is active discussion and ongoing research into possible health and environmental effects related to radio frequency signals.  The statement that cell phones are possibly carcinogenic is not new. By classifying cell phones as “possibly carcinogenic”, the IARC has placed them alongside coffee, nickel and pickled vegetables in a list of more than 900 agents it has analysed.  While smart meters and cell phones are both wireless they have different parameters.  Smart meters are located outside of the home and transmit less than a minute a day at a hundredth of the power of a cell phone.  The study that led to classifying cell phones as “possibly carcinogenic” is based on an average of 30 minutes of cell phone use per day, next to your head, over a 10year period.  The cumulative exposure to radio frequency from a smart meter - over its entire 20 year life span – is the equivalent to a 30 minute cell phone conversation.  BC Hydro’s smart meters are below the precautionary limits set by Switzerland – the most rigorous standards in the world.
