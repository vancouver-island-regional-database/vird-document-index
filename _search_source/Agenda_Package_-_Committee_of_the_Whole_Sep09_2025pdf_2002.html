SFD + Suite is 1 permit but 2 dwelling units Page 11 of 20 Page 12 of 20 Page 13 of 20 Page 14 of 20 Page 15 of 20 Page 16 of 20 Page 17 of 20 Page 18 of 20 Council CoW Submissision: Councillor Stevens Proposed Motion: That 1% of annual taxation be directed to the Active Transportation Reserve in each of 5 years of the 2026 to 2031 Financial Plan. Rationale: Within the Official Community Plan there are a number of commitments related to Active Transportation which will require resources to execute. Later this year/early in 2026 a call for proposals will go out to develop Ladysmith’s Mobility Plan (an OCP commitment itself, see Section 2.3). Execution of this Plan will require dedication of resources - the proposed motion performs this dedication. • Section 2.1 d “ Make Active Transportation the easiest and most enjoyable way to get around, with transit being a close second” • The Introduction to Chapter 2 Transportation states that “achieving net zero emissions by 2049 is not possible without a major shift in transportation modes.” • Section 2.4 states that the Subdivision and Servicing Bylaw roads standards are to be amended to include direction on pedestrian and cycling infrastructure along with other elements of the Mobility Plan. • Section 2.12 prescribes retrofitting of Downtown roads to BC Active Transportation Guide standards. • Section 2.15 calls for a cycling and pedestrian overpass as per the WFAP. • Section 2.16 calls for improved pedestrian infrastructure in areas around schools, transit stops, Downtown and in Priority Growth Areas. • Section 2.17 calls for incorporation of recommendations from the 2018 AgeFriendly/Accessibility Project. (our current Accessibility Committee will no doubt produce more such recommendations). • Sections 2.17 and 2.18 prescribe design and implementation of a Ladysmith cycling network. • Section 2.46 calls for Household Travel Surveys every 5 years to understand mode share (and hopefully changes in mode share) • Requirements such as reducing parking, bicycling parking and ebike charging, that the Town is currently asking of developers, will not have their full transportation value met if there is not an AT network for users once they leave their home. In short, density is a Page 19 of 20 necessary component of realizing the Vision of the OCP and AT infrastructure is a necessary component of successful densification. Economic Benefits: One of the primary sources of AT funding is upper-level Government grants. These grants tend to require a matching contribution from the Town - they also offer very high leverage of funds dedicated for AT (eg. the BC AT grants 100% match local contributions) and hence produce excellent value from Reserve funds. AT infrastructure is also orders of magnitude less per metre than automotive infrastructure and tends to have a longer lifespan - a further reflection of value for money, and a way to free up resources for other infrastructure. AT infrastructure increases the buying power of lower income residents by reducing transportation costs. Social Benefits: With an aging population comes aging out of licensed driving. AT infrastructure provides transportation for those who make the move to walking, biking or mobility aids. AT infrastructure also combats the transportation challenges that were noted over the course of the Ladysmith Poverty Strategy’s development by increasing equitable access to employment and services. For lower income residents, AT infrastructure investments represent investment in user equity. AT increases levels of physical activity in the Community which in turn generates improved individual health status while reducing healthcare and transportation costs. AT infrastructure increases accessibility for people with mobility issues and disabilities. AT infrastructure increases the safety of cyclist and pedestrians - which is good in itself, but also creates a feedback loop, where more people feeling safe to use the mode, means more people using the infrastructure. Environments Benefits: In addition to reducing demand for parking, and improving local air quality, AT is key to achieving GHG targets as a full 74% of Ladysmith’s GHG emissions are from transportation (OCP p. 18).
