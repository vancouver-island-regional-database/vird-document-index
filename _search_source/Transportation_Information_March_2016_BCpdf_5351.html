Nanaimo Ladysmith Public Schools BOARD OF EDUCATION BUSINESS COMMITTEE MEETING INFORMATION SHEET DATE: March 9, 2016 TO: Business Committee FROM: Secretary-Treasurer, Graham Roberts SUBJECT: Student Transportation Background: When staff presented the 2015‐16 budget, it was with the goal of becoming sustainable over two years. As districts no longer receive specific funding for student transportation, money allocated to provide such service takes resources away from student learning. A two‐step process is required to fully review the existing policies and procedures on student transportation. The attached report prepared by School Bus Consultants, provides additional information for the Board to consider as part of this process. Step 1 (2016‐17 Budget) When developing the 2016‐17 budget, staff will review ridership data to develop cost reductions through reducing courtesy ridership and greater use of transportation assistance within the existing procedure (Procedure No. 4400P – Conveyance of Pupils). Specific routes affected will be identified as the 2016‐17 budget is developed. Step 2 (Policy Revision – Effective September 2017) The statutory requirement for Boards to provide student transportation service has been removed. Additionally, Boards no longer receive dedicated funding to provide such service. It is at the Board’s discretion as to the service level which it will provide student transportation. This creates a need to develop a revised Board Policy for student transportation. This would move the level of transportation service provided to a Board Policy decision, not an Administrative Procedure. For policy development, we need to identify the critical components which should be addressed. For student transportation, this would include but may not be limited to; walk limits, safety concerns, alternate transportation options, student vulnerability and fee for service. Further, these needs have to be balanced with the overall objective of student learning, as money allocated to student transportation takes resources away from the classroom. Staff will engage the public in a conversation (April 14, Special Public Forum) about the level of student transportation Nanaimo Ladysmith Public Schools should deliver based on the components noted above. With this input, staff will develop a new student Transportation Policy for the Board to consider (May 11, Business Committee). All Policies require a 30 day notice of motion period before final approval (May 25 Board meeting to start 30 day count). The June Business Committee meeting (June 8) and Regular Board meeting (June 22) would be made available to receive public presentations on the revised Policy. A Special Board meeting (June 29) will be created for final decision. In the fall of 2016, the Transportation Department will develop new routes based on student eligibility requirements identified in the revised policy. These routes will be reported out through the December Business Committee meeting. The purpose of this analysis was to identify several different alternatives to the current transportation structure and to estimate the financial impact of these alternatives on the resources required to support transportation services. In order to perform this analysis, the following process was used:  Establish the statutory, regulatory, policy and administrative requirements for the provision of services  Define the baseline cost and service categories of the current program  Assess the alternatives to the current model The remainder of this memorandum provides the details of our assessment. Transportation Requirements and Expectations Transportation services in the Province of British Columbia have experienced significant changes over the last several years due to changes in statutory requirements and funding practices. It is these changes that have led many districts across the Province to reconsider the requirements for transportation services and the models of service they have traditionally used. What is crucial to recognize is that there is a basic hierarchy that sets the requirements for transportation service provision that includes: statutory requirements (e.g., the School Act); regulatory requirements (e.g., Ministry of Education administrative regulations and judicial ruling that establish service expectations); policy requirements (these are focused primarily on school board policy requirements); and administrative practice (this focuses primarily on the historical practices of the school district relative to transportation). Understanding the requirements of each element in this hierarchy is important point of departure because they all set service expectations in the minds of parents and students. The statutory requirements for transportation services would be established in the School Act. However, for purposes of this assessment it is crucial to note that there are no requirements for a school board to provide transportation services to any students within the School Act. Additionally, the annual funding process that used to provide some degree of offsetting funds for student transportation operations has been discontinued (although capital funds have continued to be provided). The Ministry of Education has also specifically identified transportation services as an area where administrative savings might be realized. The compilation of all these factors is that there is no statutory or regulatory mandate for the provision of student transportation in the Province of British Columbia. The policy and procedure requirements associated with the provision of transportation services are locally adopted and addressed in Procedure No. 4400P. This procedure statement was originally drafted to align SD 68 practices with the requirements of the Ministry of Education and the School Act. As currently adopted, the policy requires services to students based on an established distance from their school location based on their grade level. Additionally, there are explicit requirements associated with the amount of time students can spend riding the bus. Additional requirements associated with bus stop placement and the administration of the transportation program are also established. It is these elements of the hierarchy that are mostly closely associated with a “requirement” to provide services in the mind of the public and the students in the district. As could be expected, the recent changes to Ministry regulations that eliminated the requirement to provide transportation services will necessarily require revisions to existing district policy as the reference points included in the document are no longer valid. Given that the public has become accustom to these expectations and allowances, these are likely to be most prevalent service changes that will have to be overcome. For purposes of this analysis, we have assumed that the specific guidelines related to distance eligibility are flexible, but that ride time and distance to stop expectations would continue to be the basis for any routing alternative developed. The final guidance on route development comes from the historical practices of the district when it comes to making allowances for policy deviations. While the department has been highly effective at using existing policy documentation to manage policy deviations, there has been board guidance on the provision of service that has created institutionalized exceptions that would be a key concern to be addressed under any revised routing structure. These are likely to be the most difficult practices to change as individuals have become accustom to these deviations. When taken as a whole, there are several important takeaways for consideration in the development of alternatives:  There is no mandatory requirement for the provision of any transportation services at the Board.  Historical service practices are a major factor that will have to be addressed in any alternative service models.  The primary driver of the scenarios will be the need to address the gross cost of transportation services. There are substantial implications of these changes that extend beyond just the policy and administrative structure of the district. One of the most notable is in the area of service provision for students who do not meet any established eligibility criteria, regardless of the reason. Over time the district had defined a number of areas where otherwise ineligible students would receive services in order to address a community concern. If taken to an extreme that many boards across British Columbia are considering, the elimination of all services would result in the elimination of all services for both eligible and otherwise ineligible students. Any decision to continue to provide even limited services should consider this implication and the process that will be used to continue to address these concerns. The fiscal constraints that underlie this analysis will require difficult decisions be made relating to how services should be provided to students who have previously received service as a result of an exception to policy. Significant consideration must be given to defining when and how otherwise ineligible students could be served. There are essentially two options under a revised service model and these include: assuming that all students who are currently eligible will remain eligible or assuming those students would not be eligible and that any existing exceptions to policy would have to be considered again. Both of these options have important policy and fiscal considerations. Many of the non-distance eligible areas receiving service now were defined long ago and the logic and rationale for why service was provided is no longer available. The implication of this is that if it is assumed that the district will revise its eligibility expectations but retain these exceptions it will be extremely difficult to have a notable impact on the amount of resources dedicated to transportation. Additionally, the policy implication of retaining these services could create equity concerns where students in similar circumstances may be provided different levels of service. Summary of the Existing System The structure of the existing operation, as we have identified in previous analyses, is managed efficiently given existing constraints. Since the original assessment of operations, the board has further resourced the department to support improvements in route planning through the acquisition of a student management package that supports rider registration and integrates with the existing GPS technology. This has allowed for much more complete measurement of actual ridership relative to those who are eligible for services. Any change to the routing structure provides the opportunity to revisit the basic requirements of any transportation system ranging from one student to all students being serviced. There are a set of baseline requirements regardless of scale. Not surprisingly, an increase in scale necessitates an increase in the administrative and managerial scope of the transportation organization. Key aspects of how the structure of the transportation operation may change as the scope of the operation changes are identified below:  Policy and procedure documentation – once the district determines that it will transport even a single student it will be necessary to establish the guiding policy and procedure documentation to support operational practices. The existing procedure statement has many of the important elements included in the specifics of the statement including eligibility criteria, walk to stop distance guidance, ride time guidance, and any exceptions to policy that will be considered are identified. The existing statements would require revisions to accommodate changes to Ministry policy and to fully reflect the decisions related to the scale of operations.  Organizational staffing – the current structure provides a minimal number of staff for even a small scale operation. Assuming the district continues to provide transportation, it is unlikely that the administrative scale of the organization would change absent significant growth rather than contraction of the operation.  Technology support – SD68 has made reasonable investments in transportation technology to clarify the actual ridership that is occurring on bus routes in order to support more accurate planning efforts. These investments should be continued although there would be marginal reductions in total costs as a result of the reduced number of units.  Maintenance support – the shared maintenance services between transportation and other district departments limits the potential reductions that may occur in this function even with substantial changes Nanaimo Ladysmith Public Schools Transportation service options March 7, 2016 Page 4 to the scope of transportation services. To the extent that other assets continue to require maintenance services, it is unlikely that staff will be reduced unless nearly all services were eliminated. Even with effective management, the fixed cost components of associated with transportation make it more difficult for smaller operations to be comparatively cost efficient when looked at purely from the perspective of performance indicators. For the 2015-16 school year, the total transportation budget is approximately $2.2 million exclusive of capital requirements. A total of 22 routes (18 regular home-to-school and 4 special needs) are used to transport approximately 1,700 total students. This includes 956 riders who are transported based on board policy and 733 riders who are transported based on exceptions to policy. Within this total is also approximately 34 special needs riders. This would equate to an overall cost per bus of $104,081 and an overall cost per student of $1,269. While these numbers are comparatively high, much of that is due to the fact that approximately 30 percent of the cost of each bus is fixed whether there is 1 bus or 22. It is for this reason that the need to eliminate buses is the key driver of cost savings. In many instances unit costs may go up as these fixed costs are spread over fewer riders, but the total resources dedicated to transportation would be reduced. Consequently the focus of these assessments was on the impact that changing the students who were eligible for services would have on the total number of buses required to operate the system. In order to normalize the unit costs used throughout the analysis, existing budget information was used to establish an operating cost per bus. This operating cost value is designed to recognize that many overhead costs cannot be eliminated on a linear basis related to the number of buses. For example, the reduction of one bus from the fleet would not allow you to remove 1/22nd of the transportation supervisor’s cost from the budget. Nor could you reduce the number of fleet management technicians solely due to the reduction of one bus. As a result, it was necessary to come up with a normalized methodology that would account for the operating costs of each bus. We recognize that this overstates the cost of some assets and understates the cost of others, but it provides a more reasonable and realistic methodology to determining the likely impact of unit-based reductions. Based on a total operating budget of $1,615,995 and a total of 22 buses, the unit cost that will be used to determine the per bus reduction costs is $73,454. Under the current service model there are a variety of different categories of students who are receiving services. These include:  Students eligible under existing distance policy guidelines  Students eligible as a result of an identified special need or program  Students eligible as a result of a defined and approved hazard  Students being provided service as part of the board’s courtesy allowance As can be seen from the previous student count information, 43 percent of riders in the current system are being provided service for reasons other than distance eligibility. Any potential reduction in service should begin with an assessment of the impact of removing otherwise ineligible riders from the system. Additional alternative models for consideration Each of the models previously discussed continues to use distance as the key determinant of eligibility for services. While this is traditional for the majority of school districts there are alternatives that could be used. These alternatives Nanaimo Ladysmith Public Schools Transportation service options March 7, 2016 Page 8 are difficult to fully evaluate until additional features of these alternatives are defined, it is possible to describe the options for future analytical consideration.  Geographic boundary options – under these options a defined geographic boundary is used to determine eligibility. Students within the boundary become eligible for services and students outside the boundary are not. This type of structure is similar to the neighborhood design that is used in Edmonton Public Schools. An alternative to the geographic neighborhood boundary is an urban and rural boundary designation. This boundary is also typically geographic in that a line of separation is established between an urban and rural service area with the rural area typically being the one to receive services. This model is the most unique of the three is designed to recognize differences in access to alternative service providers between urban and rural areas and to continue to offer services to otherwise ineligible students. The basis for this approach is that the elimination of eligibility definitions from Ministry policy provides flexibility for the district to redefine service expectations. The goal is to create realistic requirements and to supplement yellow bus services for students who may no longer be eligible for services.  Grade based eligibility – a number of school districts limit eligibility to a particular set of grade ranges. These limitation are often coupled with a distance based or geographically defined boundary. This is most common in districts in the United States where secondary transportation is discontinued due to budget constraints.  School or site-based eligibility – this model uses some defining characteristic of a school to determine eligibility for services. A common characteristic is traffic volume where the number of vehicles into and out of the site creates undue hazard either for students or the surrounding community. While traffic is used illustratively, nearly any site based characteristic could be chosen. Use of an alternative method of eligibility determination is made possible by the elimination of previous Ministry requirements for specific service requirements. SD 68 has full flexibility to design an eligibility structure that allows for the provision of service that is as narrow or as expansive of fiscal resources and board expectations will provide. Conclusion The significant restructuring of provincial practices related to transportation necessitates the reconsideration of the structure and service allowances within SD 68. A substantial revision of the current policy structure will be a major effort that must be at the forefront of any future definition of service expectations. The analyses indicate that there are a substantial range of options available to the district to change the service model and reduce the resources dedicated to transportation. However, it is clear that all of the options will have some impact on families across the district. The range of impacts depend on the degree to which the district is willing to reduce service levels. SBC supports a process that provides for input on the categories of service that will be provided service in the ongoing model. The willingness to consider alternative service models that will support the districts overall mission of supporting access to educational programs is a reasonable and appropriate response to the need to balance fiscal resources and service expectations. As a result, SBC is supportive of the revised distance eligibility model that continues special needs services and increases the eligibility requirements while considering the availability of other local resources to support students who may no longer be eligible for service. Criteria STSBHN uses the following criteria to make a decision on the safe walkability of a route to school. Each item in and by itself does not necessarily warrant such a designation, but a combination of several factors may result in the STSBHN determining that a walking route is “hazardous.” 1. Volume of Traffic – STSBHN takes the volume of traffic into consideration for students that have to cross streets or roads. STSBHN uses Provincial warrants developed by the Ontario Traffic Conference with the Ministry of Transportation and adopted by most municipalities. It counts the number of vehicles in a 5 minute period and determines the number of gaps within the 5 minute period that would allow students time to cross. With this information STSBHN can suggestions if an adult crossing guard, student patrol or no intervention is required. Intersections will be visited by staff members of STSBHN to gain a greater understanding of issues surrounding a particular intersection if deemed necessary. 2. Number of Traveled Lanes of a Road – STSBHN considers the number of traveled lanes a student would have to cross. In most cases, more lanes on a road are an indicator of higher volumes of traffic. In most cases, the metered width of a road is converted to the time it takes for a student to cross. 3. Posted Speed Limits – STSBHN takes into consideration the posted speed limits of a street or road. A safety consideration is given to roads that have speed limits above 70 kph. 4. Sidewalks – lack of sidewalks are not a consideration for bus transportation in and by itself. Many of the communities STSBHN serves either by choice or design do not have sidewalks. The combination of lack of sidewalks Title : Hazard Transportation Eligibility Section : Eligibility Criteria…continued Procedure # : 030 Page : 2 of 2 Effective : Nov 5, 2010 Retire : Nov 5, 2012 and other factor(s), such as higher posted speeds and volume of traffic, may provide safety consideration for the walking route. 5. Signalized Intersection or Crossings – STSBHN takes into consideration whether there is a signalized intersection to allow for a safe crossing of a student in the absence of a crossing guard or student patroller. 6. Physical Barriers – STSBHN takes into consideration physical barriers such as construction, bridges without sidewalks or railings, unguarded railroad crossings or unprotected waterways. These may provide safety considerations for the walking route. 7. Grade Level of Students – STSBHN takes into consideration the grade level of a student that has to walk to school. In some situations STSBHN will, in combination with other criteria, designate a route hazardous. 8. Historical Designations – STSBHN takes into consideration hazardous route designations that have been in place for a number of years.
