In 2018, senior Nanaimo Ladysmith Public Schools district administrative staff proposed eliminating all Community School Coordinators from the 2019 district budget to use those resources elsewhere in the district. The decision to eliminate the coordinators was stayed by Board decision pending further study and discussion (https://www.nanaimobulletin.com/news/school-district-changes-mind-wont-cut-communityschool-coordinators/). It is in this context that School District No. 68 (SD 68, Nanaimo Ladysmith Public Schools) sought “. . . an objective evaluation of the District’s Community School Programs . . . to find out if the programs are delivered in a cost-effective manner and if there is any duplication of programs available in the City of Nanaimo and the Regional District of Nanaimo” (Request for Quotation #2522, Community School Program Review). It is important to note that we are not evaluating the way that Community School Coordinators carry out their responsibilities or the schools in which they work. Our task was to address the following questions: • • • What programs or services do community schools provide that (a) are not provided by other schools and (b) are not provided by the City of Nanaimo, the Regional District of Nanaimo, or another public agency in the community school’s catchment area? Is the current community school services and programs model effective in promoting overall student success, especially for vulnerable students? Does the current community school services and programs model provide value for money in terms of educational benefits? Our approach to assessing the community schools (including an economic evaluation) has been shaped by this general perspective: An evaluation of a program or policy, such as providing for community schools, is concerned with understanding the incremental (marginal) value of the program; in other words what additional benefits are contributed that would not have been realized in the absence of the program? This means that the analysis must consider what happens for a comparable group that is not exposed to or affected by the program. If the results or outcomes for a group that did not participate in an initiative (e.g., students in non-community schools) are no different from those of the participating group (e.g., students in community schools), the implication must be that the program/policy does not create any incremental benefits. Limitations This requested review of the community school model in SD 68 comes well after community schools were established in the district. This appraisal was not a planned part of the implementation of community schools in the district, which means that indicators of change over time related directly to the community schools were not systematically collected. Therefore, this review must depend on the data available to us. Given the post-hoc nature of the review, strong claims about the impact of the model would be misleading in the sense that they may imply that the community school model was the cause of any differences we observed. For example, while it may be possible to detect differences in student success relative to the school contexts (community schools or non-community schools), it is not possible to make causal statements about whether the differences are caused by the context. In any evaluation it is difficult to assess the contribution or impact of any single program, process, or activity because the programs exist among many of other factors and initiatives that may be influencing outcomes. While the Community School Coordinators appear to share a common perception of their roles and their work, the role, the work of Community School Coordinators, and the purpose of community schools are not well-defined. Thus, district administration, principals, teachers, parents and students must rely upon their direct experience with each Community School Coordinator and their perceptions of community schools to determine what community schools are and what they might expect from the Community School Coordinators. The training for the role is modest. Community School Coordinators largely rely on their familiarity with the work and the communities where the schools are situated, the records left by their predecessors, their ability to contact their predecessors, and the help and good will of their fellow Community School Coordinators. Community School Coordinators are members of CUPE and subject to the collective agreement between CUPE and the school district. Community School Coordinators are ‘community developers’ who said that they have been told by district staff that they are smart and resourceful people and will be able to address any of Directions Evidence and Policy Research Group, LLP Page 10 of 67 School District No. 68 – Community School Program Review the problems they encounter. Thus, Community School Coordinators enjoy considerable autonomy. In practice, Community School Coordinators are developing community capital, the connections among citizens in a school community, and the support network implied by the concept of community. To develop community capital, Community School Coordinators must acquire community knowledge, develop relationships that they can call upon to carry out their work, and have the support of the communities they serve. To succeed, they must be resourceful and entrepreneurial brokers between service providers and the students, families and other adults in the community. As the work is demanding, Community School Coordinators make their own schedule and seemingly work more than the 33.5 hours per week allotted to the position. The Community School Coordinators think of their work in practical and immediate ways (for example, the need to provide food, clothing, or transportation to students in need). We asked the Community School Coordinators about three broad purposes of community schools: improving student academic success, increasing student and parental engagement with and connectedness to school, and reducing disruptive or risky behaviour. While Community School Coordinators may not immediately think about their roles and actions in terms of those three goals, they agreed that the work they do helps to achieve those goals. Students who cannot or will not come to school because they are hungry, cold, or without transportation are going to be lost to those three goals. Community School Coordinators are opportunistic rather than deliberative in their approach to the work. That is not to imply they are not thoughtful or purposeful, but they seize opportunities when they see them. For example, when providers want to offer activities in their schools, they trade access to facilities for student seats or reduced student fees in those activities. Visibility in the community and in schools is instrumental to the work of Community School Coordinators. Being able to tell a provider that “Benita loved the bike you donated” gives concrete evidence of the difference the donation made. Being able to ask a child and youth worker who might benefit most from an after-school mentoring program connects vulnerable students with opportunities not otherwise available to them. Community schools depend greatly upon the strengths that the person in the position possesses and the personal and community resources the Community School Coordinator can call upon. The primary distinction that Community School Coordinators draw between community schools and non-community schools is that the former enrol a larger number of students living in relatively more impoverished circumstances. However, they also recognize that schools whose Directions Evidence and Policy Research Group, LLP Page 11 of 67 School District No. 68 – Community School Program Review enrollment is relatively advantaged also enrol students who are relatively less well off and who face challenges not faced by their more affluent peers. Thus, the differences between community and non-community schools are not categorical, but, rather, locations on a spectrum of student and family vulnerability. Community schools allow user groups to access space for events and activities that do not necessarily involve children, programs that directly enhance student learning (mentoring, homework help) and programs that are intended to engage and occupy students (e.g., noontime basketball, dance, cardio fitness). Community School Coordinators undertake their work with relatively modest financial support from the school district. They augment what few financial resources they have by applications for grants from community organizations and agencies, donations, and fees charged for participation in activities or programs. The general thinking about charging fees for participation of students from families in precarious financial circumstances is that a small fee ensures that the activity or program is valued by the family who will then ensure that their child attends. However, we were told that no child is excluded from a program if the fee cannot be paid. One off the obstacles that Community School Coordinators face in addressing vulnerable students occurs in relation to summer programming, which is intended assist and enrich their school experiences (e.g., English Second Language camps for new Canadians, Reading Camps for students who need a leg up for the next year). Summer programs offered by outside agencies are more expensive than the families of the vulnerable students can afford. We were told that summer programming typically consists of a variety of activities offered for four partial days/week at a rate of $20-$25. However, due to summer maintenance in SD 68, we were told that Community School Coordinators are not permitted to organize programs in schools4 that extend past the third week of July, a limitation that inhibits their ability to fulfill their perceived mandate to vulnerable students. Participation in community-school related activities is not restricted to vulnerable students or vulnerable families because restricting participation would segregate and possibly stigmatize children and families in such circumstances. And, when grant money is obtained to enable an instructor to offer cardio-fitness or dance instruction, such activities are often offered to a whole class or grades, or the whole school. In recent years there has been an increased focus on district-wide programs. We learned about a district-wide science centre initiative and one involving the symphony. There is ambiguity about a variety of issues associated with community schools. One of the ambiguities is that community schools provide space to programs (e.g., AA and Al-Anon) that are also offered in non-community schools. A second ambiguity is that the shift to a focus on programs directly related to student learning has meant that long-standing programs (bridge for seniors) are no longer as welcome in community schools as they were in the past, even though such programs use space that no others can use and occasionally provide volunteers for student-related activities. A third ambiguity are charges levied for custodial services for activities and programs for which students or their families are beneficiaries. Community School Coordinators reported that they have received charges for custodial services for times when the school custodian was present in the building. A fourth ambiguity is the financing of district-wide opportunities for students. We heard that the district pays a large sum of money to the science centre for student programming and that community schools are also asked to pay fees for the same programming. There is episodic contact between Community School Coordinators and the staff and administration in the schools to which they are assigned. While there may be a regularly scheduled meeting between a principal and a Community School Coordinator each term or each month, most contact is brief and purposeful: to inform the administrator about a program or activity being contemplated, to elicit names of vulnerable students for an activity or a program from which they would benefit, to arrange transportation, or any of a number of other functional reasons. The nature and duration of the contact between Community School Coordinators is affected by the proclivities of the principals and the demands on principals and the Community School Coordinators. In some cases, the principal indicated that s/he was only too happy to give wide discretion to the Community School Coordinator; in other schools the principal expressed a desire to be more in control of the Community School Coordinator activities. In practice, and for the most part, the Community School Coordinators have great autonomy, receiving little direction for the work that they do apart from administrative (financial) matters. Thus, it is not surprising that, given their autonomy, Community School Coordinators have little accountability to others at either the district level or school level. There was consensus among community school principals on many of the issues canvassed in our interview. Most, but not all, principals agreed with the statement that their community school provides services and supports to students and their families that are different from those provided by schools without the community school designation. The community school principals valued the time and knowledge that Community School Coordinators brought to their work. They felt that without a Community School Coordinator, the school could not do nearly as much because no one else on staff would have the knowledge of the community and the time to direct towards programming. In this respect, food and food security programs were mentioned by almost all of the community school principals as critical to the school and dependent upon the efforts of the Community School Coordinators. In one school, the principal said that approximately one-quarter of the school’s population is provided with breakfast and lunch each day. Principals mentioned a wide range of ‘additional programs’ that, depending upon the school, were offered before, during, and after school and that they believed would not be offered if the schools were not community schools. These included: cooking, yoga, lunch-time basketball, and after-school sports. Several principals mentioned that there were students who had received mittens, socks, shoes, jackets and, in at least one circumstance, a bicycle because of the efforts of Community School Coordinators. The absence of these things was described as an obstacle to school attendance. Several principals mentioned grants that had been obtained because of the efforts of the Community School Coordinators, providing support for programs such as cardio fitness, dance, and field trips. These grant-funded programs were provided on a grade-wide or school-wide basis and were perceived to be a benefit for all. It was suggested that, in the absence of the community school designation, the programs would not be offered. At least one of the community school principals indicated having tried to organize similar activities in a previous (non-community) school, reporting that it took significant effort and time. Another principal disagreed with the statement that her community school provides Directions Evidence and Policy Research Group, LLP Page 14 of 67 School District No. 68 – Community School Program Review services and supports to students and their families that are different from those provided by schools without the community school designation, noting that many schools provide additional supports to students. There was general agreement that the priorities in the community schools were to (1) reduce risky behaviour, (2) reduce attendance avoidance and other behaviours that might lead to early school leaving, and (3) improve academic performance. When asked, several principals said or implied that improving student success was the responsibility of the educators in the school and occurs during school hours. Community School Coordinators organize activities and programs that occur primarily out of school time that focus on activities such as liaising with the child and youth worker to organize presentations for parents on violence prevention, arranging for the SPCA to bring dogs to whom the children read, and getting the nurse to provide advice about health matters. Principals considered that reducing risky behaviour and making connections between families and the school were indirect influences upon academic success. Organizing spring and summer break activities were seen in a similar context as engaging students in positive activities with peers who will not lead them to do things they should not and absorbing their time productively. Community School Coordinators were, in most cases, seen as essential in coordinating and brokering services, activities, and commodities such as food, clothing, and footwear. In the absence of the Community School Coordinators, schools would find it difficult, and for some things impossible, to coordinate, broker, and organize. Principals who were inclined might try to fill some of the gaps themselves or by enlisting the assistance of other professional and support staff. They would be limited, however, if they did not have the connections to community and knowledge of the resources and grants available. In other words, it would take significant effort to assume responsibility for all the things that a Community School Coordinator does, and, without those things, the benefits to students, families, and the wider community would be lost. Sustaining the effort from one school year to the next would be very difficult. Turnover and burnout among those who might perform the work in the absence of community school coordinators were thought to inevitable. Although shared among many, these sentiments were not universal. “Just give me the $25,000 (perceived salary and benefits of a half-time Community School Coordinator) and we will do everything in-house: field trips, transportation, food, paying for the programs and initiatives [by] using the child and family support worker, the counsellor, and the leadership team, etc.,” said one principal. Directions Evidence and Policy Research Group, LLP Page 35 of 67 School District No. 68 – Community School Program Review Section 3: Value for Money Value-for-money refers, in general, to the objective of using resources to maximize the resulting benefits. In a narrow sense, a value-for-money approach may consider simply whether the value of the outcome of a program or initiative exceeds its costs. In a broader sense, and as a tool for decision-making, a value-for-money analysis is a method to compare programs to evaluate where the highest returns are to be found. The Directions team was asked to determine whether SD 68 received value for money for its investment in community schools. To make such an appraisal, it is necessary to show that community schools and non-community schools differ in their impact upon students, parents, and communities. If there are no differences between school types (community schools and non-community schools), one cannot compute the value for money of the SD 68’s investment in community schools. We identified benefits that might be attributed to community schools and compared community schools with non-community schools with respect to those benefits. Two broad categories of interdependent benefits were identified. One benefit is academic performance. Academic performance refers to performance in the classroom or on standardized tests. The second benefit for community schools is enhancing social or community capital, the sense that a community has of a common identity and willingness to work together and support one another. This might be realized as stronger community engagement with the school, or as access to programs that would provide children with opportunities that they would not otherwise have. We were confined to examining available data that have a plausible connection to the benefits/impacts because, once an initiative is underway, there are few, if any, opportunities to create measurement tools and to test their applicability and sensitivity. We used two primary sources of data about the impact of community schools on students, the prime category of beneficiaries of community schooling: (1) student performance data on the Foundation Skills Assessment, an annual appraisal of student performance in reading, writing and mathematics, and (2) student responses on the Student Learning Survey. The student compositions of community and non-community schools differ. Compared to noncommunity schools, community schools have higher proportions of Indigenous students, English Language Learners, and students with special education needs, students who are often regarded as ones needing additional supports in order to succeed in school. Community schools receive additional support in the form of Community School Coordinators in part because of Directions Evidence and Policy Research Group, LLP Page 36 of 67 School District No. 68 – Community School Program Review factors associated with these differences that make learning more challenging for learners regarded as vulnerable (e.g., because of family or community poverty, parental distrust or alienation from school, lack of familiarity with public schooling). We anticipated that there would be gaps in academic performance outcomes given the differences in student composition between the two school types. We were interested to learn whether the gap between students in community schools and students in non-community schools had diminished over time. Our investigations found that, compared to non-community schools, the community schools do not improve the academic performance of students on the Foundation Skills Assessment nor do they have a measurable impact on the way that students respond to the Student Learning Survey. If academic performance were the sole parameter of benefits expected from community schools, the value-for-money proposition would fail. However, there is a second parameter of benefits: extension of community engagement and creation of social capital. While we have anecdotal evidence about this aspect of community schools, there are no qualitative or quantitative metrics that we can construct to measure this. What we can say is that our analysis does give the Board a clear basis for decision-making. If the Board is of the view that these “social engagement” benefits (described in more detail below) are worth the cost of the program, then the value-for-money proposition succeeds. Section 4: Considerations on Nanaimo Ladysmith Community Schools and Recommendations The Community Schools Concept It is difficult to distinguish between the concept of a community school (the general idea of a community school) and peoples’ perceptions of a community school. Community schools are described as places (i.e., the school itself) and as partnerships. The partnership notion sees the school as the locus of relationships between the school and community resources that have been mobilized to serve the students attending the community school, their parents, and others in the community who may not be directly involved in the community school (see, for example, http://www.communityschools.org/aboutschools/what_is_a_community_school.aspx). Directions Evidence and Policy Research Group, LLP Page 37 of 67 School District No. 68 – Community School Program Review The literature devoted to community schools suggests that the mobilization of school and community resources (often including health, social services, child protection, etc.) will improve student academic performance, family engagement, and build social capital in the surrounding community. A related, though not a necessary, condition of community schools is their location in communities that need additional supports or opportunities to enable their community members to flourish. The partnerships between community schools and community organizations are thought to be instrumental in ensuring that children come to school prepared, the parents are engaged in and supportive of the school and its programs, and that community school students have opportunities that students in more affluent communities commonly enjoy (see, for example, https://www.nccs.org/vision-and-mission). Community schools, often described as neighbourhood hubs or centres, build social capital by fostering connectedness within the community and fostering a sense of community ownership of its community school (https://www1.nyc.gov/site/communityschools/index.page) through the promotion of programs and services that extend beyond the hours of school operation. Thus, designating a school as a community school requires significant effort and resources (e.g., providing programs and services, partnership building, mobilization of community resources) beyond the assignment of a Community School Coordinator. The Community School Concept Applied in School District 68 Community schools in SD 68 exhibit some of the characteristics of community schools as they are described above, but there is little coherence to the programs and supports that they provide to students, parents, and community members. Student, family and community needs are said to be considered by the Community School Coordinators, who, by their own admission say that the programs and services provided reflect their interests, their perceptions of what is needed, and the needs expressed to them by staff at the schools. Many programs and services are ‘opportunities’ offered by agencies and organizations outside the school from whom the Community School Coordinators have negotiated reduced fees or designated places for students. Other programs and services offered at the school are ones that the Community School Coordinator has solicited based upon the Coordinators’ knowledge of the surrounding community. Acquiring a bicycle for a student who, in its absence, would not come to school, or providing a jacket or shoes that a student is perceived to need are examples of the resourcefulness of the Coordinators. Organizing and helping to provide food for students or families in need are important dimensions of the Coordinators’ work. They do not conduct formal needs assessments and only meet with school staff on an episodic “as needed” basis. SD 68’s community schools have produced tangible benefits for some students and their families, and, in doing so, added to the community capital of the neighbourhoods served by those schools. Had there been discernable differences in student performance and student attitudes, it might have been possible for us to indicate whether SD 68 is receiving value for its investment. We could not detect academic (i.e., FSA) or attitudinal differences (i.e., SLS) between students at community and non-community schools that would be necessary for a value for money calculation on those dimensions. We did note that despite the perception of difference between community and non-community schools, non-community schools attempt to provide similar supports, activities, and programs for students, though not the range that is provided in community schools. In aid of helping the Board of School Trustees of SD 68 to arrive at a decision about the disposition of community schools, we have framed a few questions: • • Is the Board satisfied with the present state of community schooling in SD 68 and its lack of systematic evidence of impact, such that the Board is willing to continuing funding them as they presently operate? Is the building community capital benefit of community schools enough that, relative to other Board priorities, community schools should be maintained, and efforts made to more closely align them with Board priorities in order to produce measurable improvement in student outcomes? If it is the Board’s intention to retain or expand community schools in SD 68, there are several things it might do that would enhance the likelihood of impact. Recommendations Aligning community schools with Board priorities The gap between current community school practice in SD 68 and the concept of community schooling would be reduced if SD 68 articulated clear, coherent expectations for its community schools; provided consistent direction to the work of the Community School Coordinators; and Directions Evidence and Policy Research Group, LLP Page 39 of 67 School District No. 68 – Community School Program Review held them accountable for fulfilling the expectations. In its budget criteria for 2019-2020, two priorities set by the Board seem especially relevant to community schools and the efforts of Community School Coordinators (Budget Guiding Principles, https://www.sd68.bc.ca/board/2019-20-budget-process/): • • Maximize success/completion rates for our most vulnerable students Maximize support for student learning in literacy and numeracy We believe that, if continued or expanded, these priorities should guide the efforts of Community School Coordinators and the specific programming and services that are provided to community school students and their families. If community schools are able to improve the success/completion rates for the most vulnerable students and improve student learning in literacy and numeracy, they will help build the social and educational capital in the communities they serve. Balancing autonomy and accountability The operation of community schools in SD 68 would improve if more explicit expectations were set for: the relationship between Community School Coordinators and the principals of the schools to which they are assigned, the scheduling of meetings between them, the participation of community school coordinators in meetings with parent advisory councils, and service continuity when one Community School Coordinator is leaving a school and another starting work in that school. There is no shortage of needs in community or non-community schools. Community School Coordinators must have a means of prioritizing their efforts. Formal needs assessments, annual plans, and specific, measurable objectives might be helpful to everyone and beneficial to the recipients of the services and supports. We would not want to eliminate the spontaneity that Community School Coordinators bring to the role, but we think that it should occur within a framework that is aligned to the purposes identified by SD 68. SD 68 should explore the feasibility of organizing recurrent services and supports (e.g., sport and recreation activities, food and food security programs) at the district-level so long as delivery and distribution remained at the school level. Counts of participants (“turnstile statistics”) do not provide evidence of the impact of programs and services on recipients. SD 68 should develop indicators and metrics that are aligned with its priorities for community schools. If, for example, a program or programs is intended to improve Directions Evidence and Policy Research Group, LLP Page 40 of 67 School District No. 68 – Community School Program Review student attendance or to diminish disruptive behaviour, then an appropriate indicator and related metric should be used to determine whether the desired outcome has been achieved. Improving coordination and operations The Board: • Should consider establishing a formal coordinator responsible for community schools who should convene regular meetings with the Community School Coordinators, work with them to forge a shared understanding of the mission and philosophy of community schooling in SD 68, and explain the role of community school coordinators to the board, senior administration, school-based administration and teaching professionals. The coordinator should possess grant writing skills to provide support for this aspect of the Community School Coordinators’ work. • Should develop and apply clear criteria for activities and programs that are organized under the auspices of Community School Coordinators and those that are simply direct rentals. • Should not recover custodial costs for approved community school programs and activities from community school coordinators; such costs should be absorbed by the Board as part of the over-all budgeting for community schools. • Should provide bookkeeping support for community school coordinators either from one of their schools or from the central office to (a) reduce the administrative burden on community school coordinators to (b) free up time for their main functions. Conclusion The Board of School Trustees of SD 68 faces a difficult decision about the disposition of community schools. If the Board decides to continue community schools, it must accept that the contribution of community schools to building community capital is, in the short run, sufficiently consistent with Board priorities that is worth further investment in order to determine whether, in the long term, community schools can make a systematic and sustained contribution to the improvement of student outcomes.
