identify demand for potential marine-specific services and retail space; and, • Present pro forma financial analysis for new water lots in the Waterfront Plan area This report is structured as follows: Section 2: Filled Foreshore Assessment Section 3: Water Lots Analysis Section 4: Discussion of the “Intrinsic Value” of the Town-Stz’uminus partnership Section 5: Conclusions 1.2 BACKGROUND The Town of Ladysmith and the Stz’uminus First Nation are working together to develop a plan for the Ladysmith Waterfront area. In 2017, the Town of Ladysmith commissioned Urban Systems to conduct a market review and financial analysis of the Ladysmith Waterfront area to determine potential residential, commercial and light industrial market opportunities, and land values, for the area. That initial analysis was presented in the Ladysmith Waterfront Lands Market and Financial Analysis report, dated September 12, 2017. This report is a companion document that focusses on market demand and opportunities for the filled foreshore area and a financial analysis of expanding marina space in Ladysmith in Water Lot 651. 2 Page 192 of 224 2 FILLED FORESHORE ANALYSIS The “filled foreshore” area of the Town of Ladysmith’s Waterfront Plan is located near the existing parking lot at the north end of the site. This area is envisioned to support marine-specific service and retail space. This section provides an overview of specific market opportunities for the filled foreshore area. Key areas of market opportunity that have been identified are: • Marine-specific retail and services • A variety of marine -related services including fuel and border services, as well as promoting the existing pump-out service. 2.1 MARINE-SPECIFIC RETAIL AND SERVICES Through conversations with local marine-service operators, there is reportedly minimal need for marine-specific retail or service space in the Ladysmith waterfront filled foreshore area. Ladysmith Marine Services, located at the north end of the Ladysmith waterfront area, noted an increased demand over the last few years for general maintenance on boats, which requires boats be hauled out of the water. Because of increased business, Ladysmith Marine Services expressed interest in possibly expanding to include a chandlery but identified no imminent plans to expand. No specific space needs or improved dock access is required to support existing marine services. Generally, marine-service operators and marine operators in the region felt the available services were sufficient. One marine services business is reported to have opened and closed in Ladysmith in the last 18 months. Local marine-service operators identified demand and room in the market for increased marine recovery and oil containment services in the Ladysmith area. It is reported the filled foreshore area could support a marine services training facility that would require a mix of building floor space for inclass learning and waterfront access for hands-on learning. This type of facility could compliment the marina well but would need a specific interested party. The Ladysmith waterfront is seen to cater mostly to pleasure craft and yachts and the marina could be expanded to better accommodate commercial fishing vessels. Commercial vessels are reported to be based largely out of northern Vancouver Island and could be expanded further into Ladysmith if additional commercial space and access was available in the marina. The fisherman’s marina in Ladysmith is generally full when most of the commercial fisheries have ended. The Fisherman’s Warf Association is planning on expanding their breakwater to allow for an increased capacity of approximately 15 – 20 average sized commercial boats. 3 Page 193 of 224 2.2 MARINA ENHANCEMENTS When considering additional services in the proposed marina expansion area, it is important to note what potential marine services could require land space. Based on a series of discussions between Urban Systems and marina staff in and around the Ladysmith area, the following have been identified as possible service enhancements at the marina: • Marine fuel • Promote sewage pump-out • Air travel and border services 2.2.1 MARINE FUEL 1 Pleasure craft are currently going to Thetis Island or Chemainus for fuel. Offering marine fuel in the marina expansion area was cited by multiple local marina and marine-service operators as the top service that could be provided/improved. A fuel dock will encourage visitors who are destined for Ladysmith marinas to purchase fuel at the marina expansion area rather than stopping elsewhere to fuel up first. Placing a fuel dock on the outer edges of the marina expansion area where it can be seen by passing boat traffic will encourage boaters not docking at the marina to buy fuel and could encourage new visitors to the Ladysmith waterfront area. Water depth at the fuel dock location would need to be approximately 1520’ at low tide to ensure no dredging is required. When considering a fuel dock, the location of holding tanks and the land space required are significant considerations. Marine fuel is piped from holding tanks on land to the fuel dock. Fuel holding tanks must meet strict safety and security requirements such as a chain link fence, barbed wire, and concrete blocks (or similar) to protect tanks from vehicle impact. Because of these security requirements, fuel holding tanks are often visually unappealing. To address this challenge, fuel tanks could be located further back from public view; however, the cost of piping is expensive and should be carefully considered when choosing the location of the holding tanks. Potential marine fuel operators may not be willing to absorb any additional cost if tanks are located further away from the dock than necessary. The amount of land required for fuel holding tanks will depend on the size of tanks and how many types of fuel are offered. Based on discussions with marine fuel operators in the region, the ____________ 1 Detailed marine fuel services information provided by Doug Smith, Port Alberni Marine Fuels and Services. 4 Page 194 of 224 Ladysmith waterfront area may need approximately two 22,500 litre capacity tanks if offering only gasoline. For comparison, Port Alberni Marine Fuels and Services, operating at the Port Alberni harbour, currently have two fuel holding tanks on site - one 45,000 litre capacity for diesel and one 22,500 litre capacity for gasoline. These two tanks occupy an area (including security requirements) of approximately 22’ wide by 70’ long (1,540 square feet). Ladysmith would need approximately 100’ x 20’ of uninterrupted steel and concrete dock to accommodate a fuel dock and small store. A dock this size could accommodate 2 – 45’ boats on one side and could service commercial vessels from the other side. A small store located on the fuel dock would support marine fuel sales due to the higher margin on snack foods than fuel. 2.2.2 SEWAGE PUMP-OUT Marine-service operators in the region highlighted the need for a sanitary pump-out service in the marina expansion area. There is currently a sewage pump-out located on the outer edge of the harbour available to all marina users. Since this available service was not widely known, the sewage pump-out service could be reviewed to ensure it is well marketed and that visiting vessels are aware of its location. No new land space would be required as this service is already available. 2.2.3 AIR TRAVEL AND BORDER SERVICES Kenmore Air currently operates seaplanes between Seattle and Nanaimo. If Ladysmith were interested in attracting seaplanes to the marina expansion area, this option could be explored with the airline and border services. Charter flights to Ladysmith, particularly in the summer months, could be explored in addition to, or instead of, regularly scheduled flights from Seattle. Canadian Border Services Agency (CBSA) could be approached to discuss the feasibility of operating a small customs office from the expanded marina, with or without seaplane service. Border services reportedly operate in Nanaimo from a dedicated dock and board vessels that need to clear customs. Currently, if vessels travelling north from the U.S. do not clear customs at Bedwell Harbour, on Pender Island, they must travel to Nanaimo to clear customs. If either of these new services were to be offered in the Ladysmith area, it’s reasonable to assume small office space may be required in the filled foreshore area. 5 Page 195 of 224 3 NEW WATER LOTS ANALYSIS The existing Ladysmith Community Marina is operated by the Ladysmith Marine Society, a non-profit charitable organization, that operates the marina adjacent to the Town of Ladysmith-owned lands. Part of the Waterfront Area Plan for the redevelopment of the Town of Ladysmith owned lands includes the expansion of marina space adjacent to the Ladysmith Community Marina. Water lot 651, to the east of the existing marina, and west of Slack Point, is where the marina expansion is being considered. Currently, this water lot is being used by many anchored live-aboard vessels that are moored in the area for free. There are many issues with the boats anchored in this water lot, including multiple boats that have caught fire, others that have sunk, and boats that appear to have been abandoned. The Town of Ladysmith has been engaging with people moored on this water lot to provide them an opportunity to plan where they might move once the expansion of the marina begins. 3.1 CURRENT MARINA The existing Ladysmith Community Marina includes three primary docks (Dock A, B, and C), and includes 187 slips for annual or seasonal moorage, and approximately 1,000 feet of dock space that can be used by visiting boaters. • Dock A houses 96 slips, including 22 boathouses where boats are covered from the weather. • Dock C includes 46 slips. • Dock B is reserved for visitors, though in the summer months visitors can be directed to slips used by annual members who are away boating. • The revenue to the Marina from Dock B is lower than Dock A or C. However, Dock B does provide significant economic spinoff benefits to the community due to spending from visitors in the Town of Ladysmith. Visitors to the marina also support the Marina through merchandise and marina supply sales. The existing marina includes a welcome centre with a café, meeting rooms, and showers for visiting boaters. The marina also includes a museum featuring historical boating equipment, as well as a sewage pump-out for all marina users. Revenue from the existing marina was approximately $750,000 in 2017. This includes $116,000 in visitor moorage fees. Other sources of revenue for the marina include: Tour Boat income, sales of merchandise, and the provision of marina services to boaters. Visiting boaters also support the marina through donations, for example for using the showers provided. 6 Page 196 of 224 3.2 MARINA MARKET REVIEW This section provides an overview of the competitive environment for marinas close to Ladysmith, which will shape the opportunity for new marina space. 3.2.1 COMPETITION Vancouver Island has a strong boating community and is a popular summer destination for recreation boaters. High season demand at marinas is driven by residents (full-time and seasonal) who moor their boats for extended periods of time, and tourists arriving by boat from the US or other parts of BC. In low season, marinas in the Ladysmith area reported some slip availability, mostly for 30’ and smaller vessels. Smaller boats are typically hauled out in the off season which contributes to seasonal moorage availability. Marinas in the Ladysmith area reported they typically experience high demand for boat moorage in high season and expect to be operating at capacity. Some marinas offer transient moorage to visiting vessels when long-term moorage vessels are away from the marina. This practice is common and maximizes space while increasing revenues. Through a relationship, a project will have a better opportunity to be prioritized for grants, and to obtain the lowest financing rates.
