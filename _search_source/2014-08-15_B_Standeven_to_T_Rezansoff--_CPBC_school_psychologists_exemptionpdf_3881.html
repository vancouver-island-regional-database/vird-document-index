August 15, 2014 Teresa Rezansoff, President BC School Trustees Association Email: Teresa.rezansoff@sd51.bc.ca Dear Teresa Rezansoff, I am writing to request a letter of support from your organization endorsing a pathway that the College of Psychologists of BC (CPBC) has recently created to enable school psychologists to become registrants within the CPBC and, therefore, a regulated profession under the Health Professions Act. The regulation of school psychologist services under the College of Psychologists of British Columbia has been a topic of discussion between the Ministry of Education and Ministry of Health for a number of years. I have provided a brief background of past discussion (from 1987 to 2007) as an attachment to this letter. Since 2010, the British Columbia Association of School Psychologists (BCASP) and the College of Psychologists of British Columbia (CPBC) have participated in discussions to address: (1) how to maintain the role of school psychologists in school districts (2) how to secure the provision of psychological services in school districts (3) how to resolve concern over the removal of an exemption clause in the Psychologists Regulation in the Health Professions Act that allows Masters level psychologists to practice in an educational setting The Ministry of Education believes it is essential that practitioners working in school psychology positions in the British Columbia school system are credentialed by the professional regulatory body of the province in which they work, together with other professional psychologists practicing in British Columbia. CPBC has determined a way forward for regulating school psychological services – a pathway that will enable BCASP members to transition into the College through a ‘special register’. Under the ‘special register’, school psychologists would be entitled to continue using the title ‘school psychologist’. The scope of their practice would be limited to supporting students in school settings, and to the assessment and diagnosis of learning disabilities and intellectual disabilities – which is their current scope of practice in the school system. Registration with the CPBC would include the requirement that members adhere to CPBC standards of conduct. Alignment with the CPBC standards of conduct would also enable school psychologists to better advocate for higher standards regarding other aspects of their practice, e.g., obtaining informed consent, maintaining records, and supervising interns. Regulation by CPBC would also provide parents, students, and other persons supported by the services of school psychologists, access to the College’s formalized complaint process should issues of fairness and/or due process arise. School Psychologists certified by BCASP undergo rigorous scrutiny to ensure they meet current criteria specific to the scope of school psychology. Presently, BCASP includes among its membership a number of school psychology practitioners who are currently registrants within CPBC. The Ministry of Education supports this proposed pathway. Government believes the regulation of school psychologists by the CPBC would increase the level of professionalism among school psychologists throughout British Columbia. Regulation under the CPBC standards of practice and conduct would also enable school psychologists to better advocate for higher standards with respect to other aspects of their practice, e.g., obtaining informed consent and maintaining records. Oversight and regulation by the CPBC would assure members of the public that services provided by school psychologists are safe and the professionals providing services are accountable. We are currently preparing to meet and brief the Deputy Ministers of Education and Health before proceeding with regulation of school psychologists under CPBC in accordance with the process outlined above. We believe the importance of providing exemplary service is critical when working with some of British Columbia’s most vulnerable population – our children and youth. CPBC has determined a way forward for regulating school psychological services that will minimize disruption to school psychology services currently provided to boards and authorities across the province. A letter from your organization supporting regulation of school psychologists under CPBC and endorsing CPBC’s proposed pathway for porting BCASP members over to CPBC under a ‘special registry’ would be very much appreciated. Sincerely, Bill Standeven, Director Learning Supports and Early Years Learning Division Attachment: A brief background of past discussion (1987 to 2007) re the regulation of school psychologist services under the College of Psychologists of British Columbia Since 1987, school psychologists employed by school boards in the British Columbia school system have been exempted from required membership in the College of Psychologists of BC (CPBC), and have been permitted to use the title ‘psychologist’ in carrying out their duties. In 2001, the Health Professions Council, in a report to Government, recommended that the use of the title ‘psychologist’ be limited to members of the College and that exemptions from this requirement be removed. Government has not formally acted on these recommendations to date. In 2005, Ministry of Health legislation extended the exemption from membership in CPBC to school psychologists working for boards of education. This was done with the proviso that the Ministry of Education make every effort to cause persons doing this work in school districts to either join the College or meet BC Association of School Psychologists (BCASP) standards. April 2005, the Deputy Ministers of Education and Health wrote to all school districts, communicating the extension of the exemption from required membership in the College for three to five years, and an expectation that school psychologists employed by school districts meet the requirements of either the CPBC or BCASP. The Ministry of Education partnered with BCASP to encourage individuals who were practicing school psychology without membership in either CPBC or BCASP to obtain membership in one or the other of these organizations. The Ministry provided a grant to BCASP to assist practitioners meet BCASP standards, for example, by helping them prepare for the required exam. Some practitioners took advantage of this opportunity; others took action to meet the standards for CPBC membership. For five years, beginning in the 2006/07 school year, the Ministry of Education monitored progress toward school psychologist membership in CPBC and formal complaints related to school psychologist practice to determine whether further action would be required to ensure a high standard of practice. During this five-year period there were no complaints made by members of the public regarding the practice of school psychology in British Columbia school districts or independent schools. Since 2010, the Ministry of Education, the Ministry of Health, BCASP and CPBC have continued to discuss the regulation of school psychologist services.
