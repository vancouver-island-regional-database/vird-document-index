Many people still think of it as a disorder, a flaw, or something that needs fixing, but that couldn’t be further from the truth. Autistic people experience the world in ways that are just as valid, meaningful, and important as the ways non-autistic people do. The more we challenge outdated ideas and autism myths, the closer we get to real understanding and acceptance. 1. Is everyone a little autistic? No. While many people might relate to certain autistic traits, autism is a neurodevelopmental condition that affects how we think, process information, and experience the world. It’s not just a personality type or set of quirks. It’s a fundamental part of how our brains work. Autism is a lifelong condition: autistic children grow into autistic adults. What you can do: Respect autistic people by acknowledging that autism is real and 34 specific to those who are autistic. 2. Why can some autistic adults work while others cannot? Autistic people have varying daily support needs, communication styles, and workplace experiences. Health conditions, burnout, sensory needs, and discrimination can all impact a person’s ability to work. No two autistic experiences are exactly the same. What you can do: Employers can foster inclusivity by listening to autistic employees, offering accommodations from the interview stage, and valuing autistic strengths in a neuroaffirming environment. Autistic people often have unique strengths, such as deep focus on special interests and strong pattern recognition skills, which can make them well-suited for certain careers. 3. When someone is diagnosed with autism, other people often ask why. Is it because of vaccines, parenting styles, or something similar? This curiosity often stems from the desire to find a cause and, in some cases, a cure for autism. But autism doesn’t need a cure—autistic people don’t need a cure. It’s not a disease; autism is a neurotype. Autism is not “caused” by vaccines, diet, parenting styles, or anything else you might have heard. Autism is a genetic, lifelong neurodevelopmental condition. We’re born autistic, and we stay that way. What you can do: While curiosity is natural, it’s more important to focus on providing support and making the world more accessible and inclusive for people of different neurotypes. Help stop misinformation by challenging harmful myths like this and sharing accurate information from autistic-led, trusted sources. 4. Can autistic people understand how others feel? Yes. The belief that autistic people lack empathy is a misunderstanding. This is known as the double empathy problem—just as non-autistic people may struggle to understand autistic experiences, autistic people may find it difficult to relate to non-autistic experiences. Many autistic individuals experience deep empathy, sometimes even more intensely than others, but may express it differently. Some autistic people have a hard time identifying, naming, and understanding their feelings; this is sometimes known as alexithymia What you can do: Challenge stereotypes about autism by learning about the double empathy problem. 5. Can an autistic person be talkative, or are they usually nonverbal? Autistic people have different communication styles. Some may be very talkative, especially about topics they are passionate about, while others may be nonverbal. Nonverbal autistic people often communicate using Augmented and Alternative Communication Devices (AAC) devices, apps, visual aids, gestures, or body language. What you can do: Respect all forms of communication, whether spoken or not. Give those using AAC the time and space they need to express themselves. 6. Should we assume non-speaking autistic people don’t understand what’s being said? No. Just because someone is non-speaking doesn’t mean they don’t understand. Many non-speaking autistic people have a deep awareness of what’s happening around them, even if they can’t express it in the same way. Assuming they can’t process conversations is both unfair and dismissive. What you can do: Advocate for non-speaking autistic people by using respectful language and reminding others that presence doesn’t require speech. 7. Do you need a diagnosis to be autistic? Autism is a neurotype, not just a diagnosis. Many autistic people, especially adults, go undiagnosed due 35 to barriers like cost, healthcare bias, or lack of access to specialists. Some are misdiagnosed as children because they don’t fit outdated views of what autism “looks like.” Long waitlists also mean many kids go without a diagnosis for a while, but that doesn’t mean they’re not autistic. While some people choose to pursue a diagnosis—which is completely valid—it’s often to access support, funding, or to better understand themselves and their experiences. A diagnosis can be a helpful tool, but it doesn’t define whether someone is truly autistic. Recognizing and respecting someone’s identity— diagnosed or not—helps build a more inclusive community. So no, you don’t need a formal diagnosis to be autistic. Did you know? In the past, autistic people with diagnoses were told they had certain “types” of autism, which is outdated; now, there’s just the blanket diagnosis of “autism” What you can do: Support autistic people by respecting their identity—diagnosed or not. 8. I usually hear about autism in boys and men—can someone who isn’t a boy or man be autistic, too? No. The misconception that autism mainly affects boys comes from outdated research that focuses on white, cisgender boys. We now know autism presents differently in different people, leading to many autistic girls, women, and non-binary individuals being overlooked or misdiagnosed. Some autistic people mask—meaning they copy neurotypical social behaviours to fit in. Others may struggle with social interactions or choose not to mask because it’s exhausting. Socializing looks different for everyone, and masking isn’t always sustainable. Did you know? There is no one way to be autistic, and every autistic person is different. What you can do: Instead of expecting autistic people to socialize a certain way, support them by accepting their natural communication styles. 10. The name was bestowed by Commander John Parry of the Royal Navy survey vessel Egeria. Sibell Brandon was the sister of one of Parry’s officers, Lieutenant Vivian Brandon, who was involved in charting the area that year. Historical and Cultural Significance Settler History: The naming reflects the British naval tradition of naming geographical features after family members or colleagues of the surveying crew. Indigenous Connection: Long before colonial naming, the bay was a vital site for the Stz’uminus (Chemainus) First Nations. It served as a traditional village site. Ancient Roots: Archaeological digs at Sibell Bay have uncovered hearths carbon-dated to over 7,000 years old, indicating it has been a place of human habitation for millennia. 39 DUNSMIUR ISLANDS Dunsmuir Islands (the two islands across from Shell Beach) The Dunsmuir Islands (located near Ladysmith in Oyster Harbour, British Columbia) were named after James Dunsmuir (1851–1920). Who was James Dunsmuir? He was the son of the prominent British Columbian coal baron, Robert Dunsmuir. James was the founder of the town of Ladysmith, served as the Premier of British Columbia (1900–1902), and was later the Lieutenant-Governor (1906–1909). How were they named? The islands were christened by Cdr. James Parry of the HMS Egeria during a re-survey of Ladysmith (then Oyster Harbour) in 1904. Here are the details regarding the naming of Kulleet Bay: Indigenous Origin: The name refers to the original inhabitants of the area, who are members of the Stz’uminus First Nation (part of the Chemainus First Nation). Meaning: The name signifies the bay’s role as a protected, sheltered place. Naming by Admiralty: The British Admiralty formally adopted the name “Kulleet Bay” in 1895. Previous Names: Before 1895, the bay appeared on Admiralty charts as “Chemainus Bay,” a name given by Captain George Richards during an 1859 survey, referencing the nearby important Hul’qumi’num village (Shts’emines).
