Save Cedar Schools MOVING FORWARD Where We Have Been  Save Cedar Schools started as a grass roots coalition working to keep K-12 in our community.  When the 10 year facilities plan was released many people in the community were shocked by the intention to close all 5 schools in our community (Cedar Secondary, Woodbank, NCI N. Oyster plus S.Wellington on our border)  This plan involved consolidating the 4 elementary schools into our high school, and sending the high school students into Nanaimo to attend John Barsby.  From the initial stages of this plan, there has been an over whelming response from the community of Cedar, as well as the entire district. The plan had numerous flaws!  SCS has 6,500 signatures on a petition that support the concerns with the impacts of this plan.  There was a great deal of concern around the lack of consultation from the initial stages of the plan right through to the closure of CCSS.  The community in Cedar saw this plan as an attempt to fill seats at John Barsby under the guise of increasing learning opportunities.  We were told that we were going to benefit from our high school students having access to an increase in course options only available at larger schools.  We were told the conversion of the high school into Cedar Elementary was going to be state of the art and provide our students with access to 21st century learning with a focus on the arts.  Important to highlight that there were no issues with course offerings at CCSS. The reality is that CCSS was offering a myriad of courses comparable with the rest of the district.  CCSS was offering a full compliment of core courses and as a community school was committed to providing unique courses: outdoor ed, musical theatre, beginner guitar lessons, girls fitness class, girls basketball class, artisan wood working classes focused on jewelry, metal work, guitar and furniture building.  Other activities created through our community school were: Boys to Men, Spectrum Grief Support, Leadership and Mentorship, Pizza /Gaming night and Mountain Bike Club.  These are the kinds of activities that make the difference to students who live in a small rural community. The kind of activities that they can stay for after school or head out to in the evening. (All gone now with the closure of CCSS) Small School Benefits  There are numerous benefits to smaller schools.  In smaller schools, teachers and administrators have the ability to make special provisions for individual needs and talents (such as the guitar making course created to help focus a group of grade 8/9 wood working students)  Just as important is the ability for individualized instruction and attention that is given to students who might slip through the cracks.  Before moving on to where we are, I want to highlight a few key points about CCSS:  At the point of closure the school was at 107% capacity.  Had the 3rd highest graduation rate in SD68  Was ranked the 6th most improved high school in the province according to the Fraser Inst.  So all in all an amazing and successful community school, that was only 12 years old and completely full, ended up being closed down in an attempt to consolidate students into the city in the hopes to secure funding for a new school. (*Funding that does not exist presently, due to the provinces stance on new builds) Where We Are Now  Well things did not go according to plan, so it’s important to highlight how things have shifted.  The former board agreed to halt the closure of N.Oyster and turn it into a combined English/French school to house the displaced Ecole Davis Students from Ladysmith. As well a majority of students from S.Wellington chose to go to Chase River. This led to a change in the anticipated numbers for the new Cedar Elementary. Initially thought to be around 450 students, now dropping down to 250.  Roughly 60% of the CCSS students opted to attend Ladysmith Secondary rather than John Barsby.  With a remaining 40% of students choosing Barsby, NDSS & Woodlands, this did not achieve the desired placement of students.  So we have 60% of our Cedar students travelling to Ladysmith either via family transport ($100/month in gas) or for fee busing arranged by the parents to the tune of $120 per student/month.  The remaining 40% (approx 100) of students are being bused into Barsby for approx. $250,000 per year. (estimated new buses for this plan $500,000$900,000).  The majority of students have had a horrendous time with the transition. Numerous grade 12 students chose to complete the necessary courses in the 1st term to graduate early.  Countless other families describe the sense of displacement and social issues their children face on a daily basis.  Teachers, staff & parents state that there are huge issues with the Cedar students attendance. Interesting impact, given CCSS was originally built to combat low attendance & low graduation rates. (History repeating itself).  We now have decreased access to activities as the high school acted as our community centre, and the opportunity for students to participate in extracurricular events, or maintain after school jobs has been strained.  Our values & connection to educating our children has been damaged. People choose a community to live in that represents their values and at the heart of that is the schools our children attend.  Kids are not feeling connected to their new school community and now feel disconnected from their home community that they barely spend time in.  Parents do not feel as involved with the new schools that are geographically distant. Current Thoughts on Cedar Elementary  The community has numerous concerns around the Cedar Elementary Conversion :  The cost to convert the newest school in the district is much higher than anticipated (now approx.$2.5million), all dollars that are not being spent on much older schools in need of repair.  The building regardless of renovations was built as a high school with all doors leading to a parking lot, second story mezzanines, large lockers, plumbing fixtures for teens, wood shop (all built with in 12 years for senior students, not little bodies).  CCSS is also situated along one of the busier roads in our community that lacks proper side walks or shoulders for walking. (not suitable for elementary students to travel alone).  The play grounds although partially renovated are still prone to winter flooding, offering half as much outdoor play area than either Woodbank or NCI  There is a high likelihood the school will end up half full, half staffed and partially boarded up. (A far cry from the state of the art 21st century learning facility promised in the initial plan)  So to summarize where we are:  We are a community passionate about our children having access to k-12 in Cedar.  We are a community open to collaboration and communication about how this can happen in a sensible way.  We are a community that values the successes that our Cedar Spartans have achieved. Moving Forward  Save Cedar Schools is here tonight to engage with the trustees, Sd68 staff and the entire community of SD68.  We support the idea of a new facilities in the district, we just hope that it does not come at the expense of the students in Cedar.  We support the success of the entire student body of SD68 and encourage the community at large to consider how consolidations can happen with less impact on just one community.  We acknowledge Woodbank & NCI should consolidate.  The closure of CCSS and the plan to repurpose it into Cedar Elementary is failing to deliver on promised cost savings, enhanced education and elimination of empty seats in the district.  We support the District’s efforts to fill John Barsby and to satisfy criteria for new school funding to replace NDSS, but we are of the opinion that more favourable and long lasting solutions can be found by looking at older schools with low capacity issues.  We would like to highlight:  A community school, at 107% capacity, 3rd highest graduation rate in the district and a ranking of 6th most improved high school in the province should be a flagship for SD68.  The successes of CCSS contribute to the success of the whole district and we should all be working together to generate ideas on how we can re-open CCSS and provide quality education to the students of Cedar, just as we should for all the students in SD68.
