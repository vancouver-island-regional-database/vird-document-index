January 28, 2014 Ms. Dot Neary Chair, Board of Trustees School District 68 (Nanaimo-Ladysmith) 395 Wakesiah Avenue Nanaimo, British Columbia V9R 3K6 Dear Ms. Neary School and Trustees, Subject: École Davis Road PAC response to December 18 “Action Sheet” on Ladysmith Schools On behalf of École Davis Road PAC (EDRPAC) and the families of the 250 students in our school community, we must express disappointment that our request to appear as a delegation at the January 29th Regular Board Meeting was declined, as this eliminates our only opportunity to respond publicly to the Action Sheet presented by Superintendent Hutchinson on December 18th. As a parent advisory committee, we have a legislated role to advise on matters significant to the Board and to communicate about these through our elected officers. As part of the Ladysmith Working Group, officers of EDRPAC were not allowed to consult other members of the PAC executive about the content of the working group meetings. In fact, EDRPAC has never formally presented to the Board of Trustees on the matter at hand, the future of our school, and by extension the future of education in Ladysmith. We respectfully ask therefore that the Trustees carefully consider the information presented in the attached submission from the EDRPAC Executive; it is the community’s only means to respond to the concerns raised in the Action Sheet and to address the excellent questions raised by Trustees in the last stages of the Ladysmith Extended Consultation process. We thank you in advance for taking the time to read and consider the information presented in the following pages and in Appendices A-E. Below, and in the Appendices, EDRPAC provides evidence to support the continued operation of elementary schools located in the Town of Ladysmith. We are available to respond to any questions that Trustees may have before meeting to decide the fate of our community’s schools. We also are prepared to provide a presentation at the February 6th, 2014 meeting. FACTS ABOUT FRENCH IMMERSION AT ÉCOLE DAVIS ROAD On December 18th the Superintendent called into question the viability of French Immersion (FI) at École Davis Road (EDR), stating that “grade seven attrition rates are cause for concern”. The facts below suggest an alternative understanding to that presented by the Superintendent, and are witness to the development of a successful French Immersion program at École Davis Road over the course of a few short years: 1. The FI program began in 2003 almost solely because of the tireless efforts of Ladysmith parents, with support of District staff at the time; the program is only in its 10th year, with its 4th graduating class in 2014. 2. In recent years there has been a wait list on the first day of kindergarten registration; many parents who are interested in the K-7 model are faced with a second choice between the English stream (EDR, Chemainus Elementary (8 km south) and private school St. Joseph’s (10 km south). Repeated requests by EDR PAC for the intake of students in kindergarten to be increased have been declined. A higher intake, such as facilitated at Quarterway, Pauline Haarer and Hammond Bay, would ensure greater numbers in the higher grades. 3. Kindergarten class size is restricted to 22 students; Grades 1-3 may have 24 and Grades 4-7 can have 30. Entry into early French Immersion is restricted to Kindergarten and Grade 1. The EDR principal’s ability to accept students in grade 1 is contingent on the implications on division size in future years, so applicants for grade 1 are not always able to be accommodated (despite the theoretical grade 1 class capacity of 24). Consequently, and because transfer students with prerequisite background are rare, the maximum number of Grade 7 graduates can only ever be 22, NOT 30. 4. Given the de facto ceiling of 22 students in each grade, the maximum number of students that could ever possibly be in FI at EDR is 176. We currently have 139 in FI. On the basis of kindergarten wait lists, we maintain that there is potential to expand the total enrolment if additional K/1 classes were permitted based on strong population growth in the EDR catchment area (Appendix B. Population History). a. In the 2013/2014 school year, there are 26 Kindergarten students living in the EDR area attending EDR compared to 13 in the 2012/2013 school year. The Baragar population history reveals consistent movement of families into the area over the past 6 years. The current Kindergarten aged cohort consists of 11 children born in the EDR area with an additional 17 children moving into the area. This trend is continuing. From 2008 to 2013, the number of 3 primary students (K-3) living in the area grew by 2 divisions (39). Clearly, EDR is in the right location and families are moving into the area. 5. Parents and staff at École Davis Road have worked hard to secure resources required for a successful French Immersion program, none of which were in place when the program began. We now have a bilingual librarian and literacy support. Families and community have put considerable time and money into ensuring EDR has a proper playground, and FI resources in the library and classrooms. 6. The number of returning, experienced FI teachers has grown consistently as the program has deepened, providing stability and contributing to decreasing attrition. For the first time since its inception at EDR, FI now has a straight grade 3 class with 19 students; 7. Despite the newness of the program, and uncertainty about the future that has dogged much of its existence, EDR’s attrition rate (2013) of 43% by grade 7 is very close to the rate of the other French Immersion programs in SD 68. Please note that the attrition for the current grade 6 class is 10%. EDR PAC has tracked where students in Grades 3to 7 have gone (see Appendix A). Enrolment figures provided by Canadian Parents for French indicate that the average attrition rate for the other FI elementary schools in the District is 38% for the same period 8. Students have left EDR and SD 68 completely in recent years because of uncertainty about the School District’s commitment to our school and due to effects of labour disputes, not the quality of the education they are receiving. This was especially evident at the end of the 2011/2012 year. Ten students transferred into St. Joseph’s schools. 9. The data about EDR’s FI attrition is far from statistically significant. When paired with qualitative sources of information, a reasonable alternative interpretation to that offered by senior staff in the Action Sheet is that families move to Ladysmith to attend the School District 68 French Immersion program offered at EDR; some leave when their lives take them elsewhere or when they can’t tolerate the volatility the program has had to endure; half of the latter leave the public system. Current Enrolment Projections (District Criteria 6) “History of the Number of Children in this Catchment” data (Appendix C) provided by the School District confirms that the populations of children in both North Oyster(NO) and Ladysmith Primary/Intermediate catchments are declining (by 10% from 2003 to 2013 in NO and 20% for LPS/LIS). During the same period, the population of children in the EDR catchment grew by 16%. While there has been a minimal decline of 50 children living in the NO area, the long history of non-attendance at the ‘catchment school’ has been discussed elsewhere (e.g. Matrix Report 2006). Whether due to location of students relative to the school, ‘social atmosphere issues (Matrix 2006), or available bussing to Cedar and Ladysmith schools, the result is clear. Only 23% (71) of the total population of 304 elementary-aged children living in the NO area attend the catchment school: 18% travel north and 12% attend school in Ladysmith. (See Graph for current attendance). Baragar does not project enrolment growth at NO. Traditionally, much of the NO population has come from St’zuminus First Nation. Currently, there are ~120 elementary students located at St’zuminus First Nation schools. There is no evidence to support a great return to NO. An English stream cannot approach an ‘optimal size’ unless 50% of the current population located at Woodbank and North Cedar Intermediate choose to enroll at NO. Most relevant for planning investment in Ladysmith schools in the community is the geographic location of the current population and predicted growth. The population of children ages 1-4 in the EDR catchment increased by 192% from 2003 to 2013, with primary-aged children (ages 5-8) increasing by 80%. Most children living in the EDR area attend their local school. Only 7% of elementary students living near EDR are attending another school in SD68 (see Appendix C, Graph F) In the LPS/LIS catchment area, there are currently 626 children (Appendix C. Graph I). In 2013/2014, 81% of LPS/LIS’s catchment population attends school in SD68 (Appendix C. Graph H). Of the 65 children destined to enter Kindergarten, only 78% (51) enrolled in SD68: 65% went to LPS and 14% entered FI at EDR. Currently, 64% of LPS/LIS catchment population attends LPS/LIS with 18% travelling to EDR for FI and English K-7. Most of the 6 LPS/LIS catchment students (116) who are missing from SD68 appear to be attending St. Joseph’s and Chemainus Elementary. Over the last 5 years, more children living in the LPS/LIS area have moved to EDR for both French and English for a current total of 110 (74 currently in FI and 36 currently in English). It is clear that the K-7 model in English and French is appealing to caregivers. In summary, 81% of students living in the two Town of Ladysmith catchment areas attend schools in Ladysmith. The majority of the missing children can be found at Chemainus Elementary and St. Joseph’s, with less than 10 at École Oceane and Queen Margaret’s. Most of the elementary children missing from the NO catchment area are located at Stz’uminus schools and are unlikely to re-enter SD68. NO is not viable without substantive enrolment from Cedar. Conclusion: Option C is the only option to provide ongoing operation of an elementary school facility in the one area in the Ladysmith Zone where the population of children is growing. 3. Financial savings from closing EDR will be negligible Cost savings in closing École Davis Road would be minimal while having a dramatic impact on every student and family served by the Ladysmith zone of schools. In closing any school a great number of administrative, supervision, clerical, supplies and custodial costs are reallocated along with the students they serve. This is especially true in Option A, closing a school with a very high utilization such as EDR, where specifically the actual operational cost per student is among the very lowest in the district. By contrast the savings in closing an underutilized building as in Option C are much greater. In terms of the actual total cost per student to run the school (Staffing, Servicing, Maintenance, Custodial, Supplies and Utilities) École Davis Road is among the lowest in School District 68, ranking at #7 among all elementary schools (See Appendix D) . In terms of minimizing the costs related to running schools during these times of tight budgets, funding clawbacks and wage increases, Option C provides the best balance of cost savings while being able to provide adequate learning alternatives for the Ladysmith community. In a comparison of facility condition as it relates to the actual $ costs of deferred maintenance (on which the FCI ratings are based), it is clear that Option C provides the lower deferred maintenance cost than Option A. At under $1.05m in deferred maintenance, EDR is the lowest actual deferred maintenance dollar amount in the Ladysmith zone. At over $1.8M, NO has the highest in the zone. EDR serves ~250 students currently. This amounts to just under $4200 per student served in deferred maintenance costs (Appendix E). By contrast, at 1.85m in deferred maintenance and 90 students, North Oyster School has a deferred maintenance cost per student of over $20,000. As NO will likely not serve over 200 students over the coming years, this still amounts to $9250 in deferred maintenance costs per student served. Even if we take an alternative worst/best case scenario through the numbers, at enrolment of 200-EDR/300-NO, the deferred maintenance costs per student are $5250-EDR/$6167-NO. High probability of students leaving School District 68 if EDR closes The relative proximity of two options for stable K-7 elementary education in adjacent District Municipality of North Cowichan (School District 79) is a threat to the participation of Ladysmith students in their catchment schools, particularly during periods of uncertainty, as we have been experiencing in recent years. A visual representation of the driving distances to schools in Chemainus can be found here: http://www.edrpac.ca/home/driving-distances. The Secretary at St. Joseph’s Independent School confirmed on January 23rd, 2014 that at least 60% of their 140 students live in Ladysmith. The majority of these students take advantage of the bussing available between Ladysmith and the school. Based on conversations with Chemainus Elementary staff, we can conservatively say that a minimum of 90 students who live in the Ladysmith catchments travel outside their home community to access stable K-7 education in Chemainus. At $8400 per student, this is a net loss of $750,000 to School District 68. Chemainus Elementary is located 8.2 km south of EDR. Several students attending EDR currently live in SD79 (14) and the southern part of the catchment (CVRD Area G): these students are predicted to migrate to Chemainus Elementary. Many families have indicated that they are considering a move to St. Joseph’s which provides not only bussing to children in Ladysmith, but before and after school care, a valuable service which is not offered at any school in the town of Ladysmith. If Option A or the Senior Staff’s Proposal are adopted, the opportunity for students to attend the preferred K-7 model of elementary school will be eliminated in the Town of Ladysmith, which we anticipate will drive even more families south to District 79. Additionally, with only one elementary school within the Town boundaries, any student who has a conflict at Ladysmith Primary/Intermediate will have no option to change schools other than leaving his/her community, which places additional stresses on already vulnerable children. The EDRI data have revealed an increasing vulnerability in Ladysmith over the last 10 years. Currently, 41% of Ladysmith children are categorized as vulnerable (well above the SD68 District Average of 35%): the greatest vulnerability is on the social behavior scale (24%). 5. Ladysmith needs a sustainable, long term vision for schools that serves the community plan In the current climate of limited facilities funding, the choices made by the Board of Education today will affect students in the Ladysmith Zone for many years. We strongly advocate that to support projected growth and current/historical participation rates, the School District should maintain an elementary school in the south end of Ladysmith. As Option C is the only option still under consideration by the Board which maintains a facility in the south end, EDRPAC supports this option, and encourages the District to adopt Option C’s vision for Phase Three, which results in a renovated and expanded K-7 Ladysmith Elementary School at the current LPS site (an annex as suggested by Trustee Brennan should be considered) and a new K-7 dual track (French Immersion/English) school at another site within the limits of the Town of Ladysmith. The K to 7 model is preferred in light of the high vulnerability of children living in Ladysmith according to the EDI (41%), is in alignment with Board policy, and has the potential to retain and encourage greater enrolment in SD68. We have ample evidence to support the pedagogical superiority of the model, and ubiquitous implementation allows for parents to move their children among schools if necessary. EDRPAC also concludes on the basis of available evidence that the high rate of loss of children from Ladysmith to other schools (20%) is related to the lack of space in a K-7 school. 9 CONCLUSIONS EDRPAC understands that there is an expected shortfall of 3 million for 2014/2015 school year. Part of this is due to the fact that fewer students enrolled than projected (700K) in 2013/2014. We have good reason to believe that if EDR is closed, many families will enroll their children in Chemainus area schools, further exacerbating the problem. We want to be part of the solution. Ladysmith needs stable educational opportunities and parents need choice within their community in order to move their children between schools as needed and to create optimal learning environments. French Immersion is the only enrichment opportunity accessible for Ladysmith children, and its success comes as a result of immense support from Ladysmith families. Given the space to grow in its current home of EDR, we believe the French Immersion program will flourish – the likelihood of increased attrition under Option A, and sub-optimal (126 students) program size under the Senior Staff’s proposal are serious threats to the program. We ask that you experiment with Option C and keep all the Ladysmith children in Ladysmith. 10
