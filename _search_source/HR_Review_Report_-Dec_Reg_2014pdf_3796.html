NANAIMO-LADYSMITH PUBLIC SCHOOLS HUMAN RESOURCES DEPARTMENT REVIEW Progress update to November 2014 In August 2011, Seconded Director, Human Resources Ron Pound published the Human Resources Review at the request of the Board of Education, to conduct a high-level review of School District 68’s human resources department structure and services. The findings outlined two sets of recommendations. The first set of recommendations prescribed hiring an Associate Superintendent of Human Resources and a Director of Labour Relations. These incumbents were appointed in January 2012. The remainder of this report will focus on the second set of recommendations involving a number of strategic initiatives for development and implementation in order to create a functional and productive human resources department. The following summarizes the progress on each recommendation made, as of November 30, 2014. Recommendation A The Associate Superintendent, Human Resources in consultation with the Board of Education, senior management, principals/vice-principals, exempt staff, and union representatives, should immediately develop a comprehensive human resources strategic plan that aligns with the newly-developed school district strategic plan. A number of strategies were developed in 2012 utilizing the HR Review recommendations as a basis to formulate goals. Many of these strategies aligned with the organizations’ strategic goals to ultimately support student learning and are discussed below. Recommendation B As part of the human resources strategic plan, and with the assistance of BCPSEA, the Associate Superintendent, Human Resources will need to review the current positions in the human resources department, developing job descriptions, criteria for replacement, and reporting relationships. Training for new hires and incumbents, including articulation of workflow plans in the department, should also be developed and implemented. An evaluation procedure will also need to be developed and implemented for exempt employees. Page 1 of 5 In 2012-13, a new HR department structure was implemented. This included reclassifying some positions, increasing skills and knowledge and rearranging the staff portfolios. It also included working in partnership with key departments for role clarification and redistribution of some tasks. The current structure and incumbents now form a high performing team. This is evidenced by the team members’ breadth of HR knowledge across all employee groups, their quick ability to respond to a variety of HR issues organization wide, while developing strategic initiatives and systems to become more progressive. An annual goal setting and accountability system was developed and implemented for senior staff. The same tool and process is being rolled out for many exempt staff within the departments. Recommendation C As part of the human resources strategic plan, develop a comprehensive training program, in consultation with various stakeholder and partner groups, in areas such as collective agreement administration/interpretation, investigations, discipline, harassment, grievance/arbitration, accommodation/return to work, and attendance management. A number of leadership and training sessions have been held for the leaders of the organization over the past three years. This included two joint sessions with the NDTA. While many of the sessions were developed and delivered by internal staff, some were led by subject matter experts. HR and Learning Services Department have now partnered to deliver professional development sessions as a part of a Leadership Series that is designed to meet the current needs of the leadership group. Recommendation D As part of the human resources strategic plan, and aligned with the district strategic plan, develop a long-term comprehensive succession plan for all positions in the school district. In partnership with Learning Services, the first phases of succession planning are established for hiring Principals and Vice-Principal positions. This involves interviewing and appointing both internal and external candidates to a pool. When vacancies arise, the candidates within the pool are assessed for appointment. Much of the Leadership Series referred to above is also designed to support potential leaders, both instructional and non-instructional, as a part of succession planning. Additional phases would include a more formal and comprehensive succession plan that involves activities such as prescribed participation in leadership development, knowledge transfer, career mapping, and cross departmental training. Page 2 of 5 Recommendation E Linked to the succession plan, develop an articulated recruitment, selection, and retention strategy. Prior to 2012, the majority of staffing for the organization was driven by HR and treated as a transactional process. This has started to shift with departments becoming more active partners and reclaiming the authority for staffing decisions. Other accomplishments include, and are not limited to: - a more extensive recruitment process for Principals and Vice-Principals a succession pool for Principals and Vice-Principals a regulated process for hiring TTOCs (driven by Learning Services) partnership with VIU in shortlisting the top teacher graduates a rigorous reference-checking process requiring pre-training revised interview questions and protocols to incorporate strategic goals implementation of a Staff Requisition Form requiring department, senior management and Budget Manager approvals for position control. implementation of pre-testing for some internal positions analysis of replacement positions when vacancies occur negotiation of an LOU with the NDTA to explore Aboriginal recruitment and retention strategies implementation of the electronic posting and application process As a result, recruitment is treated more strategically, and the standards of recruitment have been raised. Recommendation F Conduct a system-wide review of the attendance patterns of all employees. Representatives of the district’s employee groups should be invited to participate. Develop a comprehensive program of attendance management that is transparent to all employees. Through restructuring and securing the appropriate skill levels in HR, a formal ‘medical monitoring’ system that operates under the principles of consistency and fairness is in place. Of importance is the leadership training on attendance, safety and wellness and subsequent implementation of a Conversational Approach Attendance Management Program. In addition, significant work has been accomplished in providing early return to work schedules and a system for providing accommodations to enable employees to return to the work site as soon as appropriate. Page 3 of 5 Recommendation G While the occupational health and safety department is functioning well, best practice indicates that this department should be integrated into the human resources department given that many occupational health and safety issues have labour relations/human resources implications. The Occupational Safety and Wellness department works closely with the human resources practitioners. This department also experienced some restructuring when ‘wellness’ was incorporated into that portfolio. A review of the Safety Department was conducted by Cascade Consulting in 2013 and resulted in a number of recommendations that are either implemented or in progress. The last year, in particular, has seen the introduction of new progressive safety initiatives such as preventative maintenance strategies for slips, trips and falls and investigations to determine preventative future measures. An important initiative in progress is the implementation of Certificate of Recognition (COR) which is WorkSafeBC's voluntary Partners in Injury and Disability Prevention Program and offers incentives to employers who create and adhere to health and safety management systems aimed at improving workplace safety.
