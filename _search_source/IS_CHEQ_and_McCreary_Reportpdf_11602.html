NANAIMO LADYSMITH PUBLIC SCHOOLS EDUCATION COMMITTEE PUBLIC MEETING INFORMATION SHEET DATE: TO: FROM: SUBJECT: February 7, 2024 Education Committee Don Balcombe, Assistant Superintendent The Childhood Experience Questionnaire and the McCreary Society Results from the 2023 BC Adolescent Health Survey Board Goal Continuous improvement in students with complex and unique abilities, Indigenous and overall student population on student achievement. Overview Our District gathers information about our learners from a variety of sources. From classroom observations to parent-teacher conferences to formal assessments, to one-on-one conversations between teachers and students, we are regularly gathering information about students that helps us better understand them as learners and determine strategies to support their success. Some of the information comes back to us from the Ministry of Education and Child Care in the form of Foundational Skills Assessment reports, Literacy and Numeracy Assessment reports, Graduation rates, and Student Satisfaction Survey results. Other data is collected at the classroom or school level, helping us report individual student progress to parents in the form of report cards. The District also participates in external opportunities to gather information about our learners. Two such opportunities include the Childhood Experience Questionnaire (CHEQ) and the McCreary Society Adolescent Health Survey. Background Childhood Experience Questionnaire (CHEQ) Our District participated in the Childhood Experience Questionnaire (CHEQ) for the first time this fall. Kindergarten parents/caregivers were asked to complete an online CHEQ to gather information on children’s experiences in their early environments prior to Kindergarten. We had 639 parents and caregivers complete the survey, providing a very large sample representation of our learners entering kindergarten. The questionnaire focuses on experiences in five key developmental areas that are strongly linked to children’s health and well-being, education and social outcomes. Research shows that there is great variability in children’s development by age 5 which present both protective and risk factors that influence developmental outcomes. The information collected is both comprehensive and actionable. 2 The five key developmental areas and experiences that the CHEQ report addresses are: • • • • • Physical Health and Well-Being Language and Cognition Social and Emotional Experiences Early Learning and Care Community and Context The McCreary Centre Society BC Adolescent Health Survey The McCreary Centre Society BC Adolescent Health Survey is offered once every five years to school districts throughout the province. The survey covers a wide array of topics that make up student health, including: • • • • • • • • • • • Home life Material well-being School experiences Physical and mental health well-being Mental health and well-being Eating behaviours and body image Substance use Sexual health Safety, violence, harassment and abuse Relationships and connections Sports, gambling, and other leisure activities The report ends with topics that students would like to learn more about, such as: sexual health, mental health, and physical health. The CHEQ and the McCreary Report share many common data themes. For the purposes of this Information Sheet, three of these common data themes will be highlighted here: screen time as part of social and emotional experiences, mental health of caregivers and adolescents, and access to supports. Data Themes: Screen time as part of social and emotional experiences The CHEQ asked parents and caregivers about the use of devices. 23% of children used an electronic device more than 2 hours a day. A further 39% used a device for 1 to 2 hours a day. The McCreary report indicated that 97% of youth have a phone or tablet. 37% of students felt their parents never/rarely knew what they were doing online. 67% of students reported scrolling through social media after they were expected to be asleep. 3 Mental health of caregivers and adolescents CHEQ data indicated that 22% of parents have 5 or more adults in their neighbourhood that they can count on. Parent and caregiver stress was rated as medium by 45%, high by 29%, and very high by 9%. McCreary data shows that 28% of students reported having a mental health condition, including challenges of anxiety disorder (29%), and depression (18%). Reasons students did not access mental health services in the past month (among those who felt they needed these services), 65% did not want their parent/guardian to know, 46% did not know where to go, 39% worried their information would not be kept confidential, and 37% were afraid of what they would be told. Access to supports While the vast majority of children visited a health care professional within the past 12 months, there were barriers to access identified in the CHEQ report. Barriers identified by parents and caregivers included appointment availability/waiting list (72%), hours the health care professional was available (16%), no access or loss of access to health care professional (14%), cost (9%) and distance from home or work (9%). Youth accessed health care through a family doctor (46%), walk-in clinic (23%), specialist (31%), or emergency room (17%). Many students did not need health care (24%). Among those students who needed medical help, 22% had not received medical treatment. Both the CHEQ and the McCreary Report provide invaluable information as we strengthen support for our students and mitigate the impacts that COVID-19 has had on our students, our families, and our community. Identifying existing effective supports, as well as gaps in service or emerging needs for students and families will lead to greater success and healthier students. Existing programs and strategies, as well as emerging opportunities to further support students, will be discussed during the Education Committee presentation. The full Childhood Experience Questionnaire (CHEQ) is included in the Education Committee package, as are the 2018 and 2023 McCreary BC Results for Adolescent Health Survey.
