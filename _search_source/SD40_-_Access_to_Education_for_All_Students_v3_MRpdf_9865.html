(e.g. Ministry of Education, Ministry of Finance, etc.) The BCSTA develop a template to help Boards of Education remove barriers to registration for students with precarious or no immigration status in their Districts; and that the BCSTA calls on the BC Ministry of Education to remove barriers for students with precarious or no immigration status. Rationale Provide a succinct description of why this motion is needed, plus any relevant background information. Under international human rights agreements, states have agreed to ensure access to education for every child without exception. The Universal Declaration on the Rights of the Child, which Canada signed in 1990, recognizes the right of a child to education, and protection against all forms of discrimination or punishment based on the status, activities, expressed opinions, or beliefs of the child’s parents, legal guardians, or family members. The BC School Act clearly states that any school-age child who lives in a community is eligible for public education and requires the Ministry to provide funding for every student. The late Anita Hagen, Minister of Education from 1991 to 1993, spoke in the Legislative Assembly about the importance of ensuring students not be denied registration because of the immigration status of their parents. In 1989, our association participated in a review of the School Act. Our written submission states: “all children resident in a district should be eligible to attend public school. School boards should not be asked to police the Immigration Act.” Several explanations are offered around reluctance or refusal to register students including a misplaced concern about federal immigration laws; concern that the District will not receive funding for the student or will have difficulties when audited; and concern that opening the doors to sanctuary students will somehow mean they are unable to charge international student fees which have become a significant portion of some District’s revenues. None of these objections are valid when investigated and should not continue to present barriers. Currently barriers include references to citizenship or immigration from registration information, checklists, or forms, which are not necessary to confirm residency. It is important to expand which documents acceptable to demonstrate that a student and guardian meet the requirements of the School Act as living in the community, or “ordinarily resident”. It is critical that schools do not exceed their jurisdiction by providing student or family information to immigration enforcement agencies, except where expressly required to do so by law. It is also important to communicate clearly to the community that the District is committed to ensuring all students can attend school regardless of immigration status. Training for school staff about sanctuary schools and the importance of maintaining student and family privacy at all times would also need to reviewed.
