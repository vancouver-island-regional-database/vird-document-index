There have been a number of notable improvements in Nanaimo Ladysmith Public Schools’ achievement data that deserve acknowledgement and recognition. Overall, the Six Year Completion Rates have shown an increase in the number of students graduating from our school system. This upward trend in improved student achievement is indicative of the high expectations that we have set for our district related to the Board of Education’s vision of Success for All. Currently more than 15% of our student population is of Aboriginal ancestry and 10% of our population includes students with special needs. Superintendent’s Annual Report on Student Achievement (SD68) Response to Intervention (RTI) Framework Nanaimo-Ladysmith Public Schools values the passionate commitment to student learning that is demonstrated by our staff on a daily basis. We also realize that as our students’ learning needs become more complex, we need a structure that unites us in our efforts, facilitates our ability to share innovative, effective practices, and increases our capacity to collectively support students’ learning. Response to Intervention is a framework that focuses on collaborative problem solving to improve all students’ learning and to close achievement gaps for struggling learners. It combines extensive effective schools research that identifies teacher collaboration, sharing of effective instructional practices, and problem solving as critical factors for improving student learning. While many initiatives in education have focused on implementing new “programs,” RTI is a framework that empowers educators to align essential learning outcomes, use reflective inquiry to identify the most effective instruction practices, develop a culture of collaboration, and provide proactive interventions to prevent students from falling behind, academically and behaviourally. RTI supports the belief that all students can learn at optimal levels. Terms Belonging to the RTI Framework • Tier 1: We refer to this as Universal Practices. It refers to the high quality instruction provided to all students. Examples of instructional strategies being utilized by our teachers to meet student needs include balanced literacy, real-world learning (inquiry/project-based, trades, apprenticeships), Aboriginal Understandings, Assessment “for,” “as,” and “of” Learning, and Universal Design for Learning (UDL). The district will continue to promote these instructional practices through professional learning experiences and collaboration. • Tier 2: We refer to this as Targeted Support. Despite our best efforts, we will have students who come to us without prerequisite skills or need more time and support to master essential learning outcomes, academically, socially, and behaviourally. Often, Tier 2 intervention (supplemental instruction) is provided in the classroom and possibly by the classroom teacher in small groups. Tier 2 interventions target specific student needs. We monitor students’ response to Tier 2 intervention (approximately every 3-4 weeks as appropriate) to see if the intervention is working or if it needs to be adjusted. It is critical that interventions be connected to Tier 1 classroom practices, and are identified through collaboration with the classroom teacher. • Tier 3: We refer to this as Intensive Intervention. There will be a very small group of students (generally less than 5%) who need intensive support to master essential learning outcomes, academically, socially, and behaviourally. These students may be significantly below grade expectations in basic skills and/or self-regulation. Due to the intensive nature of Tier 3, we may monitor students’ progress every one or two weeks as appropriate, and adjust interventions as necessary to meet the unique needs of the student. Time to Collaborate RTI requires collaboration between all classroom teachers, administration and support staff. In RTI, this collaboration requires everyone to adopt new ways of thinking, collaborating, and working together as a team to support all learners. 5 Superintendent’s Annual Report on Student Achievement (SD68) Additionally, it may mean that educators need to assume new identities that are not based on their role descriptions or funding source, but rather how their professional expertise can best help students meet the learning outcomes. The district is now exploring ways to embed collaboration time within the work day. It is hoped that a pilot for embedded collaboration time will be in place in February 2014 and run for the remainder of the school year. It is also hoped that September 2014 will mark the beginning of a new culture of staff collaboration to provide a school-wide focus on student performance while also providing time for embedded professional development. The RTI framework requires that basic structures and procedures be established in all district schools and not simply left to the willing. Every one of our learners, in every single classroom, deserves the opportunity to learn in innovative, collaborative environments. Self-Regulated Learning Self-regulation is the ability for a learner to perform at an optimal level physically, emotionally, behaviourally, socially and academically. In the school environment, these areas of self-regulation are addressed in order to provide the foundation for optimal academic learning. While addressing each area of self-regulation is critical to optimal learning, it is also imperative that assessment and intervention of these areas be viewed through a tiered Response to Intervention lens. At the school and classroom level, both assessment and intervention begins at the Tier One universal level. Tier Two and Tier Three supports follow, based on the progress within Tier One. District-Wide Assessment Nanaimo-Ladysmith Public Schools recognizes that rich, varied assessment processes are crucial to student learning. As a district, it is our collective responsibility to ensure that all students learn at optimal levels. As our district explores the most effective options for the identification of crucial data sets at the district, school, classroom and individual levels; professional dialogue will be key. Formative assessment serves as the foundation of our RTI model monitoring because it enables educators to carefully measure a student’s success in mastering the BC curriculum. Knowing whether a child is on track towards mastering essential grade level outcomes is a key attribute of RTI. This proactive approach empowers educators to develop effective, personalized, instructional, enrichment, and remedial interventions. Fall 2013 marked the implementation of our formative assessment pilot in which five assessment instruments were piloted K-9 district wide. Staff surveys were completed and the data collected to inform recommendations from a district assessment committee. This work will culminate in the selection of one assessment instrument for each of primary, intermediate, secondary and French Immersion. This work will be completed by spring 2014. Early Years to Grade 3 (E23) Reading Initiative Nanaimo Ladysmith Public Schools has created a district-wide literacy initiative focused on improving the reading results of our primary students. In the 2012-13 school-year, Nanaimo Ladysmith Public 8 Superintendent’s Annual Report on Student Achievement (SD68) Schools participated in the provincial initiative Changing Results for Young Readers (CR4YR). As a result, we recognized that our efforts had to become more systemic across our school district. Therefore, we have focussed our efforts to improve the reading levels for students in the primary years through targeted professional learning for teachers. Our goal is to ensure that all students will be reading at grade level as they enter Grade 4. The vision of Early Years to Grade 3 (E23) targets two major areas: Consistent use of evidence-based practices and building a collaborative culture. The Canadian Foundation of Literacy prescribes five key elements of reading, all of which are areas we plan to focus our instruction within a 21st century learning ‘Balanced Literacy’ environment . Nanaimo Ladysmith has also explicitly defined ‘balanced literacy’ to include speaking/listening, reading/viewing, and writing/representing within a conceptual frame of assessment, planning, and instruction. This cohesiveness will enable us to promote a shared understanding of balanced literacy that closely aligns to the instructional targets of the five elements of reading. In Year One, 2013-14, our projected plan of improvement involves Early Years’ educators, Kindergarten, Grades 1, 2 and 3 teachers across the district. All educators who teach at these grade levels will receive professional learning that includes contemporary research and pedagogical instruction within a collaborative inquiry process. Our instructional model is designed to build on the capacity of exemplary evidence-based practices and promote the use of consistent formative assessment practices throughout the District.
