The Superintendent’s Annual Report on Student Achievement School District 68 (Nanaimo-Ladysmith) DRAFT Submitted by Michael J. Munro Superintendent/CEO Submitted to The School District 68 Board of Education and The British Columbia Ministry of Education December 15, 2010 BACKGROUND As outlined by the Ministry of Education, Section 22 of the School Act states the following: “A board must appoint a superintendent of schools for a school district who, under the general direction of the board…, (b.1) must, on or before December 15 of a school year, prepare and submit to the board a report on student achievement in that district for the previous year.” Section 79.3 goes on to say: “On receipt of a report submitted by a superintendent of schools under section 23(1) (b.1), the board must, on approval of the report, (a) immediately, and in any event no later than January 31 of the school year in which the board receives the report, submit that report to the minister, and (b) as soon as practicable, make the report available to the public.” The report is intended to serve two main purposes:   Ensure transparency and accountability for each school district in terms of its responsibility for improving student achievement; Provide information that will facilitate subsequent planning for continuing improvement of student achievement at the school and school district levels as school plans and district achievement contracts are developed. This report focuses on student achievement results in the school district and includes information found in the Achievement Contract for 2010 – 2011, our Aboriginal Education Enhancement Agreement for 2006 – 2011, the District Literacy Plan and Early Learning. The guidelines for this report require districts to show evidence of both improving and challenging areas of student achievement, specifically in the areas which show the achievement of all students as well as Aboriginal students in:    six-year completion rates English 10 average exam and course marks Grade 4 FSA reading – meeting and exceeding expectations In addition to the Ministry required areas for reporting, we have chosen to include the results for special needs children. The Ministry of Education also requires that districts report on three specific questions regarding “Recommendation #5” of the report from the Representative of Children and Youth on the subject of Children in Care. 1 Superintendent’s Annual Report on Achievement AREAS OF ONGOING STRENGTH / IMPROVING AREAS OF STUDENT ACHIEVEMENT 1. English 10 – Required Provincial Exam Pass Rates Our 2009 - 2010 student achievement results for English 10 demonstrate slight improvement over the past five years. This slight trend is in course marks, exam marks and blended final marks for all District 68 students. Over the five year period from 2005 - 2006 through 2009 2010, SD68 students increased their English 10 final blended mark by 2% from 95% to 97%. In 2009 – 2010, the performance level of 167 of our 922 grade four students was unknown (18% for SD68 compared to 16% for the Province), 28% of our Aboriginal students’ performance was unknown and 60% of our special needs students’ performance was unknown. That means that either the students did not write the assessment or they did not provide enough information for meaningful results. We are also concerned about the politicization of the Foundation Skills Assessment as it points us away from our goals rather than toward them. We need to find a more meaningful way to measure our elementary students’ achievement to allow the district to effectively monitor system performance. 3. Vulnerable students The district’s Early Developmental Indicators (EDI) indicators continue to demonstrate that a significant number of our students entering kindergarten come from families that can be deemed vulnerable in one of more categories of the assessment. In Wave One, 31.9% of children were vulnerable on at least one domain of their development. On Wave Two, this decreased by 3.2% to 29.5 and Wave Three (2008-2009) it decreased to 27.4%. This past year, Wave Four, 31.6% of children were vulnerable on at least one domain of their development. Although these differences may point to cohort variability, this data continues to represent a trend of over one-quarter of the children in the Nanaimo population vulnerable upon entering kindergarten. We opened three new StrongStart Centres in September of 2009, which brings our total of StrongStart Centres to eight. The District Community Literacy Plan now guides our work in community development activities around literacy. The early learning community partnership work continues to guide early learning activities for families from Lantzville to Ladysmith and there is a growing understanding about the power of all partner groups to work together to support families and thus children and their learning. Following our review of LINK funded programs; we have adjusted resource allocation processes to better meet the needs of our vulnerable students in all district schools. 4. What categories of Children in Care have been successfully identified and are being monitored? All of our children in care have been successfully identified and we are monitoring them all. The categories are as follows:  Students who are Aboriginal  Students who have special needs  Students who are in primary grades (K-3); Students who are in intermediate grades (47); and Students who are in secondary school grades (8-12)  Students who are in learning alternatives (specialized programs outside the regular school) What structures are in place to provide effective communications among MCFD offices, social workers, foster parents and schools? SD68 and the local MCFD office have detailed structures in place to provide effective communication for schools and for social workers. As part of these structures, detailed responsibilities have been delineated. Examples from these documents follow: MCFD/Social Workers are responsible for:  When a child comes into the care or a child in care moves schools, the MCFD social worker will inform the principal (or designate) at the school of the child/youth’s circumstance and any information about the child/youth’s individual needs. For a child/youth who is brought into care for the first time, the social worker will be part of developing a short-term plan to ensure a smooth transition. e.g., identifying a mentor to help the child/youth adjust to new living situation, counselling, peer support, and other services).  6 In the case where a child in care changes schools:  The MCFD social worker will work collaboratively with the sending school to support a successful school transition.  The MCFD Social worker will provide and sign any necessary release forms for schools to share information to enhance planning for the child/youth. The school Principal/Vice-Principal is responsible for:    Receiving the necessary information about lines of communication and names of a child or youth in care. Consulting with caregiver(s)/child welfare worker(s) about the educational needs and program of the child or youth (e.g. appropriate program and placement). With the child welfare worker(s), caregiver(s) and or parent(s), developing and implementing a plan for continuity of services in order to support the child or youth in:  Developing or retaining a personal sense of belonging, security and acceptance (e.g. connection to Aboriginal Education programs, services or other cultural opportunities)  Meeting the expectations of their educational program, and Achieving his or her learning outcomes  When necessary, arranging for timely transfer of school records, files and information. What evidence and data is being collected to determine if monitoring and structures are having an impact? We are in the early stages of this important work. However, we have mentors in place for each child in care in the school district. Principals know who their “children in care” are. We have created three subcommittees from our main Child in Care (CIC) Project Group. 1) A subcommittee whose main responsibility is on identifying the data that needs to be collected; create the baseline data, collect data at various intervals; and pay close attention to this data during key transition periods. 2) A subcommittee whose main responsibility is on CIC Project Evaluation. This sub-committee will survey staff, students and MCFD social workers to ensure that the project is being successful. 3) A subcommittee whose main responsibility is to clearly define the "Role of the Mentor". We have our CIC meetings scheduled for this school year and we will be furthering our work on this project in early January 2011. Respectfully submitted, Michael J.
