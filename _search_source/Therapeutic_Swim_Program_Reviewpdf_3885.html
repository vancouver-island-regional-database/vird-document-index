Therapeutic Swim Program Review August 20, 2014 Background • Originally conceived as a therapeutic opportunity for students with physical disabilities ~1990s • District rented Beban Park Pool for one hour per week • water exercises and safe lifting/transfer training provided NLPS contracted Physiotherapists • EAs worked 1:1 with the students in the water • EAs transported students in their own vehicles • When the CUPE Collective Agreement mandated that EAs could no longer transport students, bussing was provided and costs were absorbed by Transportation • Almost empty busses and low numbers in the pool allowed expansion of the program to include students with other special needs • Some Secondary Skills For Life Classes attended • 1:1 ratio student/EA began to change • Sometimes one EA brought up to 6 students • Overcrowded change rooms lead to a limit of 75 students participating • SFL classes opted to attend public swim times which better suited their ages and timetables • Student Support Services used swim budget to subsidize secondary SFL swim sessions • Some EAs did not go into the pool or supervise students during the hour—sat on deck benches, went to the pool coffee shop, socialized with EAs from other schools • Some EAs used the time for personal swim exercise or relaxing in the hot tub/sauna • Parks and Recreation Department complained to the District concerning repeated reports from frustrated Lifeguards about a lack of direct supervision of students and EA disregard for Lifeguard attempts at enforcing pool rules • Physiotherapists frustrated with the ‘free swim’ nature of the hour since the high activity in the pool interfered with a calm therapeutic experience • Some EAs expressed concern about their colleagues not engaging with students 2008-2009 • Student Support Services introduced a number of changes: o the term “Therapeutic” was added to the title o Schools required to include IEP goals for time at pool o Introduced application which included embedded pool goals o A special education teacher was hired to supervise at the pool during swim times and confer with the EAs and the school-based School Support Teachers o EAs required to wear a bathing suit, go into the water, and stay within arm’s length of student (s) o The first 20 minutes designated as guided physical activity led by the teacher 2009-2013 • Principals and HR report difficulty in recruiting EAs to participate • HR clarified that swimming is part of the job description for all EA 2s • Overall number of EAs in elementary schools decreased (fewer low incidence students) • Ability to release an EA for an entire morning challenging because EA staffing is tightly orchestrated to serve as many classrooms as possible M Jane Carroll Department of Learning Services Current Program Teacher Role EA Role • Registered participants has dwindled to about 45 students • Actual student attendance has declined and fluctuates between 25-30 students per session • Principals report that there are other students that would benefit from participation but the school is unable to release EAs from their classroom responsibilities • HR and Principals continue to receive refusals to participate from EAs • Friday EA absences are high—HR has identified a few attendance issues to follow-up on • Friday mornings 9:30 to 10:30 am • Beban Park Pool • October to April • Approximately 20 sessions per school year • Therapeutic Swim teacher position has been incorporated into Integration Support Coordinator (ISC) role • EAs working with Low Incidence students who have Physiotherapists assigned through our School-Age Therapy Program, receive guidance and training to provide therapy in the pool • Provide schools with Therapeutic Swim Program Application (attached). This form requires goals to be identified and outlines responsibilities of the SST and EA: • Provide guidance and support to EAs—including sending a welcome letter (sample attached) and regularly reviewing expectations with EAs at the pool • Work with SSTs to ensure each participant has appropriate goals for therapeutic swim time • Lead the active portion of the pool time—first twenty minutes • Work with EAs to ensure that goals are addressed during pool time • Refine and update goals with the EA and SST as necessary • Wear a swimsuit, circulate amongst participants and get into the water to model appropriate interactions with students as necessary • Work with pool staff to ensure safety and student/staff compliance with pool rules • Assist with lifting and transfers as necessary • Report progress to SSTs for inclusion on IEPs • Review program annually to refine and improve the therapeutic opportunities for students • Wear a bathing suit • Stay within arm’s length of student • Participate in the 20 minute active part of the session to guide/model for students • Actively facilitate student’s program in the water for the remainder of the session • Confer with Therapeutic Swim Teacher to discuss progress and refine goals M Jane Carroll Department of Learning Services • Depending on student needs may take direction from a Physiotherapist • Assist with lifting and transfers as necessary Funding • The Board allocates $7,000 annually • In July 2014 the Board of Education signed a Memorandum of Understanding with the City of Nanaimo eliminating the cost of pool rental for the Therapeutic Swim Program Budgeting • As a result of the MOU, the following costs are negated in the 2014-2015 school year: o Pool rental of $4,417.82 for the year o contribution to each SFL class for public swim sessions—approximately $1500 • Learning Services will have these costs for the 2014-2015 school year: o Bussing—estimated at $7688 (last year’s bussing costs) o EA bathing suit subsidy of $50 per swim suit as requested—approximately $500/year o Pool toys and equipment—approximately $200/year Operational • Principals and HR continue to report staff reluctant and at times refusing to participate in the Challenges swim program • Declining registration—45 registered out of a possible 75 spots. Four years ago there were 75 participants and a waitlist. • Poor attendance (of the 45 registrants, actual attendance is usually 25 to 30) • EA absences on Fridays—Cathy Brzoza in HR is working with Principals to address attendance patterns for a number of EAs. • Consistency at the pool has been challenging because the Therapeutic Swim Teacher role has been filled by four different people in five years.
