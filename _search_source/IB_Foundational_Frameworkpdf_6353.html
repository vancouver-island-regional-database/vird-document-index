NLPS International Baccalaureate (IB) Primary Years Programme (PYP) The International Baccalaureate: The International Baccalaureate (IB) was founded in 1968 as a non-profit organization which currently works with over 3,500 schools in 144 countries to develop and offer challenging programmes to over one million students worldwide. “The IB aspires to help schools develop well-rounded students with character who respond to challenges with optimism and an open mind, are confident in their own identities, make ethical decisions, join with others in celebrating our common humanity and are prepared to apply what they learn in real-world, complex and unpredictable situations. The IB offers high-quality programmes of international education that share a powerful vision. Informed by the values described in the learner profile, an IB education:  Focuses on learners - the IB’s student-centred programmes promote healthy relationships, ethical responsibility and personal challenge.  Develops effective approaches to teaching and learning – IB programmes help students to develop the attitudes and skills they need for both academic and personal success.  Works within global contexts - IB programmes increase understanding of languages and cultures, and explore globally significant ideas and issues.  Explores significant content - IB programmes offer a curriculum that is broad and balanced, conceptual and connected. The aim of all IB programmes is to develop internationally minded people who, recognizing their common humanity and shared guardianship of the planet, help to create a better and more peaceful world. IB learners strive to become:  inquirers  open-minded  thinkers  principled  communicators  caring  risk-takers  reflective  knowledgeable  balanced These attributes represent a broad range of human capacities and responsibilities that go beyond intellectual development and academic success.” (Source: Making the PYP Happen) What is the Primary Years Programme? The Primary Years Programme (PYP) is the first of four programmes designed for students aged 5 to 12. As a curriculum framework, it focusses on the development of the whole child as an inquirer, both in the classroom and in the world outside. Knowledge in the curriculum is defined by six transdisciplinary themes of global significance. These themes incorporate six subject areas (language, mathematics, science, social studies, arts, and personal, social and physical education) that are explored through an inquiry-based pedagogy. To engage and challenge students’ with each transdisciplinary theme, concepts are explored to promote an in-depth understanding of each theme within a local and global context. The use of each conceptual lens requires the development of various approaches to learning such as thinking, social, communication, self-management and research skills. These elements contribute towards fostering positive student attitudes towards people, their environment and most importantly learning. The building of knowledge, concepts, skills, and attitudes result is an explicit expectation of the PYP which is that successful inquiry leads to responsible action, initiated by the student as a result of the learning process. “What then is a PYP school? It is a school that, regardless of location, size or composition, strives towards developing an internationally minded person” (MTPYPH, p.3). What qualities of our current NLPS program does the PYP enhance? NLPS offers excellent teaching and learning across the district. The PYP however, brings together some common qualities that concentrate these evidence based practices into one distinct framework. The aim of the IB is to offer pedagogy that models best practices, yet remains flexible enough to fit with most school systems. Student programming would look rather different from our existing model. First, the curriculum is more clearly articulated to parents and students; it is clearly understood by all staff and revolves around five essential elements. These elements (also listed above) are knowledge, concepts, approaches to learning, and attitudes and action. These essential elements all fall under the umbrella of the IB Learner Profile which ties together the definition of what makes an internationally minded person. These attributes represent a broad range of human capacities and responsibilities that go beyond intellectual development and academic success. Despite the programming differences, the BC Curriculum coordinates well with the Units of Inquiry. Furthermore, IB requires a standard of collaborative planning across all grades. This promotes a strong understanding of the student programme by all staff and a system wide understanding of the importance of assessment and evaluation that must be articulated in a school assessment policy as a requirement of the IB. One major difference in student programming is in the final year of the PYP programme when students complete the PYP Exhibition. This showcase of learning serves as the ultimate culminating task in which students conduct their own inquiry and present their learning of not only their inquiries but their understanding of the PYP to the school community. Other qualities of the PYP curriculum are:         Global perspectives Globally relevant content Inquiry-based learning Concept-based learning Focus on development of responsible citizenship Collaborative approach to planning of instruction Development of “International-Mindedness” through the IB Learner Profile A global architecture for professional development which includes online, regional and global workshop models How will it benefit NLPS? The immediate benefit of implementing the PYP will be in enrollment. Statistics have shown that Independent School enrollment has climbed steadily by approximately 5% per year while public school enrollment across most of B.C. is in decline. In addition, offering such a programme will create other options for parents and provide some relief for the rapidly growing demands for French immersion programs. Thirdly, the IB is a highly recognized name worldwide by both parents and universities. Having a PYP school in Nanaimo-Ladysmith Public Schools promotes a global standard which is highly sought after by international families. Other benefits to the programme are that its framework, standards and practices all support the district’s strategic goals of ‘meeting each students’ unique needs,’ and ‘the continuous improvement of instruction and assessment’ in the following ways:      A horizontally and vertically integrated programme of inquiry allows for targeted intervention for all students. Units of Inquiry planners use backward design which allow for meaningful, focused teaching and learning, differentiated instruction and carefully planned assessment The PYPs thrust for inquiry, assessment, and indigenous ways of knowing connect with NLPS Tier 1 Universal Strategies The 5 essential elements correlate nicely with the new BC Curriculum’s focus on big ideas and core competencies Connection to Metacognition: “In the PYP, a positive attitude to learning is fostered by encouraging students to ask challenging questions, to critically reflect, to develop research skills, to learn how to learn, and to participate in community service.” “The IB PYP transdisciplinary inquiry-based approach to curriculum supports children’s learning through their natural curiosity, provides opportunities for students to make meaningful connections between prior learning and new learning, across curricular areas, and to their own lives and growing understanding of the world.” There are currently 40 schools in British Columbia offering at least one IB programme. On Vancouver Island, only Aspengrove and Glenlyon Norfolk School (Victoria) offer the PYP. Where could it be located? In the board approved Enhanced Facilities for Learning letter (June 27, 2013) the board passed motions to initiate the Primary International Baccalaureate program (IB) at Randerson Ridge; however after reflection, a more comprehensive school selection criteria may be necessary. Looking forward, the selection process must look for suitable sites for the growth of the PYP throughout Nanaimo-Ladysmith taking into account community, facility and suitability. When selecting a potential school site the Department of Learning Services will work with the Facilities team to create a list of 2-4 schools for possible PYP implementation. How will we consult with all of our partners? Learning services will work collaboratively with DPAC, CUPE and NDTA, NSAA and parents as well as other community partners. The IB and NLPS understand the importance of school and community connectedness. To further the development of, and to provide an avenue for dialogue and community communication, information sessions will be provided for parents across the district to understand the programme and its possible benefits for students. How much will it cost? The costs related to the programme are two-fold. First are fees that are paid directly to the IB on a yearly basis. These fees are paid each year depending on the status of the school (consideration, candidate or authorized).      Application fee of approximately $4,500 due by April 1st. This is to identify our intent as the school district to implement the PYP into a school in our district. As a ‘Candidate School’ the cost is approximately $10,000 each September 1st. This fee includes 20 hours of remote services and a two-day onsite visit from an IB consultant who helps monitor the schools development of the programme. Once awarded “authorized” status, schools pay a yearly fee of approximately $8000 per year. The details of these fees can be found on the IB website: www.ibo.org . The secondary costs of the programme come from staffing, resourcing FTE and facilities, and professional accreditation.     A PYP Coordinator is required. (recommended 0.5 FTE) To support a language or acquisition an additional 0.5 FTE is recommended. Staff Training would be required based on IB standards Facility enhancements as required by IB Program. Further consideration might need to be given towards how the district may offset the costs. It is apparent that there will be many things to overcome (union consultation, costs, training, community fit), however the merits and value of the IB programme (student engagement, collaboration, academic rigor, collective responsibility) all meet the NLPS focus on the Response to Intervention framework. Additionally, the fact that the IB (PYP) vision for learning reflects the NLPS focus on collaboration, collective responsibility, and inquiry demonstrates the alignment between IB (PYP) and our district learning goals.
