The districtŌĆÖs strategic plan was developed over the course of about six months, starting in the spring of 2012. Enhanced Facilities to Meet the Goal of Improving Student Learning How do enhanced facilities contribute to improved student learning? Optimal school size is linked to the equitable delivery of learning programs and services. The purpose of the districtŌĆÖs optimal school size formula is to ensure that there are sufficient staff and programs available to students, in keeping with the district goals. Optimal school size is defined as follows: ’éĘ ’éĘ Elementary: 200 to 400 students Secondary: 600 to 1,200 students Schools operating within the optimal school size models: ’éĘ ’éĘ ’éĘ ’éĘ ’éĘ ’éĘ ’éĘ ’éĘ ’éĘ Help improve student learning through more flexible instructional arrangements; Enhance school safety with increased staff (adults on site); Better support individualized student programming with equitable access to professional support staff; Enable more schools to have specialist teachers; Allow flexibility in scheduling to better enable personalized learning; Provide more equitable curricular and extra-curricular programs; Enhance student opportunities to participate in 21st Century learning environments and related instructional practices; Enhance staff collaboration in support of improved student learning; Support and enhance teacher opportunities to understand and influence their instructional practices with 21st Century learning methodologies. (Adapted from Thinking Ahead: Renewing Regina Public Schools, November 2007) 3 Dr. Doug Player, an experienced and respected educator, worked as a consultant to assist the senior team with the definition of the key elements of the plan. A Living Plan that Will Change over Time The 10-Year Enhanced Facilities Plan will change over time, as new circumstances develop. This is the first annual update of the plan, completed in June 2014. Objectives of the 10-Year Enhanced Facilities for Learning Plan The primary purpose of this plan is to enhance facilities to support improved learning opportunities for all students. This will be achieved by creating schools of optimal size to ensure equitable delivery of programs and services; by strengthening the systemŌĆÖs capacity to offer a variety of learning options for students; and by providing modern, up-to-date facilities. While not the primary purpose, the plan will also consolidate costly positions so funding can go to the classroom; save maintenance funds; and improve the long-term fiscal position of the Board. This plan also outlines the steps the district will take to offer a variety of learning options, and to strengthen the academic expectations. Further, by the end of the ten-year period outlined in this plan, most of the schools in the district will be within the optimal school size. In addition, there will be a number of new and renovated facilities, resulting in a significant improvement in the condition of the districtŌĆÖs school facilities overall. 4 Summary of the 10 Year Enhanced Facilities for Learning Plan Public Consultation Process Phase 1: An online public engagement process ŌĆō April 26 to June 10, 2013 An online public engagement process using ThoughtStream was set up at each school. ’éĘ The following questions were asked: - What do you see as the challenges of the proposed plan? - What do you see as the benefits of the proposed plan? - What are some things you wish students at your school could have that they donŌĆÖt currently have? - Do you have any other thoughts you wish to add? ’éĘ Parents, staff, and students at each school could respond ’éĘ Each schoolŌĆÖs process was separate There was also a process for the general public to self-register on ThoughtStream ’éĘ Timeline: - Collect thoughts April 26 to May 5 - Converge thoughts May 6 to 24 - Send back out to participants for choose May 24 to June 2 - Results available by June 11 - Results reported by schools, by community and district overall Ongoing: opportunity to write to the Board At any time during the process, individuals and groups could submit written input via email, fax or regular mail. Enhanced Facilities for Learning Proposed 10-Year Planning Considerations Nanaimo Ladysmith Public SchoolŌĆÖs Enhanced Facilities for Learning (EFL) Proposed 10-Year Plan encompasses a long-term incremental approach that is linked to a strategic plan centred on the improvement of student learning. The Proposed 10-Year EFL Plan does not stand alone as a development priority, but it is one component of a comprehensive focus that includes the continuous improvement of instruction and assessment, and meeting each studentŌĆÖs unique needs. Given this, the plan includes consideration of the following: ’éĘ ’éĘ ’éĘ ’éĘ ’éĘ Program, service, and facility enhancements that strengthen public education quality and accountability; The ongoing monitoring of student achievement, enrolment, and facility condition trends to ensure that the District responds appropriately to any changes over time; The reinvestment of revenue generated through the plan to strengthen programs and supports for students; Appropriate consultation with school communities to support effective transitions for students and staff affected by consolidations; and A commitment to working with community partners pursuing similar development goals (i.e. First Nations governments, The Tillicum Lelum Friendship Centre, Vancouver Island University, the City of Nanaimo, the Town of Ladysmith, the District of Lantzville, the Regional District of Nanaimo, and the Cowichan Valley Regional District), in order to reduce duplication of service and enhance student learning experiences and program choices. Optimal School Size Because the Proposed 10-Year EFL Plan is fundamentally designed to support the equitable delivery of programs and services to each student served by the district, optimal enrolments were established to help guide the planning process. It is projected that three secondary and eight elementary schools will stay at or below the minimum enrolment threshold (200 ŌĆō elementary; 600 - secondary) over the next ten years. In addition, the English programs at two French Immersion dual track elementary schools will remain well below the minimum enrolment guideline. As low enrolments at the elementary schools generally require triplegrading, and secondary school low enrolments significantly curtail student course offerings, all such schools fitting this profile have been considered for planning purposes and are identified in the chart in Appendix C. Ultimately, the optimal school size guidelines allow the right number of staff to serve the right number of students in all schools, across the system. Consolidations Currently, 10-Year EFL Plan involves seven school consolidations in 2013-2014 and 2014-2015 school years, three secondary (Cedar Secondary, Junior Learning Alternatives, and Senior Learning Alternatives) and three elementary (South Wellington, Davis Road Elementary and Woodbank Primary/North Cedar Intermediate). Finally, the plan calls for public consultation with respect to the proposed closure of Woodlands Secondary and Departure Bay Elementary, starting in the 2016-2017 school year. It is meant to complement the other two goals addressed in the DistrictŌĆÖs strategic plan of the continuous improvement of instruction and assessment and meeting each studentŌĆÖs unique needs. Dr. Player said that his recommended plan is directed to accomplishing the following goals: ’éĘ First and foremost, to enhance learning opportunities for all students; ’éĘ Second, to garner new (and long overdue) state-of-the art replacement facilities; ’éĘ Third, to consolidate costly positions so funding can go to the classroom; ’éĘ Fourth, to save wasted maintenance funds; and, ’éĘ Finally, to improve the long-term fiscal position of the Board. Further, he said, the expectations of the recommended plan are that it will also: ’éĘ Improve the student achievement results; ’éĘ Improve program offerings for students; ’éĘ Renew the approach and improve conditions for alternate program students; ’éĘ Assist in the achievement of the Board goals; and, ’éĘ Assist in balancing the budget long term. Dr. Player outlined what he sees as the current status of the District related to the recommended planŌĆÖs objectives: ’éĘ Deteriorating facilities, including 40 percent beyond their useful life; ’éĘ Costly empty space; ’éĘ Unnecessary staffing duplication; ’éĘ Dysfunctional buildings; ’éĘ Lack of program enrichment; and, ’éĘ A five million dollar deficit. Dr. Player stressed that action in renewing facilities is long overdue and said that as a result, the recommended plan proposes reconfigurations, consolidations, renovations, rebuilds, and property disposal. The recommended plan was developed in concert with the building administrators who, as the key educational leaders, know the childrenŌĆÖs needs and the districtŌĆÖs deficits. He said that while the consolidation of the buildings will result in the loss of some positions, it is recommended that in so far as possible, the reductions be undertaken through attrition. Dr. Player said that his recommended plan has also tried to take the opportunity to improve the offerings for students at all levels, in particular in the alternate and life skills programs. He said attention has been paid to the needs of the French immersion program but more importantly there has been a concerted effort to improve the districtŌĆÖs achievement results by adding more challenging academic programs in an equitable manner throughout the district. There is also a proposal to pilot an elementary arts program using outstanding facilities and expertise. Early development of the arts has been linked to 13 improved academic achievement and the pilot is designed so it could be duplicated in other elementary schools. One of the planŌĆÖs objectives is to ensure there is adequate population in each school to offer as wide a course selection as possible. Dr. PlayerŌĆÖs recommended plan is a 10-year plan divided into three stages: immediate actions to be undertaken in years one and two; medium term actions to be undertaken in years three through five, and long term which includes recommendations for years six through ten. Dr. PlayerŌĆÖs plan recommends that the Board take the following actions: ’éĘ Consolidate ten facilities; ’éĘ Rebuild four facilities; ’éĘ Rethink and repurpose the DRC/LRC; ’éĘ Institute three new enrichment programs; ’éĘ Relocate and renew the approach to the alternate programs; ’éĘ Adjust the immersion program; and ’éĘ Relocate the administrative functions. Dr. Player also stressed that it is important that the Board focus on its core business, which means disposing of a number of properties so that the money can be added to the districtŌĆÖs capital account, allowing for upgrading and maintaining buildings that are not directly addressed in his recommendations. In summary, Dr. Player said that his recommended plan would: ’éĘ Give the students the facilities they deserve and the program choice they need; ’éĘ Raise the achievement expectations throughout the district while giving students more challenging learning opportunities; ’éĘ Give teachers better working conditions with fewer split classes, decreased preparations and more resources; ’éĘ Address the facilities at the low end of the facility condition index ensuring better use of maintenance funds and better situations for custodians; ’éĘ As a byproduct, improves the operating budget and provides additional funding for capital costs. In short, he said, the recommended plan provides for renewal throughout the district in a manner that would enhance learning for all students.
