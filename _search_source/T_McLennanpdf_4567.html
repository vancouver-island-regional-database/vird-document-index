Good  evening  everyone.    My  name  is  Tine  McLennan.  On  September  2,  2015  I   attended  the  special  board  meeting  where  the  SD  Board  of  Trustees  chose  to  enter  into   consultation  on  the  Becoming  Sustainable  –  Facilities  Plan  Update.    This  proposed  plan   once  again  threw  many  of  the  communities  within  SD68  in  to  fight  mode.    This  sadly  has   been  the  case  in  SD68  for  many  years.    Giving  communities  little  chance  to  build  and   grow  as  the  dark  cloud  of  the  threat  of  school  closures  hangs  over  their  heads.    The   continual  and  repeated  targeting  of  certain  communities  is  unacceptable.    It  does   nothing  but  further  divide  the  school  district  and  cause  unnecessary  animosity,  pitting   communities  against  each  other.        I  don’t  believe  that  The  Public  Consultation  Information  Evening  report  presented  to  a   packed  house  Rutherford  Elementary  School  on  October  8,  2015  does  anything  to  show   who  the  Rutherford  Community  is.    Yes,  Rutherford  is  a  community  –  communities  are   defined  by  the  people  that  live  in  it  and  is  unique  in  a  way  that  is  often  hard  to  articulate   and  for  others  to  understand.   In  the  report  on  October  8  it  proposed  that  the  students  currently  attending  Rutherford   would  be  sent  to  the  three  outlying  schools  next  to  Rutherford.    I  would  like  to  point  out   that  the  students  currently  attending  Rutherford  are  actually  proposed  to  be  sent  to  four   different  elementary  schools.    Frank  J  Ney,  McGirr,  Randerson  Ridge  and  Georgia   Avenue.    Although  the  children  that  attend  the  Elementary  Life  Skills  program  attend  a   District  program  they  are  part  of  the  Rutherford  Community  just  like  everyone  else.     Two  of  the  schools  that  the  students  are  proposed  to  go  to,  FJN  and  McGirr,  are  already   operating  over  capacity.    To  propose  to  send  more  students  to  those  two  schools  is   irresponsible.       The  functional  capacity  of  McGirr  is  363  and  according  to  preliminary  enrolment   numbers  presented  in  October  there  are  402  students  currently  attending  McGirr.    That   puts  their  enrolment  at  111%.       The  functional  capacity  of  Frank  J  Ney  is  205  (the  published  and  widely  circulated  report   presented  on  October  8  incorrectly  lists  the  functional  capacity  of  FJN  at  405  even  going   so  far  as  listing  the  2014  enrolment  at  55%  based  on  this  functional  capacity.    The  2014   enrolment  for  FJN  was  over  100%.        FJN  would  only  have  a  functional  capacity  of  405  if   the  proposed  addition  is  included  in  the  calculation.    (This  kind  of  misstating  of  facts  with   no  formal  correction  from  the  district  has  left  many  to  speculate  if  this  is  a  done  deal).     According  to  preliminary  numbers  presented  in  October  there  are  216  students   attending  FJN.    That  puts  their  enrolment  at  105%.       While  currently  Randerson  Ridge  does  have  excess  capacity,  they  are  the  school  best   situated  to  absorb  the  extensive  development  in  that  area  (and  if  the  district  can  justify   waiting  for  other  school  populations  to  grow  why  can’t  we  justify  waiting  for  them  all  to).     It  is  also  the  school  best  situated  to  to  take  capacity  pressure  off  of  Uplands  Park  and   Rock  City.    Both  of  those  schools  are  near  or  over  capacity  currently  and  expected  to   grow.    A  responsible  thing  to  do  would  be  to  look  at  adjusting  catchment  boundaries  to   take  the  pressure  off  those  two  schools  and  others.   There  is  not  a  published  Ministry  document  that  states  elementary  schools  must  be  run   at  or  over  100%  capacity.         The  October  8th,  report  states  that  declining  enrolment  has  been  an  issue  at  Rutherford   since  the  1990’s.    This  should  come  as  no  surprise  to  any  reasonable  person.    In  1991,   McGirr  opened  and  Rutherford’s  physical  catchment  shrunk,  in  1996  Randerson  opened   and  was  also  allocated  part  of  the  Rutherford  catchment,  in  1999  Frank  J  Ney  opened   and  was  also  allocated  part  of  the  Rutherford  catchment.    Honestly  what  did  you  expect   to  happen.    It  is  kind  of  like  turning  farmland  into  housing  developments  and  then   wondering  why  there  isn’t  as  much  farm  fresh  produce  around.           We  have  asked  on  numerous  occasions  for  a  catchment  boundary  review  for  the  north   zone  but  nothing  has  happened.    Would  it  not  be  responsible  for  the  District  to   undertake  a  complete  District  wide  catchment  boundary  review  to  balance  out  over   capacity  at  some  schools  and  alleviate  unnecessary  capital  costs  of  additions.    The   review  would  also  take  into  consideration  future  development  in  the  North  Zone   including  in  the  Seaview  Elementary  School  catchment.    As  seen  in  the  2015   preliminary  enrolment  numbers,  Seaview  saw  growth  above  what  was  projected  and   that  trend  is  expected  to  continue.    In  2007/2008  there  was  an  undeveloped  approved   school  site  in  the  North  Zone  that  could  be  used  when  the  need  arises  this  is  no  longer   the  case.    The  school  site  at  Pioneer  Park  was  part  of  the  land  swap  with  the  city  in   2014.    There  is  no  “back  up”  site  or  plan.    What  we  have  is  what  we  have  and  the   District  is  certainly  not  in  a  position  to  go  out  and  purchase  real  estate.   Capacity  Utilization   Total  seats  at  the  elementary  level  (according  to  Appendix  A  in  the  report)  is  8283.     According  to  the  2015  preliminary  enrolment  numbers,  the  number  of  full  time   elementary  students  7840.  This  is  a  district  capacity  utilization  of  95%  at  the  elementary   level.  The  proposed  closure  of  Rutherford  would  result  in  a  net  removal  of  117  seats   from  the  functional  capacity  calculation  and  the  proposed  closure  of  either  Woodbank  or   NCI  would  further  remove  229  or  300  respectively  from  the  functional  capacity   calculation.    Using  the  2015  enrolment  numbers  this  would  mean  that  the  district  would   then  be  at  a  capacity  utilization  of  between  99  and  100%  capacity  at  the  elementary   level.    (I  have  not  removed  Gabriola  from  the  calculations  although  one  could  argue  that   by  nature  of  their  geographic  isolation  from  the  rest  of  the  district  I  likely  could)     Again  no  ministry  document  says  you  have  to  run  your  elementary  schools  at  the  that   capacity.   If  we  look  at  the  high  school  capacity  utilization,  we  get  a  very  different  picture.    The   total  seats  at  the  high  school  level  (according  to  Appendix  A  in  the  report)  6450   (including  Cedar  Secondary).    According  to  Appendix  A  in  2016  when  Cedar  Secondary   reopens  there  is  projected  to  be  1354  empty  seats  at  the  secondary  level,  resulting  in  a   capacity  utilization  of  79%.    If  as  proposed  in  the  September  2  plan,  Woodlands  is   closed  thus  removing  700  seats  from  the  secondary  capacity  the  functional  capacity   would  still  only  be  at  89%.    It  is  my  understanding  that  the  Ministry  wants  secondary   schools  run  at  about  105%  capacity  utilization.    So  this  shows  that  the  issues  sit  at  the   secondary  level  and  NOT  at  the  elementary  level.     Area  Standards   The  Area  Standards  document  published  by  the  Ministry  of  Education  prescribes  areas   and  other  standards  established  by  the  Ministry  of  Education  for  space  in  elementary,   middle  and  secondary  schools.    One  of  the  core  area  allowances  specified  in  the  area   standards  document  is  special  education  space.    For  a  school  the  size  of  Rutherford   this  area  amounts  to  160m2.    Rutherford  Elementary  being  an  older  building  has  larger   classrooms  and  are  counted  as  being  80m2  each.    So  according  to  this  document  the   Rutherford  should  be  entitled  to  allocate  two  classrooms  to  special  education.    A  far  cry   from  the  oversized  broom  closet  currently  allocated  to  student  support.    A  school  such  a   Frank  J  Ney  has  two  rooms  the  size  of  a  regular  classroom  allocated  to  special   education  and  thus  not  counting  in  their  capacity.    Time  for  the  district  to  come  to  the   table  and  support  all  the  schools  in  the  District.    This  is  just  one  example  of  the   inequality  that  is  found  throughout  SD68  when  it  comes  to  District  support  to  keep  a   school  community  thriving.     I  would  like  to  suggest  that  the  District  carry  out  a  catchment  boundary  review  prior  to   voting  to  close  Rutherford.     I  would  like  to  suggest  that  the  District  look  closely  at  the  Area  Standards  Document   published  by  the  Ministry  and  ensure  that  the  nominal  and  operating  capacities  of  all  the   SD  68  schools  is  calculated  correctly  taking  into  consideration  space  allocations  and   classroom  use.     I  would  like  the  Board  of  Trustees  to  take  a  hard  look  at  the  information  you  have  been   given  by  District  and  more  importantly  consider  what  information  that  you  have  NOT   been  given.    Ask  the  hard  questions  and  question  the  answers.    Don’t  assume  that  the   numbers  are  correct  and  ask  for  source  documents.    Consult  with  other  districts  to  see   how  comprehensive  their  consultation  reports  were.       Finally,  I  want  to  take  the  chance  to  remind  you  that  according  to  the  BC  School   Trustees  Association  the  key  work  of  school  boards  is  to  improve  student  achievement   through  community  engagement.    On  September  2,  2015  you  managed  to  engage  the   Rutherford  Community  by  voting  to  consult  on  a  plan  that  focuses  on  school  closures   and  consolidations  and  specifically  naming  Rutherford  for  closure.    You  are  the  public’s   voice  in  public  education.  You  are  to  connect  with  communities  and  ensure  that  local   values  and  priorities  are  reflected  in  public  school  programs,  policies  and  operations.   Many  volunteers  in  the  Rutherford  Community  have  put  in  100s  of  hours  to  protect  our   local  values  and  priorities.    This  time  could  be  better  spent  finding  and  implementing   innovative  and  creative  ideas  to  ensure  that  the  children  in  our  community  get  the   education  they  deserve.    Give  us  the  chance  to  work  with  you,  not  against  you,  to   ensure  that  Rutherford  Elementary  remains  open  and  thriving  for  years  to  come.     Imagine  what  our  tax  paying  communities  could  accomplish  if  we  put  all  our  energies   into  something  positive  instead  of  the  constant  state  of  fear  of  closure  and  uncertainty   that  hangs  over  our  heads.
