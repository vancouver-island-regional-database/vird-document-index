Woodlands PAC Presentation to School Board #68 re: Woodlands Secondary School Potential Closure (2015-Nov-04) My name is Chris Sholberg and I live at 525 Maple Street. My daughter graduated from Woodlands Secondary in 2014, and my son is currently in Grade 8 at Woodlands. On behalf of the Woodlands Secondary PAC I have been asked to make the following presentation. It is our hope that this presentation will clearly convey the perspective and depth of feeling that the community currently has with respect to the proposed Woodlands closure. Woodlands is a wonderful secondary school, and is both an asset to the School District and an asset to the local community which surrounds it. As parents, we are very satisfied with the quality of education our children receive. For us, the school is very much a social focal point in our community, helping to promote pride in the surrounding neighbourhood and a sense of completeness in our community. We understand the funding dilemma faced by the School Board and do not blame the Board for the decisions it must make. In our opinion, the fiscal crisis that has been afflicting the district since 2002 is really a funding crisis and starts with our Provincial government and its neoconservative fiscal policy. While the logic of deficit control is understandable, we do not feel that changes to the School District funding formula made in 2001, and the Province’s continuing fiscal restraint, make a whole lot of sense when it results in the closure of valuable assets such as Woodlands. For us the school is not a business – it is a public good whose overriding goal is to nurture and educate young people who will become productive citizens in our society. In our experience, Woodlands has, and continues to, achieve this goal. 1 Closure of Woodlands will impact this community negatively, not only on an academic level, but also on a social and economic level. The only question is how deeply. In order to illustrate how deeply this closure will affect the community we want to highlight a number of specific impacts Woodlands closure will have on the local neighbourhoods surrounding the school: 1) Woodlands Secondary is situated in the centre of Nanaimo in the hospital area, a commercial/institutional node considered to have the highest residential density per block of neighbourhood settlement in the city. Closing Woodlands would essentially concentrate District resources in north and south-end secondary schools, leaving a huge geographic gap in central Nanaimo. In a community that has already absorbed the closure of Princess Anne Elementary and Dufferin Elementary, this would have devastating effects on educational access and social equity. 2) Closure of Woodlands will create an “education desert” by removing an essential, community based service from the local residential neighbourhoods. Effectively, a large service gap over 6 kilometres wide (as the crow flies) will be created between NDSS and Wellington. In comparison, the current distance between the five existing secondary schools ranges between 1.55 and 3.72 kilometers. Generally speaking, these are fairly equitable, and walkable, separation distances – this equity and walkability will be thrown out of balance with the closure of Woodlands. 2 3) The local community’s cohesion and social fabric will be disrupted by Woodland’s closure. A key community meetings space/social hub in central Nanaimo will be lost. It has been found over the past two decades that a majority of school closures across North America, tend to be concentrated in older, urban neighbourhoods. Studies in the United States have compared the impacts of this trend to the urban renewal initiatives of the 1960s which effectively eroded, and in some cases destroyed, the social fabric of inner city neighbourhoods. 4) Woodland’s closure will reduce local land values and demographic mix. By removing the local secondary school, the community will be less attractive to those looking to buy a property or place a business in the area. To illustrate this point, one study in 2008 by the National Association of Realtors, noted that 27% of home buyers listed school quality and 21% listed proximity to schools as deciding factors in their home purchase. This will in turn also affect the local population diversity, reducing the neighbourhood’s vibrancy through lack of population renewal. 5) In general, the closure will run counter to the concept of a complete and sustainable community as encouraged in the City of Nanaimo's Official Community Plan. Over time, concentration of growth will occur in the City’s urban nodes and corridors. One of five key urban nodes is located around the Hospital area and will encourage both institutional, commercial and residential growth within the node as well as growth in the residential areas around 3 the node. This development policy will continue to heighten demand for school services in the central Nanaimo area. In addition to these impacts, we also wanted to provide a number of specific observations relevant to Woodlands and the Revised Facilities Renewal Plan: 1) In 2006 plans for the renovation and expansion of Woodlands Secondary School were approved by the Ministry of Education, with the commitment to release the approved capital funds pending the results of the District's Facilities Renewal Plan. In upholding the recommendations put forward in the District's Facilities Renewal Plan, the Woodlands Vision Committee of the time requested that the District recognize its earlier commitments to Woodlands and reallocate the $31.7 million originally committed for the expansion of Dover Bay Secondary and the expansion and renovation of Woodlands Secondary to rebuild a new secondary school at the Woodlands' site that was intended to meet the educational needs of central Nanaimo. Unfortunately, this plan was changed with the election of a new School Board, demonstrating that decisions affecting the renewal/facilities plan over the years have not been immune from political intervention. 2) The decision to re-open Cedar Secondary has altered the Facilities Renewal Plan and has significantly impacted the plans equity. For example, the motion on this piece of the district-wide facilities plan did not appear to consider an analysis of the financial impacts of re-opening Cedar Secondary School, and in particular the immediate impacts on both Ladysmith Secondary and John 4 Barsby with respect to course selection and variety, decrease in extra-curricular activities, and loss of efficiencies, as well as costs related to re-converting Cedar Secondary from its proposed use as an elementary school back into a secondary school. 3) The community and educational values presented to keep Cedar Secondary open are equally applicable to Woodlands Secondary. Given the Board's decision to reopen Cedar Secondary we would assume a similar level of Board support for keeping Woodlands open at this time. We are a small high school with a strong sense of community, and in fact have had students transfer from the larger high schools to our school specifically for this community feeling. All of our students and staff know and support each other and make a point of connecting on a regular basis through a variety of methods and programs. 4) As an alternative to creating one mega-school, the District could consider repairs and additions to several schools, bringing each school population to 1100 students which is well within the optimum school size limit. Instead of spending up to $50 million or more on one facility, this would ensure the equitable spread of funds, programs and services throughout the district. 5) Finally, it is worth noting that as part of the previous Facilities Renewal Plan, the Board passed a motion on 2013-Jun-26 to postpone relocation of Woodlands students until a new facility was built at the NDSS site. Based on these impacts and observations, we would request that Woodlands not be closed, and continue to operate as the community and educational asset that it is. Thank You. 5
