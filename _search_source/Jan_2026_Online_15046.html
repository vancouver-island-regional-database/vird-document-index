Kw’umut Lelum’s *Respecting the Harvest* program, launched in partnership with the Kw’umut Lelum Foundation, offers young people from its nine member Nations the opportunity to learn traditional harvesting skills while receiving guidance from knowledge keepers, mentors, and experienced hunters. Initially created to support youth in care, the program has expanded to welcome a wider group of young participants, many between the ages of 12 and 19. Its goal is to revitalize traditional food practices and support food sovereignty, while reinforcing the Coast Salish teachings, or *snuw’uy’ulh*, that emphasize respect, responsibility, and interconnectedness. Throughout the year, participants take part in hands-on activities that range from firearm safety and rifle handling to tracking, harvesting, and processing game. After a successful hunt, youth learn to prepare the meat, tan hides, and use bones and antlers for regalia and ceremonial items—ensuring that every part of the animal is used with care and intention. In recent years, the program has expanded to include a marine harvesting component. Youth head out on the water to learn boating skills, read tide tables, and harvest traditional foods such as lingcod, clams, and sea urchins. Much of the catch is shared with elders and community members, reinforcing teachings 19 around reciprocity and community care. Elders and experienced harvesters play a central role, offering cultural teachings and mentoring participants as they gain confidence and leadership skills. Many returning youth take on mentorship roles themselves, guiding younger participants and helping to pass down the knowledge they’ve gained. Organizers say the program is not only rebuilding cultural knowledge but also strengthening identity, pride, and belonging among youth who have often faced disruptions in their connection to traditional ways. As one of several cultural initiatives led by Kw’umut Lelum, *Respecting the Harvest* continues to grow—bringing young people back to the land, the water, and the teachings that have sustained Coast Salish communities since time immemorial. How it usually works • • • • • 21 Scammers drive an unmarked white van (hence the name). They approach people in parking lots, gas stations, or outside stores. They claim they have extra inventory, a shipping mistake, or need to sell items quickly. They show convincing-looking boxes, manuals, and spec sheets with well-known brand names. The price sounds like a steal (e.g., “worth $3,000, yours for $500”). What you actually get • The electronics are usually cheap knockoffs or extremely low-quality products. • Brand names are fake or slightly altered to look legitimate. Once you get home, you discover the item: • Performs poorly or doesn’t work • Isn’t worth anywhere near what you paid • Has no warranty or customer support Common red flags • • • • • High-pressure sales tactics (“I need to sell this right now”) Refusal to accept credit cards (cash only) No store, website, or verifiable business info Too-good-to-be-true pricing Sales happening out of a vehicle, not a shop Is it illegal? Yes. White van scams are considered fraud and are illegal in Canada, the U.S., and many other countries. How to protect yourself • • • • Don’t buy electronics from someone selling out of a vehicle Research the brand and model online before buying Walk away from pressure tactics—legitimate sellers don’t rush you If you see this happening, report it to local authorities or consumer protection agencies Some of our own Stz’uminus members have fallen victim to these scams recently. Scam artists have been operating in the area of Stz’uminus/Ladysmith lately. CHEAP ASPHALT SCAM 22 What the scam looks like on Vancouver Island Police and RCMP have issued warnings to residents about door-to-door offers of heavily discounted asphalt or driveway paving, often from people in a pickup truck who show up unannounced at your home. Typically: A person approaches a homeowner saying they have leftover asphalt from a nearby job and can pave your driveway at a low price. They may be aggressive or pushy, sometimes trying to pressure seniors or hurry a decision. The asphalt material itself appears poor quality or recycled and lacks proper binder; it doesn’t match what a normal paving job should look like. In at least one reported case, after concerns were raised about the material, the crew packed up and left without completing the work. Police are still investigating the origin of the asphalt being offered for resale and whether the operators are legitimate. Why this is a scam? This is part of a wider pattern of driveway/asphalt paving scams seen across Canada and other places: Scammers claim they have leftover material and pressure you to act quickly. They may do substandard work or none at all after collecting cash or upfront payments. Some won’t provide proper identification, business licences, or contact info. If you try to contact them later, the phone number or business info often doesn’t work because it’s fake or temporary. Red flags to watch for: • • • • • Unsolicited visits by people offering to pave your driveway. Claims of “leftover asphalt” that needs to be used quickly. Pressure to decide on the spot or pay cash upfront. Lack of business licensing, ID, or verifiable credentials. Asphalt that looks like crushed gravel or recycled material rather than proper hot asphalt. What you should do • • • • 23 Don’t agree to work on the spot—legitimate contractors won’t pressure you like this. Ask for proper identification, a licence, and a written contract before paying anything. Research the company online and check reviews or licence info. If something feels off, report it to your local RCMP or police, and consider contacting the Canadian Anti-Fraud Centre. FAKE INVOICE SCAM What you’re describing is a type of “fake invoice” or “false billing” scam that targets businesses — and it’s unfortunately quite common. Essentially, scammers send invoices for products or services that were never ordered or received, and then try to use psychological tricks (like doctored voice recordings or “proof” of authorization) to make you pay. How this scam typically works • • • • • Fake invoice arrives — via email, mail, or even fax — claiming your company owes money for office supplies, toner, advertising, directory listings, domain services, or other recurring needs. The invoice looks legitimate — it uses logos, business names, contact details, and standard invoice formatting to appear real. Scammers impersonate real vendors you’ve worked with, or create completely fake supplier identities that mimic real ones. They may follow up with phone calls and pressure you to pay quickly or threaten legal action, late fees, or account suspension. In more sophisticated versions, they’ll use doctored voice recordings of your own receptionist or employees saying things like “yes” to confirm orders — and then try to play those back as “proof” that you authorized the order. This tactic has been referenced in real enforcement cases. Why this scam works • • • • Invoices look authentic and are easily mistaken for real bills from vendors you regularly deal with. Your accounts or administrative staff may assume someone else in the company placed the order. The amount may be relatively small, so paying seems less risky than verifying it. Including someone’s real name or details from your business makes the scam feel plausible. Red flags to watch for: • • • • • You never ordered the product or service. The company name looks familiar but the email domain, phone number, or address is off or unknown. The invoice demands immediate payment or threatens consequences. They try to bypass your normal verification or purchasing procedures. They reference a recording or signature they claim validates the order. This is a manipulation tactic. What to do if this happens • • • • • Do not pay right away. Pause and verify with your known supplier. Call the real vendor using a phone number you know is legitimate, not the one on the invoice. Check internally to see if anyone authorized the order. Train staff about fake invoices and how to handle them before payment. If you determine it is a scam, report it to your local police and to agencies like the Canadian AntiFraud Centre. As you can see, there are a number of scams going around in this area.
