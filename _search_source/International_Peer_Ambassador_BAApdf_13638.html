Board Authority Authorized Course School District/Independent School Authority Name: Nanaimo-Ladysmith Public Schools School District/Independent School Authority Number: School District 68 Developed by: Shelley Gvojich, Kim Darbyshire Date Developed: May 26, 2026 School Name: Ladysmith Secondary School Superintendent Approval Date (for School Districts only): Principal’s Name: Shelley Gvojich Board/Authority Approval Date: Board/Authority Chair Signature: Course Name: International Peer Ambassador Number of Course Credits: 4 Grade Level of Course: 11 Number of Hours of Instruction: 120 hours Superintendent Signature: Board/Authority Prerequisite(s): None Special Training, Facilities or Equipment Required: • • • • • Standard classroom or meeting space that allows for small-group interaction and collaborative activities Access to basic materials for communication and orientation activities (e.g., printed resources, visual aids, digital tools) Opportunities for interaction with international students within the school setting (e.g., scheduled peer ambassador support, orientation activities, or structured events) Optional access to community-based learning opportunities to support real-world application, where available Access to transportation Course Synopsis: This course prepares students to support international students as they transition into a new school, culture, and community. Students learn how to respond to real-world challenges experienced by visitors, including unfamiliar systems, cultural adjustment, and social opportunities, while also developing understanding of the diverse cultural backgrounds of international students. Through applied practice and facilitated opportunities, students help international students with orientation processes and help with access to opportunities, resources, and connections within the school and local community. This course aligns with NLPS strategic priorities by promoting belonging, intercultural understanding, and student success. Goals and Rationale: This course focuses on developing students’ ability to respond effectively to the needs of students entering a new cultural and school environment. Students build awareness of how cultural differences, language barriers, and unfamiliar expectations impact an international learner’s experience. The course emphasizes practical strategies for supporting inclusion, easing transitions, and fostering meaningful connections between international students and the school community. Students develop skills specific to peer support, intercultural communication, and community connection. Aboriginal Worldviews and Perspectives: NLPS is located on the traditional territory of the Snuneymuxw, Stz'uminus and Snaw'naw'as Mustimuxw. This course reflects First Peoples Principles of Learning through a focus on relational responsibility, respect, and connection to place. Coast Salish perspectives guide how students approach supporting international students as guests on this land, emphasizing respect, humility, and relationship-building. Students may also be introduced to local hulq’umi’num’ language and concepts that reflect Coast Salish approaches to relationship, place, and hospitality. The First Peoples Principles of Learning are reflected through this course, in particular: Learning is holistic, reflexive, reflective, experiential, and relational (focused on connectedness, on reciprocal relationships, and a sense of place). Students engage in meaningful reciprocal relationships, develop intercultural understanding, and build a strong sense of community and place while supporting international students. This allows for students to reflect on their own cultural identity and their place within our school and community as they become more self-aware and socially responsible leaders. Learning requires exploration of one‘s identity. Through cross-culture exchange, students gain a better understanding of self and others. This enriches our schools by creating a more diverse and inclusive community. Learning ultimately supports the well-being of the self, the family, the community, the land, the spirits, and the ancestors. Students will also explore local Coast Salish welcoming practices and consider how Indigenous concepts of hospitality, reciprocity and relationality can inform their work as ambassadors. International Peer Ambassador Grade: 11 BIG IDEAS Effective peer support involves developing intentional, responsive, and culturally aware practices. Structured peer support strengthens engagement, confidence, and participation in school environments. Communication practices must adapt to differences in language, culture, and experience. Inclusive school environments are built through actions that remove barriers and increase access. Intercultural understanding is developed through authentic interactions and shared experiences with others. Big Ideas – Elaborations Big Idea: Effective peer support involves developing intentional, responsive, and culturally aware practices. Providing meaningful support to others involves recognizing individual needs and responding in thoughtful and adaptable ways. Ambassadors help reduce confusion and uncertainty by offering clear guidance, modeling expectations, and adjusting their approach based on the situation. Big Idea: Structured peer support strengthens engagement, confidence, and participation in school environments. Peer ambassadors provide reassurance, help clarify expectations, and model how to engage in school routines and activities. This support increases confidence and encourages active participation. Big Idea: Communication practices must adapt to differences in language, culture, and experience. Effective peer ambassadors recognize that individuals may interpret communication differently. Adjusting communication strategies—such as pacing, clarity, and use of visuals—helps reduce misunderstandings and supports more effective interactions. Big Idea: Inclusive school environments are built through actions that remove barriers and increase access. Barriers such as language, unfamiliar routines, or lack of access to information can limit international student participation. Identifying these barriers allows students in the course to begin to explore strategies to reduce them. Big Idea: Intercultural understanding is developed through authentic interactions and shared experiences with others. Intercultural understanding is developed through interactions where individuals respond to others with awareness and adaptability. Actions such as adjusting communication, offering guidance, and creating opportunities for connection demonstrate this understanding and strengthen relationships within the school community. worldviews - emphasize interconnectedness, relational responsibility, and learning through experience, story, and community. Knowledge is understood as holistic and grounded in relationships between people, land, and place. Empathy - involves recognizing and understanding another person’s feelings and experiences and responding in a way that shows care and consideration. It supports positive relationships and effective support across differences. Perspective-taking - is the ability to consider situations from another person’s point of view, especially when their experiences differ from one’s own. It helps reduce misunderstanding and supports more thoughtful responses. Respectful dialogue - Respectful dialogue involves listening actively, communicating with openness, and valuing differing perspectives. It creates conditions for understanding and constructive interaction. Culturally responsive - approaches adapt communication and actions to align with individuals’ cultural backgrounds and experiences. This helps ensure interactions are respectful, relevant, and effective. Peer support strategies - are structured ways students assist others through guidance, encouragement, and shared experience. Effective strategies maintain appropriate boundaries and connect peer ambassadors to further support when needed. Cultural adjustment - refers to the process of adapting to new cultural norms, expectations, and environments. It often involves stages that include initial excitement, challenge, adaptation, and increased confidence. Bias - refers to assumptions or judgments that can influence how individuals perceive and interact with others. Recognizing bias is essential for responding fairly and thoughtfully in diverse environments. Peer-level responsibility - defines the appropriate role students have in supporting others without taking on professional or adult responsibilities. It includes knowing when to seek help from staff. Communication strategies - are techniques used to improve understanding, such as clarifying, simplifying, checking for understanding, and using visuals. These strategies are especially important in multilingual or unfamiliar contexts. English proficiency - refers to a person’s ability to understand and use English in speaking, listening, reading, and writing. Levels of proficiency impact how individuals access information and participate in school. Multimodal communication - uses multiple forms—such as visual, verbal, written, and gestural—to support understanding. It is especially effective when language barriers are present. Non-verbal communication in cultural contexts - includes gestures, facial expressions, eye contact, and body language, which can vary significantly across cultures. Understanding these differences helps prevent misinterpretation. Barriers that limit participation in school - may include language challenges, unfamiliar routines, cultural differences, or lack of access to information. Identifying and addressing these barriers helps improve inclusion and participation. in the school community Content – Elaborations Cultural norms - shared expectations and behaviours that guide how individuals communicate, interact, and participate within a group. Understanding these norms helps individuals interpret actions and respond appropriately in different cultural contexts. Stages of cultural adjustment - include initial excitement, challenge or disorientation, gradual adaptation, and increased confidence. Recognizing these stages helps explain changes in behaviour and emotional responses during transition. Clarification techniques - are strategies used to confirm understanding, such as asking follow-up questions, paraphrasing, or using examples. These techniques reduce confusion and support clearer communication, especially in unfamiliar or multilingual settings. Active listening - involves fully focusing on the speaker, interpreting both verbal and non-verbal messages, and responding thoughtfully. It supports understanding, builds trust, and improves communication across cultural differences. Cultural perspectives on education - influence expectations around teaching styles, authority, participation, and assessment. Recognizing these differences helps explain varied approaches to learning and supports more effective interaction in diverse classrooms.
