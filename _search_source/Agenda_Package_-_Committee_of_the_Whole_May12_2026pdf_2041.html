EXECUTIVE SUMMARY: This is the first quarterly update since the 2026-2030 Financial Plan was adopted. At the time of this report, collective agreement negotiations with CUPE 401, as well as initial collective agreement negotiations with CUPE 606 (Firefighters), had not been concluded, resulting in potentially significant unknown costs. PREVIOUS DIRECTION: N/A INTRODUCTION/BACKGROUND: This is the first quarterly financial report to the Committee for 2026. General Revenues – Taxes & Other Revenues Projected to receive $16k more in revenue This report reflects financial activity as of March 31, at which time property taxes had not yet been levied. The 2026 property tax bylaws were adopted on May 5, and property tax notices are in the process of being mailed. The due date for payment of all property taxes is July 2, 2026, after which a 2% penalty will be applied to the outstanding current balance. An additional 8% penalty will be applied to any current taxes remaining outstanding as of September 9, 2026. The interest rate charged on arrears and delinquent taxes is set by the Province and is currently 7.45%1. The Town is also experiencing a decrease in interest earned on investments. As of the end of the first quarter, interest earned totalled $308,775, which is approximately $20,000 lower than in 2025 and nearly $149,000 lower than the first quarter of 2024. Fortis franchise fees, which represent revenue to the Town, are projected to be higher in 2026 than in 2025 by $14,844. 1 https://www2.gov.bc.ca/gov/content/governments/local-governments/finance/requisition-taxation/arrears-ordelinquent-taxes-due-to-local-governments Page 20 of 122 General Government Expenses projected to be $56k more than budget The revenues in this area are slightly greater than in prior years by approximately $3,000 largely due to additional parking rental revenues associated with the work at 440 1st Ave. Legal fees are higher than during the same period in 2025; however, this is partially offset by cost savings related to the municipal election that occurred during this period last year. It is anticipated that contingency funds will be required to fund legal costs, which are expected to exceed budget by year end. Spending for Mayor and Council is higher for the period at $60,240 (2025 – $43,059), reflecting that all positions are currently filled and that some conference registrations and accommodation costs were prepaid. Community Contributions are lower in 2026 at $68,970 (2025 – $92,920), as three organizations that were removed from the Grant-in-Aid process and transitioned to servicing agreements are scheduled to receive their funding on July 1. Information Technology expenses are higher by $19,151, primarily due to timing differences related to software renewals. Cybersecurity initiatives are currently underway. Expenditures in the Human Resources department are lower in 2026 at $70,921 (2025 – $86,309), as some consulting costs have been deferred until the conclusion of CUPE negotiations. Unexecuted annual debt servicing payments budgeted at $965,570 for the new City Hall project were included in the 2026 budget. These funds may be utilized in 2026; remaining funds will be reserved and, for the purpose of this report, do not show as surplus. Protective Services Projected to be $9k under budget Net Fire Department expenditures total $188,297 for the quarter (2025 – $129,804). The increase is primarily attributable to the addition of the Deputy Fire Chief position, the timing of turnout gear purchases, and the restocking of first responder supplies on fire apparatus. The total number of calls increased by 13 in 2026. The total personnel hours spent responding to calls and false alarms were lower than in 2025. Training hours are also lower this quarter, as department-wide training occurred during the same period last year. That training is scheduled for Q2 of 2026; therefore, no overall cost savings are anticipated. Overall, paid-on-call firefighter compensation is approximately $11,000 higher for the period. Fire vehicle servicing costs, including debt servicing, are approximately $3,600 higher than the same quarter in 2025; however, expenditures remain within budget. Net policing costs are approximately $24,000 higher in 2026 compared to 2025, primarily due to E-Comm costs of $53,820 recorded in the first quarter of 2026 (2025 – $0). At the time of this report, the Town had not yet received the RCMP quarterly Page 21 of 122 invoice. An amount of $405,835 is budgeted for the quarter, representing staffing of 7.9 RCMP members and three administrative positions. Net Bylaw Compliance costs are approximately $3,000 lower in the first quarter, reflecting protective equipment purchases that occurred in Q1 of 2025. The Town has also recorded additional fine revenues during the period, partially offsetting costs incurred in responding to a nuisance property. Public Works & Engineering Revenues projected to receive $9k more than budget Expenses projected to be $35k less than budget During the first quarter, additional operating expenses were incurred, with the most significant increases related to storm sewer maintenance and pavement maintenance. It should be noted that funding for both of these programs was increased in the 2026 budget. Due to a milder winter, overtime expenditures associated with snow clearing were significantly lower in 2026. Total snow and ice control costs for this period amounted to $25,840 in 2026, compared to $76,330 in 2025. Subdivision fees for a development at Churchill were received in this quarter, resulting in the subdivision fees target budget being met (2026 - $32,621; 2025 - $112,884). Vacancies within the Engineering Department during the first quarter of 2026 are expected to result in cost savings; however, GIS-related fees were higher in 2026 than in 2025. Solid Waste Revenues projected to receive $6k more than budget Expenses projected to be $2k under budget The solid waste collection contractor was changed earlier this year. The new contractor’s monthly charges are approximately $24,000 higher than those of the previous contractor. While the fees charged to properties for this service have also increased, reserve funds remain budgeted to offset the resulting cost difference. Cemetery Operations Revenues projected to be $3k less than budget Expenses projected to be $9k under budget Cemetery burials, and the associated revenues, were slightly lower than those recorded during the same period last year. Cemetery operational costs were higher this quarter, as mild weather conditions allowed maintenance activities to commence earlier than in previous years. Development Services Projected to receive $268k more in revenue Expenses projected to be $38k less than budget There were 747 Business Licences levied for 2026, of which 38 were new for 2026. The budget of $70,000 was exceeded by $5,950. These funds are used to pay for the Page 22 of 122 rent and a servicing agreement for the Chamber of Commerce, budgeted at approximately $67,000. There was $225k in building permit revenue deferred from 2025, plus an additional $52k received in the first quarter of 2026 which will result in an expected surplus of building permit revenue by the end of the year. Development Services expenses are greater by $9k over last year (2026 - $226,278; 2025 - $217,223), mainly due to an increase in legal fees associated with planning files. Most of these fees will be recovered as the projects proceed. There was a vacancy in this area for the first quarter and an expected vacancy later in the year in the area that will result in employment costs being under budget, though contracted services will be used to partially offset the vacancy workload. Parks Expenses projected to be $4k over than budget The Parks department is preparing for the upcoming seasonal work. Additional staffing is lined up to complete the scheduled work. Net expenses were $163,615 for the quarter in 2026, compared with $148,990 at this time last year. Much of the difference is in the employment costs as often the parks crew are dispatched to perform snow clearing operations. Though the gas prices started to rise at the end of this quarter, there was minimal impact to the net costs. Should the gas prices remain high, the use of equipment charged is anticipated to be impacted. Recreation & Culture Services Projected to receive $31k more in revenue Expenses projected to be 11k more than budget Aquatic revenues are $5k less this period than last year (2026 - $105,380; 2025 $110,501). Much of this difference was in the aquatics programs which show a drop of $13k though offset with pool rentals of $5k more. Fitness Studio revenues are also lower in this quarter over last year (2026 - $89,994; 2025 - $100,159). Again, programs show a decrease in revenues over last year, though passes and admissions are expected to exceed budgeted revenues. Recreation programs are also lower than previous year and are not expected to meet budget by year-end based on current trends. Employment costs in both Aquatics and Fitness are greater for the quarter than in previous year as there was a vacancy last year. For this quarter, the total expenses were $437,601 (2025 - $411,741). Overall, the expenses are expected to be within budget at year end. Facility Maintenance Expenses projected to be 10k more than budget Facility Maintenance expenses are greater this quarter than last year (2026 - $576,006; 2025 - $534,592). Much of this increase can be attributed to increased insurance costs at $204,177 versus $187,395 last year. Utility costs are also higher this quarter than last year and may exceed budget by $5k at the end of the year. Page 23 of 122 Expenses –Sewer & Water Services Revenues projected to be on budget Expenses projected to be $131k under budget The first quarterly utility bills were mailed in early April and are due May 28th, 2026. Water consumption for the first quarter was lower than in previous years, which is reflected in reduced consumption revenues for the period (2026 – $375,019; 2025 – $378,542). Despite this variance, revenues are expected to meet budget projections by year end; however, a number of external factors may impact overall water revenues. Sewer revenues are tracking as expected for the quarter at approximately 25% of the annual budget (2026 – $516,615; 2025 – $495,997). Employment costs are less than budgeted for this quarter as there continues to be a vacancy in this area. Treatment chemicals in the water utility are greater this period than last year, though in the sewer utility, they are lower (2026 - $62,179; 2025 - $73,265 for both utilities). The temporary borrowing will be executed on the Holland Creek Dam raising loan in 2026. Not all of the funds will be utilized in 2026; remaining funds will be reserved and, for the purpose of this report, do not show as surplus. INTRODUCTION/BACKGROUND: 6th Avenue is a key street in the Ladysmith community. It is roughly 1km long and provides access to the FJCC, Primary School, Secondary School, Fire Hall, Public Works Yard, RCMP Detachment and residential properties. Pavement and utilities are in decline and in need of replacement. In early 2025, a consulting engineering team led by Koers Associates was hired to take this project through the following 3 stages: 1. Develop Conceptual Design for the replacement of 6th Avenue between Symonds Street and Methuen Street with: Modern street design and geometry New underground utilities Improved active transportation Page 110 of 122 Attachment A 2. Develop Phasing Plan 3. Prepare Design Drawings & Cost Estimate for Phase 1 With the Conceptual Design now complete, staff bring this update report to the Committee for consideration. Transportation Study (Watt Consulting Group) A traffic study focused on 6th Avenue was completed in the first half of 2025. The study included:  Turning movement counts at Symonds Street, Malone Road and Kitchener Street  Vehicle counts and speed measurement (pneumatic tubes) between High Street and Gatacre Street, and between White Street and Methuen Street  An inventory of street parking: 173 spaces (54 parallel, 119 angled or perpendicular)  A parking utilization study  Analysis of ICBC collision reports in the area  Use of tools from BC Active Transportation Design Guide Recommendations for Design: 1. Reclassify 6th Avenue from Local Road to Activity Street (as defined in the OCP) 2. Implement traffic calming measures 3. Include separated sidewalks where possible, at least 1.8m wide 4. Consider bike lanes or a separate bikeway 5. Give enhanced consideration to the needs of vulnerable users (young, old, mobility challenged) Conceptual Design (Koers Associates) The consultant then created a conceptual design for a complete rebuilding of 6th Avenue. Conceptual drawings for each block are included in Attachment A. Highlights include:  Road grades adjusted to avoid ponding/slow drainage  Drainage confined by curbs and directed to catch basins  Concrete curbs with access to properties restricted to driveways only  Traffic calming: curb bump-outs and shorter pedestrian crossings  3.0 m wide asphalt bikeway on south side of street  1.5 m wide concrete sidewalk on north side of street  Slight increase in parking (184 spaces: 91 parallel and 93 angled)  Increased capacity and better looping of watermains  Replacement of storm drains in rear yards on Stillin Drive, to Tyee Creek  Replacement of storm drains along 6th Avenue Staff have reviewed the conceptual design and believe the project as presented will address long-standing infrastructure deficiencies while creating attractive options for Page 111 of 122 Attachment A active transportation.
