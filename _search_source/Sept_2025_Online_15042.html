EDUCATION UPDATES TRIBAL JOURNEY TO ELWHA 2025 Stz’uminus First Nation – July 26, 2025 In July, Stz’uminus First Nation welcomed seven ocean-going canoes as part of the annual Tribal Canoe Journey, a cultural tradition that has united Indigenous coastal nations for decades. Community members gathered on the shores to greet the canoe families, who had paddled for almost 6 hours from Snuneymuxw. Elders George Harris and Buffi David officially welcomed the visitors into Stz’uminus territory, acknowledging their strength and the sacred purpose of the journey. After a shared meal, paddlers and supporters exchanged teachings, offered words of wisdom, and celebrated with songs and dancing late into the evening. The Tribal Canoe Journey traces its roots to July 1989, when 15 Tribes completed the first modern-day Paddle to Seattle, arriving at Golden Gardens Park as part of Washington State’s centennial celebrations. The event, initiated by Emmett Oliver of the Quinault Indian Nation, aimed to revive traditional canoe travel, honor ancestral pathways, and strengthen intertribal connections. Since then, over 30 Tribal Journeys have been held, each hosted by a different Nation. This year’s journey will culminate in Elwha, Washington, on July 31. Organizers expect a historic gathering of approximately 112 canoes representing 81 Canoe Families, along with more than 4,400 02 canoe family members and 78 support boats. The canoes departed from Stz’uminus at 6:45 a.m. on Sunday, continuing their sacred journey southward with the next landing in Cowichan. We extend heartfelt wishes to all skippers, paddlers, safety crews, and ground teams for a safe and powerful voyage to Elwha. These losses often bring deep and complex grief̶layered with guilt, regret, anger, stigma, and systemic failures̶leaving many people feeling isolated. The program recognizes that substance use is often tied to pain and trauma. Its focus is to remove blame and shame from both the loved one who used substances and their family. For Indigenous communities, this grief is further complicated by intergenerational trauma from colonization and historical injustices. HIH provides a caring, trusting environment where participants can share their stories, support one another, and begin to heal. “Connection is medicine.” Peer-Led Healing HIH circles are peer-led, meaning they are guided by those who have also lost a loved one in the toxic-drug crisis. This shared experience creates a powerful source of connection, empathy, and understanding. The group is not therapy; instead, facilitators walk alongside members in their grief journey, offering listening, compassion, and culturally safe practices such as Ceremony, teachings, and prayer. Guiding Values • Commit to fostering a caring and just community where all are treated with respect. • Advocate for members to share their stories and have their voices heard. • Value integrity, honesty, and mutual respect to keep our circle safe. • Emphasize belonging and connection, ensuring no one grieves alone. • Stand for diversity and inclusion, free of racism, stigma, and discrimination. • Uphold the rights of people marginalized by substance use and work toward systemic change. • Recognize the strength of partnerships and the power of a united voice for healing. Honoring Grief Grief connected to substance use is often disenfranchised grief̶unacknowledged or invalidated by society. HIH exists to ensure that these losses are recognized and honored. Our lands are surrounded by heavily wooded areas, and the current dry conditions mean the risk of wildfire is extremely high. Even small fires can spread quickly and put our homes, families, and the land at risk. We ask all community members to please stop having any fires until the fire ban is lifted. This includes backyard fires, campfires, smokers and any open burning. Your cooperation is essential in protecting our community and the safety of everyone living here. Thank you for respecting this important precaution. I am honored to have this opportunity to serve our community and am committed to being accountable and transparent to the Stz’uminus First Nation Community in all that I do. Together, we will work to create educational opportunities that honour our past, serve our present, and build our future.
