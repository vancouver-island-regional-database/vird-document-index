] \ BOARD OF EDUCATION SCHOOL DISTRICT 68 (NANAIMO-LADYSMITH) Today’s Learner – Tomorrow’s Future Office of the Board Chair 395 Wakesiah Avenue Nanaimo, BC V9R 3K6 Telephone 250-741-5238 Fax 250-741-5309 From the Office of the Secretary-Treasurer From January 30, 2013 The Honourable Don McRae, Minister of Education Parliament Buildings Victoria, B.C. Dear Minister, RE: A Poverty Reduction Program for British Columbia As you know, child poverty continues to be the source of great concern for all British Columbians. In our District, stakeholders have raised the issue many times, expressing great concern that many of our students arrive at their school unfed, poorly clothed and, in some cases, in distress. I am sure you would agree, as a former teacher, that this situation should not continue. In addition, some parts of our district receive children in Kindergarten who are not well-prepared for schooling. A review of the Early Development Indicators shows that due to deficiencies in language development, lack of self-regulation and the impact of continued poverty or neglect these children need extra help to reach the most basic educational starting points. It is our belief that British Columbia can do better for our children and families. Six provinces in the country have instituted Poverty Reduction Programs to ensure that more citizens are provided with the means to support themselves and their families. These programs have already been effective in having an impact on the amount of poverty in their province. For example, Newfoundland and Labrador has reduced the child poverty rate, based on the Low Income Cut Off from 19% in 1998 to 6.5% in 2007. (3rd lowest in Canada) Quebec, using the Market Based Measure has gone from 15.8% in 2000 to 7.3% in 2007. So, it is clear, that if a province focuses on the matter of poverty, it can change the situation for the poorest of its citizens. The poor need not always be among us. There are some key elements to a poverty reduction plan: • • • • They have been advanced with all-party support. They have specific targets and timelines, which in most cases are embedded in legislation. They are comprehensive (meaning they address not just income – both social assistance and other government income supports, as well as measures to boost labour market income – but also the social infrastructure on which low and modest income families depend, such as housing, education, child care and community health care; and they have specific measures to address poverty among those populations where poverty is most acute, such as Aboriginal people, recent immigrants, people with disabilities and mental illness and single parents). They have accountability mechanisms, such as public consultations, funding for independent monitoring groups, and obligations to report annually on progress. A key need is for benchmarks that are frequent enough that a government can be held accountable • within the life of each mandate. There is a cross-ministry secretariat to coordinate and integrate plans, and to ensure that policies do not act at cross-purposes. (Source: CCPA, May 21, 2010) We strongly believe that if your government initiates such a program, it will improve the well-being of our less fortunate citizens, but also assist schools and educators in fulfilling their most important mandate, to provide participating and effective citizens for the future. Finally, we have endorsed the proposal for a $10 per day child care program. This program would provide affordable child care for British Columbians who are currently unable to enter the work force due to the high cost of child care and, in some cases, the unavailability of suitable child care places. We look forward to working with you to improve the lives of the children we serve. CUPE President Pc: Board Chair Trustees D. Hutchinson, Superintendent P. Turin, Secretary-Treasurer J.
