That was done in a hybrid model – which meant that the teacher had a number of enrolled students likely less than 30 students, and would deliver that course in a traditional delivery model. That teacher would also provide support for a small number of distributed learning students in that room, completing a variety of courses, but within the scope of that teacher’s qualified areas.  The cafeteria program was populated with 4 blocks of Grades 10-12 splits – students often took a number of cafeteria courses (10,11,12)  PE offered a full complement of courses, including girls’ fitness, co-ed fitness and both a girls basketball course and a boys basketball course. Each of those courses were offered as 9-12, but would be filled by Grades 10-12, mostly 11-12, but it gave the ability to “overflow” capable students from other grades and not have to provide additional sections at younger grades.  The array of senior electives were able to run by the grace of a few primary conditions: o Split classes were common o A very diverse and willing teaching staff that were prepared to offer creative modes of delivery o A belief that students would carry full and diverse timetables, with few (if any) study blocks or spares being offered o The school did not offer a traditional alternate or resource program, which availed more blocks to the timetable for regular programming  There was a common question of sustainability, as staffing became more complex with class size limits, funding limits, the itinerant nature of newer teachers, etc. Staff cannot provide empirical data to support that, only anecdotal evidence that it was becoming more difficult to offer a complete timetable with staffing and funding conditions changing regularly.
