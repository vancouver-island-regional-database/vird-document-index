School board chairman Jamie Brennan said Wellington's upgrades include plans to tear down and completely replace a block of the school where the labs are currently located and will allow for some new lab equipment to be purchased. But he said there isn't enough money in the budget for the school's seismic upgrade, which was announced by Education Minister Don McRae last week, for the entire school to be upgraded with state-of-the-art equipment and facilities. A brand new $50-million state-of-the-art high school was to be built in the Nanaimo-Ladysmith school district as part of a facilities plan that was approved by the Ministry of Education in 2008. As part of ongoing efforts to reduce excess space in the district due to declining enrolment, the plan called for the closure of both the aging Nanaimo District and Woodlands secondary schools in 2012, and the construction of the new high school on the Woodlands site that would have been up and running this year. The plan also included the much-needed seismic upgrades at Wellington school, but the old school board rejected the plan in 2009. The upgrades at Wellington were only resurrected from the ashes of that plan after a ministry-sponsored seismic survey of all the province's schools determined it to be a high safety risk in the event of a major earthquake. But Brennan said it's likely many aspects of the old plan will soon be back on the table, including the possible closure of NDSS and Woodlands that could be replaced with one new school, as well as the closure of many smaller schools in the district. Dr. Doug Player, a former superintendent of the West Vancouver school district, was hired by the district last month to coordinate the process in which the school board is expected to make a number of longanticipated decisions regarding its schools and other facilities later this year. The district has recently completed reports on its French Immersion program, transportation services and its overall strategic plan to assist in the decision-making process regarding facilities renewal. "I expect his recommendations will be pretty far-reaching and give us plenty of options to choose from," Brennan said. It will involve students as active participants in creating, communicating and implementing, innovative solutions. Students will be introduced to the principles, concepts, and methodologies required for understanding complex inter-relationships of the natural world. Students will participate in a variety of projects and activities designed to promote a sense of stewardship for both constructed and natural ecosystems. These goals fall under the BC Ministry of Education’s framework of Environmental Learning and Experience (ELE), and its "Sustainability Course Content: A Curriculum Framework" document (November 5, 2012). Rationale: The units are based upon ideas in the "Sustainability Course Content; A Curriculum Framework" document (November 5, 2012) provided by the BC Ministry of Education for use in developing board authorized courses. Environmental Stewardship 11 is designed to facilitate learning of the outcomes outlined in this document. The design of this course and its units are intended to provide students with a chance to personalize learning st and develop 21 century skills such as: collaboration, critical thinking, ethical citizenship, communication, creativity and innovation, use of computers and digital technologies, through inquiry while exploring issues of environmental relevance. A solid understanding of the concept of environmental sustainability and stewardship is crucial if students are to take on proactive roles in these areas. There shall be a minimum of 60 days between the acceptance for consideration of the Report and the Board deliberation and decision on the Second Report. 6.
