J.Praud Impact of the Proposed Move on the Learning Needs of Francophone and French Immersion Students As a member of the APÉ (Association des parents d’élèves) from École Océane, Nanaimo’s Francophone elementary school, and a parent of two Francophone children, aged 9 and 12, I am extremely concerned about the proposed relocation of the French program offered at Nanaimo District Secondary School (NDSS) since 1988 and relocating it to John Barsby Secondary School. The proposed relocation of the program will immediately and directly impact over 400 French Immersion AND Francophone students. As of September 2015, 348 French Immersion students AND 40 Francophone students, or 388 students, are enrolled at NDSS in grades 8-12. If the 58 students currently in grade 12 are set aside, the total number of NDSS students affected by the move is 330. As of September 2015, there are 135 grade 7 French Immersion and Francophone students enrolled at Hammond Bay (42), Pauline Haarer (22), North Oyster (13), Quarterway (45) and École Océane (13). So, this means that 465 French Immersion and Francophone students (grades 7-11) and their parents would be immediately and directly affected by the proposed move. Even with a 20% attrition rate for the grade 7 students, resulting in 27 students less, the total number of students affected would be 438, so well over 400. (The 2015-2016 figures were provided by the schools mentioned above). In the medium- and long-term, the move will impact the 1084 Francophone and French Immersion children enrolled in grades K-6 and their parents. J.Praud As parents repeatedly asserted during the October 27, 2015 public consultation, uprooting the Francophone and French Immersion program from NDSS will likely result in a sharp drop in grades 8-12 Francophone and French Immersion enrollment and consequently a sharp drop in the federal funding that the School District receives for Francophone and French Immersion students ($202,584 for this year, BC Ministry of Education). Indeed, faced with the prospect of enrolling their children in a program that has just been uprooted and relocated and thus may not be readily able to meet the immediate learning needs of students and also the prospect of their children spending much longer periods of time commuting, many parents will simply withdraw their children from the Francophone and French Immersion secondary (and very possibly elementary) programs and look at other options, including private schools, homeschooling, and out-of-district schooling. This is a very important point to keep in mind, since as indicated in several documents related to the Facilities Plan Update, overall secondary enrollment in School District 68 has been declining for the past 10 years. However, while secondary enrollment has been on a downward trend, enrollment in French Immersion and Francophone elementary and secondary schools has been steadily increasing. As clearly stated at the October 27, 2015 public consultation, the priority of the District and the Board is the learning needs of students. Yet, at this point in time, the case has not been made that such a drastic and potentially very disruptive move would actually enhance the learning needs of 438 Francophone and French Immersion students. J.Praud Francophone and French Immersion students are hardworking, brave and curious young people who have taken the immensely difficult challenge of mastering Canada’s two official languages. Students and their parents need the Board and the District to take steps to ensure that the Francophone and French Immersion program continues to attract students and thrive. Francophone and French Immersion students and their parents do not need the Board and the District to take steps that will make it much harder, if not impossible, for students to continue their education in the French language. Keep Francophone and French Immersion students at NDSS!
