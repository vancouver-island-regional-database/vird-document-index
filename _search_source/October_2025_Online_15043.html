COMMUNITY CENTRE CALENDAR COMMUNITY EVENTS ANNUAL AUDIT MEETING WILL BE RESCHEDULED Dear Community Members, Chief and Council would like to remind everyone that community meetings and events are open to all members of Stz’uminus First Nation who attend with respect for one another. These gatherings are meant to strengthen our community and create space for open dialogue, sharing, and collective decision-making. Bullying, harassment, or threatening behavior towards any individual—whether staff, leadership, or community members—will not be tolerated. Such actions undermine the safety and well-being of our Nation and go against the values we uphold. On September 17, 2025, the Annual Audit Meeting was cancelled due to a staff member being threatened by a community member. This behavior is unacceptable. Going forward, anyone who engages in threats, harassment, or bullying toward another person will not be permitted to attend future community events or meetings. We encourage all members to come together with kindness, respect, and a commitment to building a stronger Stz’uminus First Nation. Together, we can ensure that our gatherings remain safe, inclusive, and meaningful spaces for all. Thank you for your understanding and cooperation. In Unity, Chief and Council Stz’uminus First Nation 04 COMMUNITY NOTICE FIRE BAN IN EFFECT Stz’uminus First Nation reminds all community members that a fire ban is currently in place. Our lands are surrounded by heavily wooded areas, and the current dry conditions mean the risk of wildfire is extremely high. Even small fires can spread quickly and put our homes, families, and the land at risk. We ask all community members to please stop having any fires until the fire ban is lifted. This includes backyard fires, campfires, smokers and any open burning. Your cooperation is essential in protecting our community and the safety of everyone living here. Thank you for respecting this important precaution. Chief and Council Stz’uminus First Nation 05 COMMUNITY UPDATE LONGHOUSE & COMMUNITY BUILDING Dear Community Members, It is with great pride that we now gather in our newly renovated Longhouse and Community Building— together representing an investment of more than four million dollars into the future of our people. These spaces are at the heart of our community, built for cultural, social, and family gatherings, and it is our responsibility to ensure they are well cared for so that future generations can enjoy them as we do today. The original Longhouse was completed in 1970 and served us faithfully until it closed in 2021 for muchneeded renovations. After years of work, we are excited that both buildings are now complete and available for community use. To preserve and protect these important spaces, a committee of Longhouse members has been formed to oversee their day-to-day use. As part of this process, all bookings for the Longhouse or Community Building will now require a security deposit. This deposit is important because it: • • • Secures your date (must be paid at least two weeks prior to your event). Covers propane costs to keep the buildings comfortable for use. Supports cleanup costs, should workers need to be hired following an event. This security deposit ensures that we can maintain our facilities with respect, fairness, and care— keeping them clean, safe, and ready for everyone’s use. All bookings are to be arranged with Committee Member Gertrude Seymour, and the security deposit (cash only) is to be paid directly to her. A receipt will be issued upon payment. Thank you for your understanding and cooperation as we work together to protect and preserve these special spaces for our community. Sincerely, Stz’uminus Longhouse Committee Gertrude Seymour 12784 Kulleet Bay Road 06 STZ’UMINUS ELDERS ATTEND 4TH ANNUAL STALEW POW WOW LANGLEY, B.C. — More than 70 Stz’uminus Elders traveled to Langley to take part in the 4th Annual stal̓ ̓ əw Pow Wow, held September 12–14 at the Langley Events Centre. The three-day gathering, organized by the stal̓ ̓ əw Arts & Cultural Society, brought together Indigenous nations and communities to celebrate culture, tradition, and healing. Elders witnessed dancers in regalia fill the arena floor, sharing their medicine through movement, and had the opportunity to enjoy traditional foods while supporting Indigenous artists. “The number four is culturally significant and connected to physical, spiritual, mental, and emotional well-being,” explained stal̓ ̓ əw Arts & Cultural Society manager q̓wat ̓ ̓ica Phyllis Atkins. “Think about the four directions and colours of the medicine wheel and the four stages of life: baby, youth, adult, elder. They are celebrated at the stal̓ ̓ əw Pow Wow, which takes place each year during the transition from summer to fall on the shared lands of the four host First Nation communities: qʼwa:n ̓ t’ən ̓ , q̓i̓c ̓ əy, Máthxwi, and Se’mya’me’.” The event featured traditional songs, dances, regalia, languages, medicines, foods, and artwork. Highlights included the Orange Shirt and Red Dress Specials on September 13, honouring residential school Survivors and Missing and Murdered Indigenous Women, Girls, and Two-Spirit people (MMIWG2S). Juno Award-winning artist Fawn Wood performed both solo and alongside the host drum group Cree Confederation. The stal̓ ̓ əw Arts & Cultural Society, whose name means “big river” in the hən ̓ q̓əmin ̓ əm̓ language, is an Indigenous women-led registered charity dedicated to celebrating and sustaining Indigenous culture. 07 The Stz’uminus Elders expressed gratitude for the opportunity to attend the pow wow and raised their hands to those who supported the trip, including Della, Monica, Anne, Sam, Trevor, Raven, Celestine, Damien, Naomi, Ben, Sandy, Taylor, Melainee, Richard, and Andrea. 08 09 10 11 12 New Teacher-Education Program in Stz’uminus Stz’uminus Education Society is proud to share the launch of a new community-based teachereducation program in partnership with the University of Victoria (UVic). This program is designed to support the training and certification of future Stz’uminus K-12 teachers, while advancing Stz’uminus Education Society’s commitment to Hul’q’umi’num’ language revitalization. The program begins with a Hul’q’umi’num’ language revitalization certificate, which progresses into a diploma and ultimately leads to a Bachelor of Education in Indigenous Language Revitalization. Our first cohort includes 20 First Nations learners, who are taking the first steps toward becoming certified teachers for our community schools. To support this initiative, the Ministry of Post-Secondary Education and Future Skills has provided $1 million in funding for the program. This investment ensures that learners can pursue their teacher training here in the community, removing barriers to access and strengthening Hul’q’umi’num’ in our education system. On September 15, 2025, Stz’uminus Education Society hosted a celebration at the Longhouse to mark this milestone and the $1 million in funding. The event brought together Chief John Elliott, representatives from the Ministry of Post-Secondary Education and Future Skills, the First Nations Education Steering Committee (FNESC), the Indigenous Adult and Higher Learning Association (IAHLA). Together with Stz’uminus elders and community members, we celebrated this new opportunity to grow local teachers and strengthen Hul’q’umi’num’ in education. Having more Stz’uminus teachers in classrooms is vital for ensuring our students see their language, history, and culture reflected in their education every day. This program builds on the vision of strengthening education for our community and ensuring Hul’q’umi’num’ continues to thrive in our schools, community, and future generations. This is a proud moment for Stz’uminus Education Society and a step forward in shaping an education system led by Stz’uminus, for First Nations learners. 13 'Uy' skweyul Vicki Post Secondary Coordinator Uy' skwey'ul. COLUMBIA (BC). Anyone can have cockroaches, as they are attracted to any environment with access to food and water. Where Do German Cockroaches Live in Homes? German cockroaches prefer humid environments with access to food and water. They’re often found in kitchens and bathrooms. They will typically be found in and around appliances, under sinks, dishwashers, medicine cabinets and anywhere food may be kept overnight such as storage closets and pantries. It’s possible these cockroaches will slip into homes on items you carry inside. Signs of an Infestation: • Droppings: small black droppings that look like black pepper or coffee grounds. 19 • Egg Cases: tan and about ¼” long, often found in cabinets, under sinks or near entry points. • Shed skins: As they grow, they molt several times leaving behind exoskeleton. • Roaches Scurrying at Night: Especially near the kitchen or bathroom • Musty Odor: A strong, unpleasant smell that intensifies with a growing population Three steps to help rid your home of cockroaches: 1. Starve them out: Cockroaches need food to survive. If they can’t find food, they leave. Clean up crumbs and spills promptly. Keep all food in sealed containers (no open bags or boxes of food). Do not leave food or water out overnight. 2. Dry them out: Cockroaches can’t live without water. Get rid of any standing water. Fix leaky faucets and water pipes. 3. Keep them out: Stop cockroaches for getting in your house. Inspect secondhand furniture for signs of infestation, watch for hitchhikers on guests bags/material. Contact the Housing Department if you believe you have pests in your home. 250 245 7155 ext282 There are steps that you will need to take before treatment occurs. They are as follows: • Clear clutter from the floor, dressers, counters and appliances • Ensure all baseboards are exposed (pull furniture one foot from wall) • Scrub stovetops including under the burners, inside oven and oven drawer • Clean range hood and behind the stove if possible; cockroaches are really attracted to grease and food bits • Ensure all clothing is folded, put away in sealed bins or dressers • Keep all food in sealed containers (no open bags or boxes of food) • Ensure furniture is raised up off the ﬂoor on legs or strategically placed blocks • Only eat in one designated area of the home (kitchen or dining room), to prevent spread of food sources for cockroaches • Remove any cockroach carcasses, as they are a food source for the living cockroaches 20 HALLOWEEN SAFETY TIPS Here are some clear and practical Halloween safety tips: • Costume Safety • • Choose bright or reflective costumes so kids are easier to see in the dark. • Remind kids not to eat any candy until an adult has checked it. • Use face paint instead of masks when possible (masks can block vision). • Watch for choking hazards with younger children. • Make sure costumes fit properly to avoid tripping. • If your child has allergies, double-check ingredient labels. Wood heat is reliable and comforting, but it’s important to keep safety in mind when using your stove. A little preparation and care can help prevent accidents, fires, and health risks. Here are some helpful tips to keep your family safe this season. 3. Maintain Proper Airflow 1. Inspect and Clean Your Chimney 4. Keep a Safe Zone Around Your Stove • • • • Creosote buildup is one of the leading causes of chimney fires. Have your chimney inspected and cleaned at least once a year. Look for cracks, blockages, or nests before your first fire of the season. 2. Burn Only Dry, Seasoned Wood • • • Wet or “green” wood creates more smoke and creosote. Seasoned wood burns hotter and cleaner, reducing buildup in your chimney. Avoid burning painted or treated wood, as it can release toxic fumes. • • • • • Always use the air vents properly to ensure a steady flame and good draft. Don’t overload the stove with wood—this can cause overheating. Make sure your stove’s door seals are tight to prevent smoke leaks. Maintain at least three feet of clearance between the stove and anything flammable (furniture, curtains, kindling). Use a fire-resistant hearth pad under and around the stove. Keep children and pets at a safe distance. 5. Install Smoke and Carbon Monoxide Detectors • • • Place detectors on every level of your home and test them monthly. Replace batteries at the start of the heating season. Carbon monoxide is colorless and odorless— 22 detectors are your only warning system. 6. Always Supervise Your Fire • • • Never leave the stove burning unattended overnight or when you leave the house. Use proper tools to handle hot logs or ashes. Keep a fire extinguisher nearby, just in case. 7. Dispose of Ashes Safely • • • Allow ashes to cool completely before handling. Store them in a metal container with a lid, away from the house. Never use paper bags or plastic containers for ash disposal. Final Thought Woodstoves can bring warmth, comfort, and tradition into our homes during the fall and winter months. By following these safety tips, you’ll protect your home and family while enjoying the cozy heat of your fire. Stay warm, stay safe, and enjoy the season! MORE FALL MAINTENANCE TIPS Baseboard Heaters need cleaning. Shut off heaters and let them cool before cleaning. Check for missing or damaged prongs and ensure nothing is stuck inside the heater. If you find damage, please fix or replace the heater. Ensure heaters are kept clear of materials that could cause fire. Clean your Woodstove Chimney before lighting your stove. Your woodstove and chimney should be cleaned and inspected at least twice per year. This will ensure it is safe for you and your family. Yard Maintenance - Deter Rats and Mice! o Do not pile firewood against your house, it is the perfect place for rats and mice to hide. o Mow your lawn regularly…long grass can attract rats and mice and give them a place to hide. o Remove all blackberry bushes from the side of the house and away from entrances o Trim any tree branches or shrubs that are touching the house. Outside the house o Clean Gutters and downspouts. o Clean your yard. Keeping your yard clean and tidy is your responsibility. Not only does it make your house and neighborhood look better, it also helps deter pests like rats and mice. Unfortunately, dogs and wild animals can’t resist the temptation of a garbage bag filled with garbage. So, in order to avoid an unsightly mess, please ensure your garbage bags are set out in a container or bin on garbage day. We appreciate everyone’s cooperation in this matter. 23 Staying Active & Healthy DURING THE COLDER FALL SEASON As the days grow shorter and the temperatures drop, it can be tempting to stay indoors and hibernate. But fall is actually the perfect time to focus on health and wellness! With cooler air, beautiful autumn scenery, and plenty of seasonal foods, there are many ways to keep both body and mind strong. Here are some simple tips to help you stay active and healthy this season. 1. Embrace Outdoor Activities Take advantage of the crisp air by going for walks, hikes, or bike rides. Raking leaves or doing yard work counts as exercise too! Try setting a goal to get outside for at least 30 minutes a day. 2. Layer Up and Stay Comfortable Dress in light, breathable layers so you can adjust as you warm up. Don’t forget gloves, a hat, and reflective gear if you’re out in the early mornings or evenings. Staying comfortable makes it easier to keep up your routine. 3. Make Movement Part of Your Routine If it’s too cold or wet outside, try at-home workouts like yoga, stretching, or bodyweight exercises. Take the stairs, stretch during TV time, or put on music and dance in the living room. Consistency is more important than intensity. 4. Nourish Your Body with Seasonal Foods Fall brings an abundance of nutritious foods like squash, apples, carrots, and pumpkins. Warm soups and stews can be packed with vitamins and keep you feeling full. Stay hydrated—even when it’s cold, your body still needs plenty of water. 5. Boost Your Immune System Get plenty of sleep to help your body recover and stay strong. Wash hands often, especially during cold and flu season. Consider adding immune-boosting habits like herbal teas, vitamin-rich foods, and daily outdoor time. 6. Take Care of Your Mental Health The shorter days can sometimes affect mood and energy levels. Make time for activities that bring joy—reading, crafting, or connecting with loved ones. Stay social! Join community events, workshops, or exercise groups to stay connected. Final Thought Fall is a season of change—and a great reminder to focus on balance and well-being. By moving your body, eating nourishing foods, and caring for your mental health, you can enjoy everything this season has to offer while staying strong and energized. 24 Indian Dance LIST 2025/26 25
