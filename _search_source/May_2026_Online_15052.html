CHIEF ELLIOTT RE-ELECTED IN 2026 SFN ELECTION Stz’uminus First Nation has re-elected Chief John Elliott in the 2026 election, securing a decisive victory with 251 votes. Elliott defeated challengers Tammie Myles, who received 116 votes, Margaret Seymour with 59 votes, and George Seymour Sr., who received six votes. The newly elected council will include both returning and first-time members. Councillors Greg Seymour, Herb Seymour, Cathy Harris, Leona Smith, and Terry Sampson were re-elected to their positions. They will be joined by newly elected councillors Dale Seymour, Terry Seymour, Marvin Seymour, and Cecelia Elliott. In a statement following the election, Chief Elliott emphasized the importance of unity and progress for the Nation. “We must work collaboratively as a team and make decisions that truly serve our people,” he said. “I’m optimistic about this term and confident in this leadership group. At its core, the day is about visibility, remembrance, and accountability. The symbol of the red dress comes from the REDress Project by Métis artist Jaime Black. Empty red dresses displayed in public spaces represent the absence of those who are missing or have been taken, while also making the issue impossible to ignore. The color red was chosen in part because, in many Indigenous cultures, it is believed to be the only color spirits can see—calling the spirits of loved ones back and honoring them. Red Dress Day matters for several key reasons: It honors lives lost and supports families Thousands of Indigenous women, girls, and Two-Spirit people have gone missing or been murdered in Canada. The day creates space to remember them, speak their names, and acknowledge the grief carried by families and communities. It raises awareness of systemic issues The crisis is rooted in broader issues such as colonialism, racism, poverty, and gaps in policing and justice systems. Red Dress Day helps educate the public about these realities and challenges misconceptions. It calls for action and accountability Following the National Inquiry into Missing and Murdered Indigenous Women and Girls, there were 231 Calls for Justice directed at governments, institutions, and Canadians. Red Dress Day serves as a reminder that meaningful change is still needed and that these calls must be implemented. It creates space for community healing and solidarity Events like walks, ceremonies, and gatherings allow communities to come together, support one another, and strengthen cultural connections while honoring those affected. It amplifies Indigenous voices Red Dress Day centers Indigenous leadership, storytelling, and advocacy, ensuring that the issue is addressed with respect, truth, and cultural understanding. 19 In
