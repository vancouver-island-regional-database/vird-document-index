Appendix A 2023-24 District Program Report for: • • • • • Learning Alternative Career Technical Center District Skills for Life Island Connect Ed Inclusion Outreach Team (Tier 3 Program) 1 Learning Alternatives: Learning Alternatives is a dynamic blend of forward-thinking education and personalized programing. Focused on creating individualized academic plans that focus on the students’ strengths and use innovative strategies to help them progress in areas that need improvement. Program staff includes creative teachers, supportive education assistants, caring child youth and family support workers, and a dedicated administration team. The goal of the program is to help students achieve personal successes and guide them in becoming productive members of the community. Authentic and meaningful experiences that connect students to the natural world is a key focus, as well as inspiring sustainable values and behaviours. The Circle of Courage is woven throughout all the Learning Alternative programs as this model of youth development is based on the principles of belonging, mastery, independence, and generosity. Senior Learning Alternatives (Approximately 84 students) Senior Learning Alternatives is a grade 8-12 program that inspires students to create a vision for their future while teachers offer supports to help translate that vison into reality. At the core the program believes in building trusting student-teacher relationships that are built on mutual respect. We value diversity and positive self-expression. A variety of academic and elective course options are offered within a flexible but structured timetable. Work experience and volunteer opportunities are highly encouraged to help students reach their full potential. All programs provide elective credits that are used towards graduation, additionally, some courses are dual credit which are courses taken at high school but are also accredited to a postsecondary institution. The following are programs offered and supported by the CTC. Train in Trades (78 students will participate in this program for 23/24) Students in their grade 12 year leave their home school to join the CTC Program. CTC staff coordinate the student’s enrolment into a VIU trades or certificate program and provide them with support through to completion. Examples of Trades/Certificates programs include Carpentry, Auto Mechanics, Office Admin, etc. Durations vary in length, but upon completion students have fulfilled the foundational portion of an Apprenticeship or Certification Program. Ministry provides per course funding. SkilledTradesBC provides $1,800 for each student enrolled in the Program. Trade Sampler (56 students will participate in this program for 23/24) Students in their grade 10, 11, 12 years leave their home school for one semester and sample various trades. Students are still attached to and supported by their home school but are ‘cross enrolled’ with the CTC Program, accessing their supports for the one semester. The CTC Program facilitates four cohorts per year which include two that are Co-Ed, one specifically for Women and one for Indigenous Learners. Ministry provides per course funding for each of the 4 trade samplers. SkilledTradesBC provides $250 for each student enrolled in the Program. Work in Trades (35 students will participate in 23/24, however the program can accommodate upwards of 75) Student as young as grade 10 who are working in a job that is classified as a Trade can sign up for the Work in Trades program. Upon application facilitated by the CTC Program, the student’s personal job is registered under the Work in Trade Program, and students then begin to incur hours towards completion of an apprenticeship. 6 There is no course work involved, the hours they are scheduled to work at their job, either after school or on weekends, generates hours towards their Trade. However, they are accredited up to 4 courses for program completion under this program. Also, upon attaining 900 hours students qualify for a $1,000 scholarship to be used to cover schooling costs. Students do not leave their home school; they remain attached and continue to complete their regular course load as well as have the 4 courses for Work in Trades (WIT) applied to their transcript for graduation. Ministry provides per course funding for their regular high school courses and up to 4 for WIT. SkilledTradesBC provides a flat amount per enrolment targets (Max of $40,000 for 35 students) Dual Credit Enrichment (No limit) Students in their grade 12 year are able to take up to two University courses at VIU while also taking a minimum of two courses at their home school each semester. While the students continue to be supported by their home school, course completions earn them credit towards graduation. When students attend and successfully complete courses at VIU, they earn credit towards their university transcript, as well as their high school graduation diploma. CTC staff assist students with application to this dual credit enrichment opportunity as well as act as their district liaison while they attend VIU. Ministry will fund up to a max of 4 courses per student for courses taken at VIU, the remainder of course funding would be attributed to the courses taken at a student’s home school. Trades Trailer – Each year the Trades Trailer visits nine elementary schools, focused on students in Grades 5-7, giving them opportunity to view, handle and use a selection of Trade tools within a supervised environment. The district receives a $4,500 grant from SkilledTradesBC which allows for the continued replacement of supplies, tools and equipment used during the educational overview. Health Dual Credit Grant – The $50,000 grant is meant to provide the district with the resources to advertise, promote, and facilitate student interest and entry into dual credit opportunities in the Health Care Field, either in-person in an afterschool setting, or online. The Program provide learners with disabilities and diverse abilities an opportunity to develop the skills and confidence needed for functional living. The program provides a multitude of opportunities for learners to build as much independence as possible, and to be active members in the school and broader community. Life Skills is for learners who: • • • • • Are complex in multiple functional skills domains Require highly individualized support with functional skills Require 1:1 support from a support person at all times May require augmentative or alternative communication support Have complex behaviours or other needs which cannot be safely met in a neighbourhood school The decision for a student to attend the Life Skills program is made collaboratively between the parent, the team at the neighborhood school and the Director of Instruction – Inclusive Education. As schools work to be as inclusive and supportive as possible, children will not be directly enrolled in the Life Skills Program unless it has been determined that they require an exceptional level of support that their neighborhood school is unable to provide. Thus, it is rare for a student to enroll directly into the program. Families are asked to register with their neighborhood school first. To initiate a referral, the school Principal from the neighbourhood school will schedule a meeting to discuss the potential placement of the learner in the Life Skills program. The two District Life Skills Programs, located at Georgia Avenue Elementary and Nanaimo District Secondary School, provide registered students with support under two broader components which includes: • • Basic Living Skills – skills required to safely navigate home and community settings such as dressing, grooming, toileting, bathing, and health, safety, and first aid. School Skills – includes readying the student for general classroom mechanics, routines and expectations, meals at school, and social interactions. Cost for the Programs are somewhat fixed as the staffing is 1:1 and enrolment at each site is to a maximum of 12, however, general wage increases, and cost of district paid benefits increase costs year over year. 10 11 Island Connect Ed: Island ConnectEd K-12 is a distributed learning school which provides students with a flexible, online learning environment where they are able to complete courses from a distance as well as access inperson supports at the school location. This program is an educational option for those students who may struggle with learning in a regular classroom setting, as well as students who wish to take courses in addition to the scheduled timetable available at their enrolling high school. As well, community adults who have not yet graduated but are working towards their dogwood diploma may take courses that lead to graduation, under Ministry of Education and Child Care guidelines. Student enrolled with Island Connect Ed work towards the same curricular and core competencies as those in traditional schools, however, the distributed learning environment requires a partnership between students, teachers and parents where a continued cycle of participation, feedback and assessment ensures the students successfully navigate and complete the required course work. At Island Connect Ed, “connections” are a fundamental component of student success. Connection to a particular course, a teacher or learning assistant, another student, or an arranged activity ensures there is a positive link between the student and the school; staff at Island Connect Ed are focused on these connections in order to enhance student outcomes. Funding for Island Connect Ed comes from the Ministry of Education and Child Care via the Operating Grant, under Distributed Learning per pupil rates. These rates are marginally lower than traditional schools based per pupil rates due to the online (distributed/distant) learning platform where less staffing is required to support this type of educational delivery. Students in Kindergarten to Grade 9 are funded at 1.0 FTE, whereas students in grades 10 to 12 are funded for the number of courses taken at Island Connect Ed during the school year. Students may be cross enrolled and taking courses at one of the districts high schools, course funding applicable to a school is directed there to support the associated staffing. 12 Island Connect Ed – Funding, Resource and Financial Summary Important to note that many students who take courses with Island Connect Ed are also enrolled and taking courses at their local high school. It is a program aimed at supporting students experiencing long-term school refusal, or who have not returned to school following the global pandemic. The team works in collaboration with school teams and is an extension of the services provided by schools. The goal is to create a continuum of wrap around support for students and families of learners with diverse needs. This includes students who require highly individualized supportive planning due to a specific diagnosis or developmental need, (e.g., students with a diagnosis of autism who are non-verbal and have complex behaviours in the school environment), or students who are experiencing significant barriers in attending school. There are three main areas of focus for this team: supporting students with complex learning needs, supporting students with complex behaviours, and system-wide capacity building and resource development. Services are accessed through referrals from school-based teams. The outreach services provided through this team are available for students in grades K-7, the capacity building and school-based consultive services are provided K-12. In addition to the intervention and capacity building the team provides for individual students, they are also engaged in broader work across the system. Our Inclusion Outreach Coordinators serve as district partners for the provincial outreach programs for autism (POPARD); fetal alcohol syndrome disorder (POPFASD) and inclusion outreach (PIOP). They provide site and student specific Non-violent Crisis Intervention Training in collaboration with the trainers who provide the larger-group opportunities, and they assist with the creation and curation of resources for supporting our learners with diverse abilities and disabilities. They also provide support and capacity building for staff in both of the District Life Skills programs. In 2022/23 the team processed referrals for approximately 192 students, spanning all 28 of our elementary schools, and 4/6 secondary schools. For 2023/24 there are currently 108 students receiving support from the Team. The Inclusion Outreach Team is funded from within the District Learning Services Budget.
