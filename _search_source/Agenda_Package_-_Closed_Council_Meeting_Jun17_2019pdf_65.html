Review of a recently completed legal survey of the property indicates that the retaining wall actually encroaches on the private property in certain areas, (see attachment – green line.) In addition to the actual retaining wall encroachment, the wall was constructed utilizing geogrid which further encroaches into the property (see attachment -- red line.) The retaining wall also essentially eliminates any practical access to the property from Chemainus Road. The entire south-west side of the property fronts the rail corridor with no access. The only potential access point to the property is at the north-west boundary, but the adjoining property is privately owned by the Sandy Beach Strata. ALTERNATIVES: Council can choose to: 1. Page 13 of 31 Mr. Geoff Goodall April 30, 2019 Page 2 At common law, an owner of land adjoining a public highway is entitled to access the highway at any point at which the land actually touches the highway, and for any kind of traffic which is necessary for the reasonable enjoyment of the land. The common law right of access has been modified by statute. In British Columbia, the Community Charter has been interpreted such that a municipality may, by bylaw, prohibit access to certain streets or categories of streets – under the former Municipal Act, municipalities only had the authority to “regulate” access and could not adopt bylaws that prohibited access to any particular street. However, the exercise of the statutory right to prohibit access could give rise to a claim for damages, if the owner suffers “injurious affection” as a result – see discussion below. The Town also has the statutory power under section 32 of the Community Charter to enter onto, take or enter into possession of and use real property and to construct works over real property. However, section 33(1) provides that if a municipality expropriates real property, compensation is payable to the owner. Section 33(2) of the Community Charter states that if a municipality exercises a power to enter onto and use property that does not constitute an expropriation, or injuriously affects property by the exercise of any of its powers, it must pay compensation for any loss or damages caused by the exercise of the power. The law of “injurious affection” was recently clarified by the Supreme Court of Canada in Antrim Truck Centre Ltd. v Ontario (Transportation) 2013 SCC 13. The statutory right to compensation for “injurious affection” is contained in many pieces of legislation authorizing the construction of public works because otherwise, public authorities such as local governments that construct works which create or cause a nuisance to property owners could avoid compensation by relying on the defence of statutory authority. In the Antrim case, the owner of a truck stop claimed damages for injurious affection when the Province’s construction of a parallel highway/bypass made access to the truck stop very difficult. The Court stated that compensation for injurious affection is payable where the activities complained of interfere with a person’s land in a way that is both substantial and unreasonable. The burden to prove a substantial interference is relatively low. Once the plaintiff has proved that, the court is required to consider whether the interference is unreasonable, taking into account both the public benefit of the project and the degree of harm. The question in any particular case is whether the individual owner has been required to bear a greater degree of the burden of construction than it would be reasonable to expect individuals to bear without compensation. I expect that at the time the retaining wall was constructed, the Town was likely concerned about the stability of the adjoining slope and the potential damage that could be done to the highway and to passing motorists. Still, the effect of the construction of the wall seems to have been to completely preclude any chance of a highway access to the Property being constructed. While the Property may have been a challenging lot to develop all along, I expect that a court would be sympathetic to the position of the owner in this case. Additionally, and quite apart from the fact that access to the highway has now been closed off in any practical or real sense, a portion of the Town’s works were constructed on the owner’s property, apparently without any formal permission having been obtained from the owner. At Page 14 of 31 Mr. Geoff Goodall April 30, 2019 Page 3 common law this would be considered a form of trespass that would entitle the owner to damages, if not an injunction requiring the trespasser to remove its works from the owner’s land. However, the Town has the statutory authority to enter onto, take possession of and construct works upon a private property owner’s land. While it is unlikely the owner could obtain an injunction requiring removal of the lock-block wall, the Town could be required to pay fair market value for the land taken for purposes of the lock-block wall and, potentially, damages for injurious affection as a result of any negative impact on the remainder of the land. At a minimum, the Town should give consideration to negotiating or (if needed) taking by expropriation a right of way over the portion of the Property where the lock-block wall has been built. If the owner refuses to voluntarily grant a right of way or dedicate a portion of the land for highway purposes, the Town could consider expropriation. If the Property has become unbuildable because of the construction of the lock-block wall, the owner would likely be entitled to damages, perhaps approaching the fair market value of the Property. If that is the case, the Town may need to consider offering to purchase or expropriating the whole of the Property. I suggest that before entering into negotiations with the owner, the Town consider having a formal appraisal done so that it knows, with reasonable certainty, the range of compensation that the owner could be expected to claim in this case. Based on the results of the analysis, the slope is potentially unstable, in terms of applicable regulation and guidelines, to within 11 m of the cut slope above the lock block wall. However, as slope stability modeling software computes factors of safety for discrete surfaces within a slope, precise placement of the crest of potential failures is practically inefficient. For this reason, recommended setback is necessarily behind the limits of potential slope failure. Recommended set back distance is made with the assumption that standard residential foundations and construction practices will be used. Reduction in set back could be realized through construction of engineered retaining walls, use of deep foundations, or other appropriate methods. Analysis of slope stability took place at Sta. 0+490, the location of the tallest road-cut. It is assumed that setbacks from this section of slope represent the maximum slope setback. Therefore, reduced recommended setbacks could be realized in areas where the height of the road-cut is less. However, based on inspection of figures provided by the Town and the site reconnaissance, the Property narrows as the road-cut height decreases. Therefore, construction in areas of lower road-cut height may not equate to wider buildable area. 3 Page 22 of 31 350 CHEMAINUS ROAD – SLOPE STABILITY FILE: 704-ENG.VGEO03605-01 | MAY 17, 2019 | ISSUED FOR REVIEW Potential slope failure surfaces were identified beneath the level of the lock-block wall foundation.
