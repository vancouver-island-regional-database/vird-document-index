Multicultural and Race Relations Committee Report to Nanaimo/Ladysmith Board (SD 68) November, 2013 Scott Saywell joined our committee in February 2013 as our Senior Management Representative. Early in the 2012/2013 term the chair met with student groups from four high schools to discuss their thoughts and input re our desire to challenge racism. The groups were organized by each school’s administration. Not long after, we met with the superintendent and agreed that we should be a governance board so these meetings did not continue. The previous committee had organized a poster contest to promote positive multicultural and race relations in all Nanaimo/Ladysmith schools. During this term we were able to arrange to print, frame, deliver and mount both the winning high school and elementary school posters in all of our schools – Special thanks to Pete Sabo’s people for delivering and mounting the posters. In early July Scott Saywell and Bill Robinson arranged a meeting with approximately 20 principals and vice principals to discuss what is being done to support our Aboriginal students. Notes from that meeting are attached. Over the summer we were able to connect with three strong representatives of our local Aboriginal People. They are Joan Brown, Chris Beaton and Erralyn Thomas. They will be working with us during our 2013/2014 term to help our staff develop a Hul’q’umin’um school signage program that we expect will be piloted in several elementary and high schools. Joan, with help from her sister, Mandy Jones, who teaches Hug’q’umin’um in our district, are working on the appropriate language for these signs. They are also going to recommend a selection of Hul’qumi’num short sentences or phrases that teachers could use in their classrooms and others that could be used as part of morning announcements. Our plan is to see a digital version of these words, phrases and sentences provided so school personnel will have the correct pronunciation easily at hand. Our hope is that these efforts will be a first step toward introducing more Hul’q’umin’um language and culture to nonAboriginal students while also making Aboriginal students feel recognized and more welcome in their school. When we discussed the possible usage of signs in other languages represented by many of our students, our committee members, especially those representing the Central Vancouver Island Multicultural Society, felt that this would be overwhelming as there are so many different languages involved. These members also felt that using Hul’q’umin’um signage was an important way to recognize that we are on Traditional Snuneymuwx Territory. Our Aboriginal subcommittee is also envisioning SD68 students producing a digital version of at least one of the Hul’qumin’num children’s books that have recently been translated by Elders and printed. Bob Brooks, Vice Principal at Dover Bay has indicated an interest in helping to find support among his students and teachers to do this work. We feel this would be an exciting program for these students. It would be an opportunity for them to leave a gift that would be used for years by teachers not only in this district but beyond wherever Hul’q’umin’um is the language of local Aboriginal People. Finally we have begun to do some work on planning a Spupitul (a Hul’q’umin’um competition) for SD68 students …. We have been inspired to do this because for the past two years Ladysmith students have won a similar competition organized in Duncan! Before moving forward with any of these proposed projects our plan is to get approval from the Elders group that is currently being developed. Finally our committee met recently with Bob Esliger and Patricia Trick to review our Anti-Racism policy as a Health and Safety issue and to discuss training programs for new and current staff. Both Patricia and Bob committed to explore appropriate actions in this area while recognizing that all senior management is especially busy at this time. Trish will report back for our next meeting. North Oyster We have traditionally included elements of Aboriginal culture as a part of our Christmas and Spring Concerts, and had a celebration for National Aboriginal Day. This coming school year, the staff has decided to expand on the school-wide focus of Aboriginal Culture. • • • • • • • • There will be a different Aboriginal theme each month. Got instance arts, crafts, games, storytelling. Each month students will be put into ‘cross-grade’ groups and will work with a teacher or EA to complete one of these activities. Typically it will be the last Friday of the month. The materials produced will be displayed in the school hallway. During the month of June, students will go to a different teacher/ EA in cross-grade groups each afternoon for the last hour of the day. In the group each teacher will teach the group a lesson related to our themes. The group will rotate to a different teacher each day, so the teacher will only plan one lesson and all the students will have the opportunity to experience it. On June 20th, the school will hold a celebration to acknowledge National Aboriginal Day. We will do this on Friday the 20th, even though the actual date is Saturday June 21st. The celebration will include students going to different stations in the morning. This year the stations were: carving, lacrosse, traditional storytelling, the bone game, snack (fry bread). In the afternoon, this year we had an Aboriginal music group put on a concert in the afternoon. The celebration of National Aboriginal Day will follow a similar agenda in 2014. We have also been connecting the AEEA in our district – connecting to the mind, body, emotion, and spirit – to build academic success & a sense of pride & belonging for our Aboriginal learners. We have developed a school wide culture of multi – age clan groups that meet regularly to learn about Canadian Aboriginal culture, language, perspective, and historical significance. This past year we focused on the Northern Inuit culture, prior years we focused on local First Nations cultures, and next year we will be focusing on the Metis. Our clans are represented by the bear, whale, wolf & eagle – and connect to the traits of belonging, generosity, independence & mastery. We have also developed, through teacher collaboration, our own understandings, and are continuing to invite experts to assist us (thank you to Laura Tait, Sharon Hobenshield, and other district staff). We have been very fortunate to have Elder Jerry Brown. He connects bi-weekly with our students & staff, and teaches drumming, language, and culture. He is regarded highly by all students & they welcome him in Hul’qumi’num. We have also been developing ways to invite our parents & families in – we have an annual school picnic, an October family potluck, and a year-end field trip.
