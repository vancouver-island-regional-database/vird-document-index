SUSTAINABILITY? Thank you for giving me this opportunity to comment on the current updated facilities plan - 'Sustainability'. The Oxford dictionary defines 'sustainability' in two ways that pertain to this exercise; 1) Able to be maintained at a certain rate or level: and 2) Able to be upheld or defended. The need for this consultation is not really about sustainability it is more about maintaining Cedar Secondary School. We know this because it says so on the district's website under FAQ, responding to a question regarding the best use of district funds for the whole district. The response was "This public consultation is directly addressing the Board's motion to re-open CCSS through the proposed closure of schools and the possible relocation of district programs. Is this the best way to become 'sustainable'? The job of a Trustees is first and foremost to enhance student achievement so when the previous Board closed Cedar Secondary is was because the secondary population could not 'sustain' diverse programs for students. According to Mr Blain, student growth was declining and would do so for many years, never recovering to the level it was at. During the consultations at LSS, I felt there were some good options being suggested but when I mentioned that to Mr Blain his response was that management's plan was better. Imagine my surprise when I hear at the regular Board meeting that Mr Blain believes that the numbers in Cedar will rise in 5 years, probably less. The Board is counting on all the Cinnabar students going to CCSS but you haven't even surveyed Cinnabar to find out which school they will choose. There is a possibility that the numbers at CCSS could even be fewer than the predicted 202. How can a catchment area with only 391 K-12 students grow fast enough to make CCSS 'sustainable'? A single K-12 school would not be able to provide a varied course selection for the secondary students. By re-opening Cedar Secondary, you have in fact, relegating Cedar students to second class status because they will not be able to take all the courses they require to graduate in a classroom with a teacher dedicated to teaching that particular course. The Learn at Home program will not solve this problem making equity across the district non-existent. I guess since this facilities update is focused on the central schools, only they deserve diverse course offerings. Is this what 'sustainability' means to you? The other rationale for the facility update is a perceived structural deficit due to the possibility /or probability that funding protection will cease to exist. A good thing to be aware of but this Board currently has a $5M surplus separated in two bundles of $2M unrestricted capital and $3M restricted. Why is the Board not paying their future, estimated structural deficit from the $5M surplus? The Board has the final say in what is restricted or not and can unrestrict funds at any time. Is this not a 'sustainable' solution? Closing a secondary school of approximately 560 students and re-opening a secondary school of 202 student does not make sense and is far from 'sustainable'. Closing an elementary school with a huge playground, a forest as well as specially designed classrooms for children with special abilities also make one wonder at the priorities of this Board. Nine boilers may be a problem but not insurmountable. Changing boundaries and dispersing students to neighbourhood schools that are already full adds further costs to the district budget. If you don't know the full cost of the plan with all its permentations, how can you say the plan is 'sustainable'. Mr Blain claims that the addition to Frank J Ney will be finished in 2017 but he left out the most important component - money and permission from Government to put the plan into place. Where is the Board going to find the matching funds required by government? The sale of property could provide those funds but you have to have the government's permission to access said funds. We have over 2000 empty spaces in our district and although you may close one school doesn’t mean the money bag opens especially when you re-open a secondary school for 202 kids! Making such statements without a government letter granting permision for renos or builds does not make the plan 'sustainable'. Transferring the French Immersion program to John Barsby will certainly fill the school but to what end? Will there be more advanced classes offered so all students have access to classes they need to fulfill their dreams or will some have to resort to Learn at Home which we know does not suit everyone? Enhancing student achievement should be the primary goal but without surveying students around course planning or preparation in anticipation of student wants and needs will not make this plan 'sustainable'. And what about Ladysmith Secondary? Will they be 'sustainable'? The role of the superintendent is to advise you in all matters pertaining to the education of the students held in your trust which also includes facilities. It is also the role of the Superintendent to advise you when you are making poor decisions and inform you what may be the effect on the district if you continue down a poorly chosen road. However, if the Board persist, the superintendent will do as directed knowing he did his due diligence. Staff advises but YOU decide and YOU will be held accountable for those decisions.
