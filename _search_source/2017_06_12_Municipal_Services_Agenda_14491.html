INTRODUCTION/BACKGROUND: This matter has been considered by Council in the past; however, it had not been identified as a priority during strategic planning processes over the past several years. Currently Animal and Poultry Bylaw 1994, No. 1136 prohibits the keeping of animals (i.e. horses, cattle, sheep, goats or swine) or poultry (i.e. chicken, turkey, goose, duck or other fowl) on properties of less than two acres (.81 hectares) in area. If Council wishes to allow the keeping of hens and ducks on smaller residential lots within the Town of Ladysmith, it must amend the bylaw. The Town's legal advisors have in the past recommended that the Town also consider amending the Zoning Bylaw be amended accordingly. Current regulations regarding chickens in the Cowichan Valley and Nanaimo are as follows: City of Duncan • Maxi mum of 6 hens • Zones R1 - Urban Residential (Lot Size: 460m2) and R2 - Suburban Residential (Lot Size: 600m2) • Provisions regarding licensing, enclosures, fencing, odor control, enforcement matters, etc. outlined in bylaw • One complaint registered, relating to rodents. Only two properties have poultry in Duncan. District of North Cowichan • Maximum of 12 poultry on property larger than 1675 m2 (0.41 acres) in area but less than 8000 m2 (1.98 acres) in area • No restriction on roosters City of Nanaimo • Maximum of 6 hens or ducks on lots less than 0.4 hectares (1 acre) in size • Maximum of 4 hens or ducks on lots less than 450m2 (4843.75 ft2}. • Provisions regarding licensing, enclosures, fencing, odor control, enforcement matters, etc. outlined in bylaw • 6 chicken-related complaints in 2017: o 3 of people keeping roosters o 1 hens at large o 1 excess hens o 1 coop too small • 12 chicken related complaints in 2016: o 7 of people keeping roosters o 4 hens at large o 1 excess hens o (4 of these complaints were regarding the same subject) 5 Maximum of 3 hens for the purpose egg harvesting for consumption exclusively by residents the parcel on which the hens are kept, minimum property size acre III Provisions regarding appropriate enclosures, setbacks, etc. outlined Bylaw • Hens must be registered about 6 permits have been There are few half-acre lots in Parksville, so issued • The low number of permits means that very few complaints have been received III Town of Qualicum Beach • Started a 3-year trial on May 1, 2017 for 30 households III Up to six chickens or ducks may be kept on properties in residential zones 1 through 17, provided there are no roosters, cocks, cockerels or peacocks III Structures for the chickens or ducks must be in accordance with required yard setbacks District of Saanich Number of hens allowed depends on size of coop. Each hen is required to have 0.37 m2 of indoor coop space and 0.92 m2 of roofed outdoor enclosure. The maximum coop and enclosure area should be less than 9.2 m2 • Hens are for personal use only and must be registered. No roosters are permitted • Owners must live on the property where they keep the hens • Hens cannot run at large. Hens are to be kept in a building and enclosure in the property's rear yard, at least 3m from the rear property line and 3m from any door or window • Bylaw enforcement staff state that they have received "the odd complaint' related to odour or flies when chicken coops are not properly looked after, or when poultry are not effectively penned • Staff generally are of the opinion that as long as poultry owners follow regulations laid out in the for numbers, care and appropriate housing, problems associated with the keeping of hens in backyards are minimal. ® Concerns related to the keeping of hens in backyards include ability to enforce the bylaw, the potential for attracting rats or other predators to the neighbourhood (including properties that do not have hens); ensuring that standards of care for the poultry are met and enforced. Anecdotally, while there appears to be support for the keeping of hens on residential properties in Ladysmith, there are also citizens who are opposed to the prospect for a variety of reasons. 6 Prior to preparing an amendment to the Animal Control Bylaw, Council should determine its preferences with respect to the number of hens to be permitted, minimum lot size, regulations on the number and type of structures used for housing the hens, setbacks/location criteria for such structures, and whether keeping hens would require a permit. This provides Council with an opportunity to engage in a public consultation process to determine the level of public interest and support prior to proceeding with any amendment to the Animal Control Bylaw. Such consultation can take place online, through PlaceSpeak, through an online survey, and through comments cards or other non-electronic means of seeking public input. The input process could include feedback on possible regulations including but not limited to any restrictions on numbers of birds, size of housing structures or fencing. ALTERNATIVES: In addition to the above recommendation, Council could choose: • To direct staff to prepare an amendment to the Animal Control Bylaw that would permit the keeping of hens in backyards on residential property • Not to pursue the matter any further.
