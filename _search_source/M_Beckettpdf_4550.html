SD68 Budget Presentation Hello everyone, my name is Matt Beckett, I am a Nanaimo resident, tax payer, and father of 2 children at Rutherford school. Today I would like to talk to you about budgeting, specifically about the budget outline presented to us as justification for the closure of Rutherford school. The thing about budgets is that they need to be accurate. The more accurate a budget is, the more confident we can be making decisions based on it. The budgeted projections presented to us for the Rutherford closure has some issues that I would like to take the time to point out. First of all, in the summary of savings on page 21 there is an addition error. Simply by adding the operating savings to the future capital expense savings CORRECTLY, you will realize that the savings listed are overstated by $45000. That's 10% of the first year savings erased due to doing math correctly (Fig 1). Figure 1: Math Secondly, the largest component of the budgeted savings comes from the elimination of teaching positions. 6.12FTE positions for the district overall, 3.8 of those generated by the closure of Rutherford school. It's not clear how those positions are accounted for, or what that would do to class sizes, regardless, it is claimed that we'll save $393,200 by eliminating these 3.8 teachers. Figure 2: Proposed teacher savings That works out to a little over $103,000 per teacher in savings. Is this realistic? My accounting consultant has informed me that 26% is an appropriate number to take into consideration benefits and associated employment costs. Working backwards from $103,000, taking 26% into consideration, leaves us at an $84,000 teaching salary that is expected to be reduced from all 3.8 positions. According to the Nanaimo District Teachers Association the salary range for teachers starts at $44,500 and caps out at $84,300 (Fig 3). Figure 3: Teacher Salary Grid - http://www.ndta68.ca/salary-grid Teaching is a unionized profession. I think it is highly unlikely that we would be saving based on the salaries of our most experienced teachers with the highest seniority. Doing the same calculations using the low end of the pay scale results in savings of only $213k/yr, which is a difference of almost $180k/yr $44594 x 1.26 x 3.8 = $213,516/yr $393,200 - $213,516 = $179,684/yr range The calculation on the median salary reduces the projected savings by $89k $63,615 x 1.26 x 3.8 = $304,589 $393,200 - $304,589 = $88,611/yr Are the assumptions made for the projected savings accurate? I don't think they are. Maintenance savings for Rutherford is the next item that is used to determine future savings, and it is based on prior year expenses. Some of the items included in that are listed as electrical upgrades, a left hand turn lane, paving, and specialty renos. These are one-time costs, not ongoing expenditures, as such, they cannot be considered ongoing savings. Savings need to be based exclusively on future costs. The proposed plan rests on the ability to move students to other schools, with an addition to Frank Ney for an additional capacity of 200. As the building size and occupancy increases, so too will the utilities and maintenance costs. The district has stated that this is difficult to calculate. It is better to budget for a reasonable amount than to ignore it. Extrapolating existing utilities and maintenance based on student number or square footage (Fig 4) seems like a reasonable approach, and that results in an increase expenditure at Frank Ney of $30k - $50k/year. Figure 4: Frank Ney operating expenditures 1100m^2 x 42.42 $/m^2 = $46,662 estimated maintenance/utilities increase Presumably McGirr and Randerson will also see additional expenses due to increased occupancy and the potential need for portables. The cost of moving the Life Skills program, renovating the new location, the administrative cost of closing the school as well as the cost of physical relocation of assets. These are all expenses that offset the savings but appear to be unaccounted for. Finally, this whole plan is predicated on the assumed investment of $5.5M into the expansion of Frank Ney. When I see an investment, I want to see a return on that investment. If the budget as stated is to be believed, and I think I've made some convincing arguments that it shouldn't be, then closing Rutherford would save about half a million per year. The break even point for that investment after the future costs are accounted for is 6.5 years, at which point our enrolment is projected to exceed the new capacity. It doesn't make any sense to spend $5.5M to reduce capacity, only to need that capacity again in a few years time. There is no return on this investment, it is not a good deal for the tax payer. In conclusion, the budgetary outline presented for the closure of Rutherford school is based on some extremely best case scenario numbers, combined with the omission of some foreseeable expenses. I respectfully ask that the trustees confirm the ministry funding is in place before any more time and money is wasted on this, and look at the numbers involved in this plan critically. Pressure the district for sources and reliable data, and make sure the decisions you are making are based on accurate information. Thank you for your time.
