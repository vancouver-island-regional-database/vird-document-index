April 29, 2014. Dear Trustees: In response to the board meeting on April 23, we felt it was important to provide a few points of clarification in response to our presentation and the discussion that happened after. School Construction Committee versus School Transition and Consolidation Committee: At the meeting on Wednesday there was to be a lot of discussion dedicated to what responsibilities each of the various committees in this process hold. In doing so, it was decided that many of the concerns that were brought up in our presentation were actually not the responsibility of the School Construction Committee (SCC), but rather those of the School Transition and Consolidation Committee. The purview of the SCC and its exact responsibilities were discussed at length at the first SCC meeting that parent representatives were invited to. At this meeting the responsibilities of the committee were described and being related to “construction” associated issues, i.e. anything that engineers and architects need to be involved in, the actual physical structure of the building or anything that would require “construction”. In this vein we were told that the SCC was expected to meet all the way until construction began, and perhaps even during construction IF something came up that required a significant departure from the plans that the School Construction Committee had come up with. Alternately, the responsibilities of the School Transition and Consolidation Committee were related to non-construction related issues, such as school culture, the transfer and placement of important school artifacts and the purchase and installation of equipment that happens AFTER construction is complete. We were told that the School Transition and Consolidation committee would begin meeting early in the 2014-15 school year. This dichotomy was described in detail at our meetings because in the first meeting confusion arose over the list of requests and concerns that the parent reps brought to the table. For instance, when we talked about a PAC request to have a new piano since none of the pianos in ANY of the schools in Cedar are currently in good working order, we were told that this was the responsibility of the transition committee. When we talked about the big outcry from parents for smart boards, we were told this is the responsibility of the transition committee. HOWEVER when we mentioned fields, we were told that the maintenance department had been requested to complete an inventory and we would be hearing the results of that inventory for discussion at the meeting. When we talked about lockers and bathroom fixtures we were told that we would be discussing these things in relation to how we decide to design the school with the school principal. This meant that we needed to figure out together what the best layout of the school would be, i.e. would there be primary versus intermediate wings, if so where would they be? This would impact whether and where toilets would be lowered and lockers would be removed. From the above description, it is quite clear that the concerns we brought forward on April 23 were indeed SCC related issues. We are bringing this important definition of the various committees in this process to your attention so that you understand exactly why it is that we made our presentation on Wednesday April 23. It was not because we were confused about what issues we were supposed to be discussing and talking about within the school construction meeting. It was because the EXACT issues that we were supposed to talk about and work together on were ignored and the issues surrounding them were not solved. Quite simply, if it wasn’t the responsibility of the School Construction Committee to discuss and solve CONSTRUCTION related issues, then what was the responsibility of the school construction committee? If the fields and outside play areas were not the responsibilities of the school construction committee then why did we have two presentations from Brian Hackwood, discussing the cost of updating the outside spaces of the school to bring them in line with what the students at Woodbank and NCI currently have? The response by the district staff to kick these issues further down the line to another committee demonstrates their reluctance to actually SOLVE these problems in any concrete way. These issues were brought up by the parent reps at the SCC because they are of great importance and concern to the community in which this school will be located. They are issues that require long term planning and funding in order to be solved. Now, FIVE MONTHS later, these issues are no closer to being solved. Furthermore, by muddying the waters of the school transition and consolidation committee with responsibilities that are supposed to fall under the umbrella of the school construction committee, the district, and your selves by allowing it to happen, have now impacted the ability of the school transition committee to actually address the issues it was designed and created to do. As a result, you will now have two committees that are incapable of completing their assigned and defined responsibilities. Therefore we would like to make it abundantly clear, that the issues brought up by the school construction committee at the board meeting on April 23, most certainly and most definitely fall under the responsibility of the school construction committee. Rather than push these issues further down the line to a committee that already has defined tasks, the board of education should demonstrate the leadership for which it is elected to carry out and direct the district staff to reconvene the school construction committee to finish the job that the School Construction Committee is mandated to do. It is not “too late”: With over $2million in facilities improvement money on the line, the parent representatives of the school construction committee would like to express our utter and complete outrage and disbelief that the elected trustees would rather hide behind the excuses of “because this is what the board has decided” and “it is too late to consider alternatives” then attempt to find a solution to this problem that would better serve not only the students who will attend Cedar Elementary, but the district as a whole. It seems apparent that the Board of Education made the decision to create Cedar Elementary in the renovated Cedar Secondary building without access to all the relevant information. This includes: 1. A complete understanding of the actual cost to convert the high school into a safe, functioning and usable elementary school. Instead of the originally proposed $1.5 million, the current budget has ballooned to over $2.5 million. $2 million to complete the proposed “scaled back renovation” plus another $500 000 to make the play fields usable. What happens if 400 students want to go to this school? How much more money will you have to put into this project? To create the school that you envisioned last June, that would be the “envy of the district, the crown jewel of elementary education in SD68” will actually cost closer to $5 million. 2. A complete understanding of the enrolment trends for the area. The school will now only be home to 250-275 students and Mr. Sabo said that learning services is still trying ascertain just how many students there will on opening day. With only 250-275 students you have more options available to you in terms of where to locate Cedar Elementary that are far more educationally sound and cost effective than the current proposal of deconstructing Cedar Secondary. 3. A complete understanding of the many hazards associated with the relocation of elementary students into a facility previously used as a secondary school. I.e. the traffic safety issues associated with having an elementary school located on two busy roads in a rural area where there are no sidewalks, traffic lights or street lights. The actual state of the fields at Cedar Secondary and the implications of their conditions for elementary use. Attached to this letter (addendum A) you will find correspondence between Woodbank parent representative to the School Construction Committee, Stephanie Higginson and Superintendent Hutchinson between October 21-25, 2013 (you were cc’d on this correspondence). In it, you will see that these issues were raised to district staff well before the April 23, 2014 board meeting. Of note in this correspondence is the projected enrolment figures detailed by both Stephanie and Mr. Hutchinson. It seems the enrolment for Cedar Elementary continues to decline. Rather than listen to the local knowledge available to them through meaningful consultation with parents in the community, the district staff, with your approval, has simply dragged these issues on for months and months. If you continue to ignore these issues, which parents and community members have been attempting to discuss for months, then before too long, you will have indeed created a self-fulfilling prophecy through a lack of action, thus making it in fact “too late” to do the right thing. With the knowledge of this new and relevant information isn’t there a better way to solve this problem than to simply create a new committee and wait for another report? Isn’t it is time for the trustees to do what you were elected to do? That is to lead this district to make the most educationally and economically sound decisions. Continuing to ignore the will of parents, while simultaneously refusing to take a stand on this issue by pushing it down the line to committee after committee is quite simply poor leadership. This is $2 million of facilities money. This is over half of the Annual Facilities Grant available to the district for the next two years. To state that “it is too late” and “because this is what the board has decided” demonstrates a grave lack leadership over an issue that requires the guidance and conviction for which many of you were elected to this board to use. Without your input and leadership on an issue of this magnitude, there might as well not be a board of education at all because as it stands you are simply allowing this district to move forward with a disastrous decision that is not supported by the community who you were elected to represent and will have a negative impact on the academic achievement of students across this district. It is not “too late” to take a second look at the plans for Cedar Elementary. You haven’t spent the $2million yet, you haven’t started the construction yet. What is an enhancement? It is important to touch on the issue of the “enhancements” as it was brought up on Wednesday evening. Trustee Saunders said “I guess these parents don’t see a larger library, a drama stage, a music room, a larger gym as enhancements.” That is simply not true. The parent representatives on the committee and we feel safe saying, the parents in this community, do see these facilities as enhancements. However they were not touched on in our presentation for two reasons. The first is that we only had 10 minutes to present and it was hard enough to get everything into that 10 minutes that we wanted to. If we had more time we could have had a much more inclusive discussion regarding enhancements versus hazards. The second reason they were not brought up is because quite frankly it is hard to see these enhancement when we have to fight so hard for things that we view as basic necessities such as access to year round usable playing fields, safe roads for small children and toilets, vanities, water fountains and sinks that are at age appropriate heights. It is also important to note that all these “enhancements” are on the inside of the building. In this day and age, where we can’t open the newspaper without finding some story about how children are more disconnected from nature than ever before, it is even more important to get our kids outside. The PAC presentation demonstrated the lengths to which we go to in Cedar to ensure this is reflected in our children’s’ education. Add to that the success of the parent led, no cost outdoor kindergarten and you can see why it is we are struggling to celebrate these enhancements. Finally, while Cedar Elementary might be getting a larger library or a big art room or music room, it is not getting extra funding to take advantage of these facilities. Our library will still only be open 1.5 days a week, the art room will remain underutilized unless a teacher on staff has the wherewithal and skill to make something exciting happen in that room. So, to be clear, we are not attempting to relish in the negative or only highlight the undesirable, we are simply attempting to ensure that the most basic elements of elementary education are not forgotten or overlooked by the district and board in THEIR attempt to relish in the enhancements or highlight the desirable aspects of Cedar Elementary. Traffic Safety, Lockers and Foods Rooms: Clearing up the misconceptions At the meeting on Wednesday, there was discussion around the issues of the traffic safety, foods rooms and the lockers remaining in the schools. Since no one at the board table asked the members of the school construction committee why these were important issues to them but did ask Mr. Sabo for further clarification, we felt it was important to explain why we brought these issues up and what our understanding of these issues is. Traffic Safety: We appreciate Trustee Brennan’s attempts to discuss this in more detail, while at the same time making it clear that the school district cannot spend money on any property outside the boundaries of school property, we were discouraged by the response from district staff and the lack of understanding for the depth of this issue by many of the Trustees. This issue was brought up by the parents on the committee very early in the process in order to ensure that we were not playing catch up on opening day of Cedar Elementary. Quite simply, we were attempting to be proactive, rather than reactive, because in this scenario, reactive is not only unacceptable it is just plain dangerous. When we were treated to a series of questions from Trustee Bard, implying that it was the responsibility of the parents in the Cedar and our PAC to contact the Ministry of Transportation we were completely dismayed. We were even more dismayed in learning, that upon a question from a parent in the audience, it turns out that, despite our asking about this issue in November, that the district staff have still not contacted the Ministry of Transportation to address this issue. For the parents on the school construction committee, this is a perfect reflection of how the district staff have treated our concerns. They have heard them and then they have done nothing to act on them and then when pressed on the issue, they push the issue down the line to another committee. This issue is not one that can or should be pushed further down the line, IT NEEDS TO BE SOLVED. Trustee Bard was not the only trustee to demonstrate a lack of understanding for the complexity and importance of this issue. When questioned by the board, the district staff referred to a “safe walk to school program” that has already been deemed inappropriate for Cedar roads due to the lack of sidewalks and street lights. Furthermore, Chairperson Neary made a comment that compared the roads the current elementary schools were on, to the road that Cedar Secondary is on. This showed us that some members of the board of education have made this decision without a comprehensive understanding of the issues at play when trying to turn Cedar Secondary into Cedar Elementary. Quite frankly, the lack of understanding with this particular issue is deeply disturbing to all of us do to the severity of the issue at hand. Foods Rooms: Throughout numerous meetings of the SCC there were numerous discussions about the importance of keeping at least one food prep room for school community programming uses. During these discussions it was also clear that a complete inventory of how all three schools are used for community programming was never completed. We can add this to the list of relevant information not available to trustees when the decision was being made about Cedar Elementary. The original plan showed the foods rooms becoming kindergarten rooms. When we moved from 475 students to 425, it became apparent that we could now keep at least one foods room. There was a lot of discussion in the meetings about these particular rooms and the importance of these rooms to community programming. The new plan shows these rooms being converted to classrooms and the facilities department compromise as it currently stands is to leave one or two food stations in the back of one of the classrooms. Interestingly, when questioned about this, Mr. Sabo responded that Learning Services is still trying to ascertain how many students will be attending the school on opening day and therefore if there will be a need to renovate those rooms at all. We appreciate that it seems that facilities has recognized the importance of these rooms to the community, but the fact that the district is planning a $2 million renovation for a school in which they still cannot ascertain enrolment numbers is also something that the trustees should be questioning at this point in time. Particularly since there is a perfectly good alternative 2 km away that will solve the foods room issue. Concerns with lockers in the school: The question was asked “what is wrong with lockers in the school?” The short answer to this question is, nothing. However, only paying attention to the short answers, without further analysis of the issues involved in this entire situation will result in a short sighted elementary environment that cost $2million to produce. Quite simply, lockers are not a welcoming environment for small children. This is a feeling that has been echoed by staff who were on the committee as well as principals who were on the committee. Is there a compromise to be had here? Absolutely. That compromise would be to put cubbies in the classrooms of the primary grades and allow the older students to have lockers. This could be accomplished through having primary and intermediate wings in the school. This was brought up in the March 13 meeting as a discussion point but we were told it was not up for discussion and that the budget at this point does not allow for the removal of any lockers at this point in time. So, lockers that are not being used will be bolted shut. Picture that environment for small children, for the SMALLEST children. That is not the environment of an enhanced facility. Conclusion: On April 23, Trustee Welch recognized the importance time when it comes to these issues and requested that staff make a report at the May general board meeting rather than the June meeting. We would like to thank you for recognizing the importance of this issue to the parents of students who will attend this school and attempt to get answers sooner rather than later. Quite simply, the end of June is too late to get answers to these questions. June is too late for many reasons. 1. First and foremost is that construction is set to begin on this project in July. To have a meeting at the end of June where these issues get discussed actually is too late. It would make the statement that “it is too late” true simply because you waited too long to discuss these issues. 2. For parents of students who will attend this school, forcing them to wait through the summer while solutions may or may not be found is just not fair. 3. These are issues that parents have been attempting to get addressed since last April when this plan was announced. It is now one full year later, the staff has admitted that the fields don’t work, the enrolment is half of what was expected and the cost to properly renovate the school is actually twice the amount that was projected. The district staff and the Board of Education have been kicking the responsibility for these issues back and forth and from committee to committee for MONTHS now. These are not imaginary issues that can just go off to committeeland to die, they are real issues that cost $2million to fix. The time for committees has passed. It is time to pay serious attention to these issues and to fix them…NOW. If you wait until the end of June to begin discussions on these very serious issues it will indeed be “too late”. Suggestions: 1. Hold a special board meeting in the first week of June where these issues are discussed. This will allow an open and extensive discussion regarding these issues to occur between the trustees and the staff without impacting the regular board business that must occur at the end of June in the regular board meeting. It will also address the issues associated with waiting until the end of June to have this discussion. 2. The board of education should also consider delaying the beginning of construction on Cedar Elementary until a fuller understanding of the actual costs of converting Cedar Secondary into a functional and safe elementary school versus the possibility using one of the current elementary schools in Cedar can be determined. 3. All trustees should come to the Cedar area before this meeting is held in order to better understand the issues that have been addressed. The responses to these issues by some of the trustees demonstrates that many have not actually walked the roads, the fields, the actual buildings that we are talking about flooding with elementary aged children. Come to Cedar with a new lens to better understand why parents are so reluctant to accept this plan as a good one for their children. We are not talking about small issues here. We are talking about issues of health and safety. The trustees need to think long and hard about the implications of allowing a school with major road safety concerns and DANGEROUS playing fields to move forward. These issues are a matter of public record and have been for months now. Putting children into an environment that the Board of Education knows is UNSAFE will only lead to liability issues in the future when a child breaks an ankle on the playing fields, gets sick from the unsanitary condition of the fields, or worse yet, darts onto the dark and dangerous roads, in the middle of February, where there are no sidewalks or traffic lights in an attempt to avoid being late for school. In the meeting on April 23, Mr. Sabo stood up and in response to a question and finished his answer with something to the effect of, “The board should be aware that the construction of Cedar Elementary is putting significant strain on the facilities grant and the ability to maintain other schools in the district.” This is not a word for word quote, but it he said something along these lines. When you now know that there are only 250-277 students who will attend Cedar Elementary and that the originally proposed $1.5 million is now $2.5 million, it is time to finally stop this plan and take a sober second thought. As it stands, you will be spending $2million to tear apart the NEWEST high school in the district in order to make it into a dysfunctional and DANGEROUS elementary school that will be half full, leaving more empty elementary operating seats in the area than the district currently has. There are numerous options available to the district which do not require this huge amount of money to be spent. As resident Shelagh Wynn recently stated in a letter to the Nanaimo Daily News about this issue, there is more than one way to wasted taxpayers money. In this case, the lack of rational decision making is responsible for this unnecessary flow of money. Unless someone from this Board of Education steps forward and starts showing some leadership, the responsibility for this boondoggle will directly on each of your shoulders. Sincerely signed by the four parents on the School Construction Committee who were representing Woodbank PAC, North Cedar Intermediate PAC and the community of Cedar, Yellowpoint and Cedar-bythe-Sea, Tobi Marcoux Woodbank Stephanie Higginson Woodbank Natasha Young Scott Kimler. As I said, the way the response was worded was a little confusing, so I just want to make sure we are all operating from the same assumptions in terms of programming available in Cedar. Cedar already has a flourishing strong start program (with the best strong start leader in the world) as well as a daycare operating out of Woodbank Primary. Furthermore, we have a full range of after school and during school programs running, such as mommy and me music, the rainbow program, jump start, Holiday Hamper programs, art programs. Your answer that I have quoted above left myself and my colleagues confused as to whether you knew that we have many programs already running at the schools, specifically the two you mention as being able to add with more space, strong start and daycare. We have these programs already and have had them for years. I would like to add that your points about the opportunities that the increased space that Cedar Secondary would provide are well taken. When I first started teaching I was fortunate enough to end up in a school that had twice as much space as we needed for the number of students that we had. It was wonderful and provided a lot of opportunity for flexibility. However, a change in government brought a new policy that outlined a per student square footage for school space. My school was only 425 students (high school level with a 90% university transition rate), so we had to make some hard choices. Stay in the building we were in and increase our student body to 1100 or move and remain a small school. Because of our unique programming we chose to move (this is the public system I am talking about, it was not a feeder school, so we could move around the district and take our students with us). We were moved into a space that was renovated specifically for the needs of our special programming. Even with those unique renovations in mind, the lack of space was felt by all. So, I recognize the importance and value of a lot of space when teaching. With the shift in student population, I want to propose something here and I would like to know your thoughts on this proposal, especially in light of the formation of the building committee at the meeting on October 15 and the way that many of the parents who would be eligible to serve on that committee are feeling about that possibility. In the original plan the proposed student population of Cedar Elementary was going to be 475 students, made up of the students from North Oyster, South Wellington, NCI and Woodbank. With a student population of this size, it would indeed make more sense to renovate CCSS than one of the existing elementary schools in Cedar. However the intended student migration did not work out as planned in the original proposal. When South Wellington closed, only 9 students from that school migrated to the NCI/Woodbank schools. North Oyster is now a part of the Ladysmith family of schools. This change in student migration and population is something that the Save Cedar Schools Coalition has been pointing out for months now. It was nice to hear that the district and board of education are finally accepting a more realistic student population of 330-350 students for Cedar Elementary. Since between 330-350 is the accepted student population perhaps the renovation money would be better spent by renovating Woodbank instead of CCSS. Here are the thoughts behind this: There is only $1.97 million for the renovation. Before we can even begin to address the unique structural learning opportunities of the 21st century learning model, there are a number of repurposing renovations necessary to the high school required to make it safe and usable for the elementary level. Let's review these from the community standpoint: 1. 2. 3. 4. 5. Retooling of current plumbing to lower and or replace fixtures with smaller fixtures Tearing out of lockers Making the second floor mezzanine safe Fixing the play fields to make them usable all year round Moving and or purchasing outdoor playground equipment All of these things need to be done before we can even start to address some of the architectural models of the 21st century learning at CCSS. However if you consider renovating Woodbank, you eliminate all of these issues. The plumbing is already appropriately sized and placed, there are no lockers to tear out, no second floor mezzanine to address, the play fields are perfectly usable the playground equipment is already in place. Furthermore, as you know, the shape of Woodbank lends itself nicely to the small learning communities that have been discussed by Cathal Walsh. The three classroom wings of Woodbank could easily be altered to make these small learning communities possible. (Although many are still undecided about how they feel about this model, the point is that the opportunity is already there at Woodbank). Woodbank's current capacity is 229, so there would need to be more space created when the renovation is happening, however this could easily be done during the construction. This allows the outdoor kindergarten to stay in tact because of the proximity of Woodbank to Hemer Park. Cathal used the outdoor kindergarten as a model of 21st century learning that is currently being practiced in the classrooms. Why get rid of one example of 21st century learning, to replace it with another? Especially when that example is a grassroots initiative that reflects the values of the community in which it is being practiced. Furthermore, in your response you mention that 350 makes a capacity utilization of 80%, but if you renovated Woodbank you could get a capacity utilization of 100%. Which we all know is very important for the ministry. Since Woodbank can not be sold, this would be a good use of the real estate and NCI could be sold. The building is just far too big to be used as an elementary school and that is a big fear for parents in this community.
