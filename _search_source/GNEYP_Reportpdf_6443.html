The Partnership is made up of individuals and representatives from a diverse set of agencies throughout Nanaimo. The original intent of the group was to plan collective action to improve the health and development of children aged 0-6. Currently, the mission is to create a community that supports the optimal development of children and their families. Over the years, the partnership has worked diligently to raise the awareness of the importance of the early years. Notable projects include:  The State of the Child Report  The Greater Nanaimo Family Magazine  Parent-Child Mother Goose  I Love when We Play DVD and  “Unplug” and “Early and Often” messaging campaigns which have been replicated in many communities across BC. Currently GNEYP is funded by two sources. The Ministry for Child and Family Development (MCFD) provides Children First funding that allows GNEYP to contract a part time coordinator. This “backbone” funding is intended to provide a resource to lead the initiative and, with a community development approach, work across sectors to assess, identify and plan for the unique needs of young children in their community. In addition, Success by 6 funding is provided through a partnership of Credit Unions, MCFD and the United Way of Central and Northern Vancouver Island. In Nanaimo these funds have been project focused and act as leverage for other funding opportunities or as a catalyst for strong partnership work. The impetus for the 2014 consultation process was driven by significant changes brought about from retirements, staff changes and the decision to change the working relationship between Greater Nanaimo and Ladysmith. For many years Ladysmith and Nanaimo worked with a joint leadership approach. For sustainability reasons and the desire to have an opportunity to focus on the unique needs of each community, the collaboration has been redefined with separate steering committees. Greater Nanaimo Early Years Partnership – Community Consultation 5 Consultation Methodology The Greater Nanaimo Early Years Partnership, recognizing an opportunity to evaluate effectiveness, initiated a consultation process with two distinct intentions. The first was to examine the working structure of GNEYP in order to address barriers to meaningful participation. The second was to build an Early Years Plan which reflects the current needs and priorities as seen by both parents and services of Greater Nanaimo. Tracy Smyth and Tammy Dewar of Raising the Village Consulting were hired to conduct a community consultation process to unearth a range of perspectives required to influence a new direction. The consultation process worked to re-focus and re-energize GNEYP and better position the group to harness the enthusiasm and passion of a wider range of people who are willing and able to contribute to collaborative work to support young children and their families. The results, described in detail in this report and reflected in the 2014-2016 Greater Nanaimo Early Years Plan, address the following questions: 1. What are the priorities that will help to strengthen the effectiveness of the existing Partnership (including relationships, communication and structure)? 2. What are the priorities that will help the Partnership to best support the young children and families in the Nanaimo area? The consultation included the application of relationship mapping software, personal interviews, online surveys to potential partners and parents along with dialogue sessions at the GNEYP table and with the Nanaimo Early Years Leadership Team (NEYLT). March 31, 2014 6 Results/Key Learning Focus Area #1 – GNEYP Working Structure Relationship Mapping The relationship mapping, using software called Partnertool, included organizations that were already connected to GNEYP. The goal was to analyze, record and track the existing Partnership as a baseline network in order to design strategies to enhance current and future relationships. The results validated the immense commitment of the core group of organizations that consider themselves a part of the Partnership. It also highlighted the fact that the group lacked a “periphery” - the very important connections beyond the core group of organizations. Figure 2 shows a map created using the relationship mapping software. It was generated based on a series of questions that analyzed both the perception of relationships and the evidence of working together. The map demonstrates GNEYP’s tight core by the many multiple connections between each agency. Finally, the key learning from the relationship mapping process was the indication that GNEYP’s periphery is weak. This can, in part, be explained by the fact that the mapping participants were limited to those who received GNEYP minutes and there may be some organizations that connect to GNEYP and don’t receive minutes. The anecdotal stories from Partnership participants, however, was that relationships beyond those already connected were, indeed, slim and tenuous. The danger of having a strong core but without an active periphery is akin to an “old boys network,” that fosters homogeneous thinking and is a structure that prevents innovation. The opposite of what we found GNEYP participants want in a network. Greater Nanaimo Early Years Partnership – Community Consultation 19 Focus Area #2 – Early Years Priorities Who was consulted and how? From February 7th until March 5th, 2014 the third wave of the consultation process was underway. The “digging deeper” phase included interactive methods to garner quantitative and qualitative data with a focus on the early years priorities GNEYP should focus on in the coming years. 3. Electronic survey to parents: a parent survey was crafted and sent out using the solid database of 8000 parents currently engaged via Children First Early Years Community Coordinator, Amber Bruner. In addition GNEYP/NEYLT members were asked to circulate the survey via the parents they work directly with. A total of 684 parents responded. 4. Electronic survey to potential partners: potential partners were identified via the PartnerTool survey responses around the question of “who else” GNEYP would like to engage with, learn from or strengthen a partnership with. The potential partner survey was emailed out to an initial list of 95 people; several people also forwarded it to further contacts. This list included funders, elected officials, the private sector and other social sector organizations not currently connected (Appendix A). A total of 33 potential partners responded to the survey. 5. Personal interviews: were conducted as a form of developmental evaluation. The purpose of the personal interviews was to gain perspective and understanding around the previous and current effectiveness, potential and strengths of GNEYP. In addition, people were asked to respond to priorities and where GNEYP should focus their energies in the coming year(s). The list of interview participants was identified based on those that could give deeper historical context (not already gathered at GNEYP meetings), those who could provide an Aboriginal/cultural perspective, and those identified through GNEYP/NEYLT meetings as organizations of particular interest to learn more from (Appendix C). Initially, the results of the consensus process resulted in 5 overall priorities 1. Influence Public Policy 2. Reduce Barriers 3. Increase Mental Health Supports 4. Sustainable Quality Childcare 5. Opportunities for community connections/ownership The group then decided that the priorities could be reduced to three by combining priority #4 with Influencing Public Policy and #5 with Reducing Barriers. This decision resulted in 3 priority Early Years areas for GNEYP to focus on in the coming year (s). 1. Influence Public Policy –public policy at the local level seemed to be the targeted first step identified from most groups. Areas of focus may be around local sustainable child care and family friendly policies. 2. Reduce Barriers – with an effort to ensuring families are able to find and access services and supports. Additionally, it was noted that barriers could be reduced with increased community connections/ownership and improved information on accessing support. 3. Increase Mental Health Supports – for adult and children. After the Forum The priority information was synthesized by the Raising the Village Consulting team and appraised based on the three month long harvest of information. In order to keep the priorities manageable, it was decided to propose a roll up of priority #3 (Increasing Mental Health Supports) into priority #2 (Reducing Barriers). There were similarities in the “barrier” and “mental health” discussions and not enough distinct information at this time to illicit two separate priorities. Influence Public Policy – including municipal, SD68, and provincial with a child lens on development.
