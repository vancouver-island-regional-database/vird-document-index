Trustee Stanley – Zone Report 1 School and Community Mental Health Conference (2019) Report Submitted by: Jessica Stanley to the NLPS Board of Education Core Themes: Systems Thinking and Systems Leadership Systems Theory: An understanding of the individual as related to and influenced by the whole. A perspective that behaviour, thinking, and reactions are influenced by all the interconnected dynamics in a whole system. This can be an individual in a family, a student in a class, a principal in a school district, a person in society. All individual behaviour is viewed as being influenced by the system dynamics and also as influencing the system. Each factor in a system influences the system. Sometimes behaviours are outcomes of the systems. Context and Application: Mental Health in Education. This includes students, teachers, staff, principals, administration and Trustees. Systems perspective views all as related and influencing on another. For example, we cannot have a positive well-being in a school without considering the well-being of the principal. We must view classroom well-being as connected to the well-being of the school district as well as the student’s families. Systems leadership is rooted in self-awareness, social connectedness, interpersonal skills and holistic decision making. Systems leadership requires an awareness of our interconnectedness and an interaction approach that honours and builds upon generative relationships. Healthy systems and learning environments foster individual resilience and well-being, positive working environments and support positive mental health. Mental health literacy and Social/Emotional Learning are important skills in healthy systems. Report Submitted to BCSTA Mental Health Working Group A basic understanding of mental illness and systems of well-being is necessary for Boards of education to understand and create policy that supports mental health. Mental illness is understood from a diathesis-stress model meaning that both a vulnerability to mental illness (e.g., genetics, trauma background, epigenetics) and stress factors (e.g., poverty, traumatic events, job loss, relationship demise) lead to the manifestation of mental illness. From a systems perspective, the symptoms of mental illness (to the extent that they are not biologically determined) are understood in the context of the greater system; individual symptoms (e.g., behavioural problems) are often reflections of dysfunction in the system. To view mental illness and well-being from a systems perspective, we must consider the environment, community, and family life of the children in the K-12 system. Not taking care of Trustee Stanley – Zone Report 2 aspects in our system will add stress into the system. Hence the statement: if we have stressed Principals, we will have stressed teachers and stressed students. Further, we must understand ourselves as interconnected not only in our relationships with others but also to our environment. Nanaimo-Ladysmith Public Schools have recently embraced a framework that acknowledges our interconnectedness with one another and with our environment which may be useful for consideration at a provincial level. https://www.sd68.bc.ca/wpcontent/uploads/Syeyutsus_Policy.pdf Incorporating a systems perspective in our work to improve mental health and well-being in our schools requires us to step back and look at the factors influencing the system, practically determine our priorities, and accept that individual and system well-being is not achieved by waiving a magic wand, techniques or quick fix-it solutions. Rather, a change in perspective is required. There needs to be a shift to a preventative model and away from a reactive model. Our primary organizational mechanisms must foster collaboration and our leaders need to understand and embrace that leadership exists at all levels. Healthy systems need to become an intention with collaboration and awareness of interconnectedness at the forefront. These practices are best determined at local levels through engagement with partner groups, families and communities. However, Trustees can promote these values in their strategic plans and budgeting decisions. It must be acknowledged that public education is a system that constantly makes do with insufficient funding. We deny needs because we do not have sufficient resources to meet those needs. This is an inherently unhealthy system that is inconsistent with established knowledge of well-being. To ignore this fact is the equivalent of denying a neglected child’s complaints of unmet needs. Systems perspective requires honestly acknowledging the problems and strengths of a system. The message of “we are doing the best we can with what we’ve got” is more accurate. As is, the public education system has much division with many of these differences in perspective related to funding levels. In this context, we seem to have forgotten that we are all on the same side. Chronic underfunding and constant stress cannot be ignored. However, we must also celebrate our considerable achievements. Openly embracing challenges and successes communicates that we are working together to best meet needs – this encourages collaboration. Further, honest acknowledgement of challenges is a necessary step towards well-being (e.g., it is a necessary first step in any therapeutic endeavor). To address the question of how we, in the public education sector, can work toward improving mental health, we need to shift from “doing” mental health actions to “being” mentally healthy. Mental health strategies should be embedded into daily routines rather than just teaching strategies. As well, greater information about mental health and psychological Trustee Stanley – Zone Report 3 disorders could be included in our curriculum not only as a way to support well-being but also as a way to support inclusion. How do we foster well-being and healthy systems from a systems perspective? Some ideas: 1) Best practices of engagement and collaboration should be encouraged both within our school districts and with the external community. 2) Human resources priorities need to be rooted in knowledge of a systems perspective (i.e., that individual issues are usually reflection of the greater system). For example, dropping attendance rates are a symptom of something in the system (e.g., school culture) 3) As leaders in our schools and pivotal determinants of school culture, Principals should be well-versed in issues of mental health, good communication, and collaborative working styles. School districts need to support our school-based administration with clear processes about mental health related issues (e.g., reporting standards about sexually intrusive behaviour) and with well-being supports (e.g., established consultation structures when facing challenging situations and in bearing the emotional burden of those situations). 4) School Counselors and School Psychologists vary considerably in their training on the epidemiology of psychological disorders potentially leaving them ill-prepared to understand, diagnose, support and/or treatment mental illness in our schools. Their training could be adjusted to include these topics or we should consider hiring Clinical Psychologists to work in our school districts. Further, Trustees and senior administration need a better understanding of the differing expertise of School Psychologists and Clinical Psychologists to allow for better decisions making. 5) There needs to be clarity about the role of schools in treatment of mental health. As is, communities are increasingly relying upon and expecting services from our schools. If this is a role that the provincial government expects the schools to play, the appropriate services and necessary funding need to be in place. BCSTA should clarify this with the provincial government and this role should be clarified with the community. If schools are expected to play a role in treatment of mental illness, then the appropriate service providers (e.g., Clinical Psychologists) should be present in the schools and school districts funded accordingly. Trustee Stanley – Zone Report 6 Report from BCSTA Mental Health Working Group To: BCSTA Board of Directors Date: March 5, 2019 BCSTA established an ad hoc working group and appointed the following members: Moyra Baxter, Laura Dixon, Jen Menzie, Raymond Raj and Margot Swinburnson. Also, we are grateful for the assistance of Jessica Stanley to this report as she attended the School and Community Mental Health Conference along with the members of the Working Group. A. Purposes of the BCSTA Mental Health Working Group 1) Review motions adopted at the 2018 AGM related to mental health issues; 2) Make recommendations to the Board of Directors as to how the Association might best achieve the specific desired outcomes: 3) Make any additional recommendations to the Board of Directors regarding K-12 focused initiatives on mental health B. Introduction B.C.’s Ministry of Education defines the purpose of the public school system this way: “to enable students to develop their individual potential and to acquire the knowledge, skills and abilities needed to contribute to a healthy society and a prosperous and sustainable economy.” Student outcomes are the joint responsibility of Boards of Education and the Ministry of Education. BCSTA member boards have long recognized through their advocacy that we must support both staff and student well-being in order to improve system wide outcomes. Boards of Education work to ensure their school districts are meeting the needs of their communities and therefore have been advocates for policy, funding and programming to support student and staff wellness but we have yet to be invited to partner in the policy and programming work currently underway by government in this area. C. The role for Boards of Education in supporting student mental wellness and improving student outcomes Currently, all school districts all engage in the four pillars of comprehensive school health: Trustee Stanley – Zone Report 7 Relationships and Environments: This pillar is about: 1) the social environment, such as the quality of relationships and emotional well-being; and 2) the physical spaces in the school, such as building, equipment and outdoor areas. Teaching and Learning: This pillar is not about “teachers” and “students” but about teaching and learning opportunities, both inside the classroom and out, that help to build knowledge and skills to improve health and well being. Community Partnerships: Referring to connections between the school and the community, including parents, other schools, community organizations and health professionals. This pillar can also refer to partnerships within the school such as between-class partnerships. Our School Policies: This pillar refers to provincial, district, school or classroom policies, rules, procedures and/or codes of conducts at all levels that help to shape a caring and safe environment and promote student health and well being. (see more: https://healthyschoolsbc.ca/key-focus-areas/comprehensive-school-health/ Each of our 60 member Boards of Education are progressing at different rates on these four pillars, with different capacities amongst us to provide program resources, infrastructure and in-service training for staff. Perhaps the most significant disparity is the wide variety of community based relationships and supports for our student which differ region by region, health authority by health authority. Most concerning is the extreme variability of treatment and intervention options for students which Boards of Education and our community partners have been unable to overcome. This inequity requires a policy and funding response from government. Some young people are just not receiving the timely support they need in order to be ready to learn, and inevitably student success is impacted. We know that an individual student’s future potential is impacted by this lack of support, but collectively we are all negatively impacted as a result of poor public policy and insufficient resources. Conclusion Our Working Group report discusses the Role of Boards of Education in supporting Student Mental Health and Wellness and the connection to improved student outcomes. Our analysis of the members’ motions from our 2018 AGM reveals a disconnect in our co-governance role with the Ministry of Education, a lack of clarity of the respective roles between the Ministry of Education and the Ministry of Mental Health and Addictions. Yet Boards of Education have the responsibility for the majority of the work in that would be defined as early intervention and prevention. At the same time, we are encouraged that the Premier, Education Minister and Minister of Mental Health and Addictions have each articulated a commitment to develop a strategy for a seamless and co-ordinated system of care with an emphasis on children and youth. We also recognize that much of our work under the Comprehensive School Health framework is making a real difference for our students and their future. We can strengthen our expertise in the four pillars with professional learning within our existing Trustee Stanley – Zone Report 11 BCSTA structures and by providing governance in our own districts that maintains a policy and funding focus on practices for which we have evidence of improved student outcomes. The members of the Working Group appreciate the opportunity to support our colleagues in this key work while at the beginning of the four year 2018 -2022 term of office so there is the greatest opportunity to make a difference for all students in public education.
