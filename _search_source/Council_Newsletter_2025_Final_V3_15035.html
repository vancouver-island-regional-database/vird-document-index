The length of the park will be determined by desired activities, with potential to extend north to accommodate desired uses. • Initial LiDAR contour information shows that the site has steep slopes, dropping from about 22 m at Tideview Road to 0 m at shoreline. While accuracy of grading information will need to be confirmed, initial observations are as follows: » Average slope from road to shoreline is about 18% at the south, increasing to 19%-20% as the road goes north. » In the south part of the site, slopes are flattest at the top (nearer to Tideview Road) with steep embankments between 2:1 to 3:1 closer to the shoreline. » This results in a flatter “bench” of about 8% grade through the centre of the site. This central bench is a strong candidate location for situating park elements. » The location of the bench positions main park elements relatively high above shoreline (about 13 m) which supports excellent views, but more limited water access (stairs/long ramps required). This higher elevation improves access from Tideview Road and reduces distance/grade for bringing services and road access to the site. Renovations Stz’uminus First Nation will provide a renovation program that is fair, transparent and within the limits of the resources available to the Nation. The renovation program will address health and safety concerns, improve living conditions, provide modifications to improve accessibility for elders or persons with disability, and provide major home repairs. The Housing department will manage the renovation program on the basis of set criteria for eligibility and priority. The Housing department will administer the renovation program by prioritizing requests for renovations and accessing funding from ISC, CMHC and other sources when required. The Housing department will ensure applications for funding meet all criteria and guidelines set out by the external funding agencies. a. Eligibility 1. Must be a SFN band member, who is the head of the household, living in a home on-reserve. Note: A non-SFN member may be eligible if they are the head of the household caring for SFN children. 2. Must fill out a Renovation request form. 3. Must meet criteria of external funding agency if external funding is being requested (i.e. income limits) 4. Have not received renovation funding in the past 10 years (in some cases, funding is not available for 15 years once received). 5. Members who are currently renovating an abandoned house are not eligible for assistance. Once the renovation is complete and they have lived in the house for at least one year, they are eligible to apply. Eligible requests The following items qualify as renovation requests. • Health and Safety requests – i.e. safe entrance, deck, ramp, mold remediation, fall hazards. • Electrical, Plumbing and heating repairs. • Modifications to improve accessibility and suitability for individuals with disabilities. • Major home repairs – Roof replacement, foundation, window replacement, siding, kitchen and Bathroom fixtures (these repairs may only be available by application to external funding sources). • Address a problem with overcrowding (only available by application to external funding sources). page 29 Major Project Major Project Updates Ineligible requests 1. A Journey Back in Time: The Quest for Tl’uqtinus The roots of this legal saga date back to 2007 when the Cowichan Tribes embarked on a quest to reclaim ancestral land. This quest was ignited by the Tsawwassen treaty, which granted fishing rights to the Tsawwassen First Nation in the Gulf Islands and returned land along the Fraser River. The Cowichan Tribes, however, questioned, “Where is our land? We had a village site there too!” and sought answers. Research commenced in 2007, uncovering historical survey records pinpointing the village of Tl’uqtinus as the heart of their ancestral land. This land, now federally owned, offered the potential for recovery. Extensive anthropological research also revealed that Tl’uqtinus was not solely inhabited by Cowichan members but was a shared space among various nations, including Stz’uminus, Penelakut, Halalt, Lyacksun, and Cowichan Tribes. It’s crucial to differentiate between the “Cowichan” village, a multi-nation community, and the Cowichan Tribes, a band with reserve land in the Cowichan Valley. Formation of the Cowichan Nation Alliance In 2009, a critical turning point occurred when Chief John Elliott of Stz’uminus agreed to join forces with the Cowichan Chief. This alliance was forged on the condition that it also addresses fishery recovery. page 33 Major Project Major Project Updates Updates and cranberry grounds. The Fight for Fishing Rights Despite historical records pointing to a Cowichan Nation village on the Fraser River, the Cowichan Nation currently lacks Department of Fisheries & Oceans licenses to fish the South Arm of the Fraser River. This disparity underlines the importance of their legal battle. Declaration for Reconciliation: 2013 In 2013, lawyers representing the Cowichan Nation Alliance drafted a Declaration for Reconciliation, making five key requests: Reclaiming the land at Tl’uqtinus. Mapping Tl’uqtinus: Recovering the Past Dr. Ken Brealey played a pivotal role by meticulously mapping the Tl’uqtinus village based on historical records. The name “Tl’uqtinus” translates to “long chest,” “long shore,” or “long beach.” In 1824, Hudson’s Bay Company explorers counted 108 bighouses at Tl’uqtinus, signifying the village’s historical significance. The first Europeans arrived in December 1824, estimating over 1000 men lived at Tl’uqtinus during the summer fishing season. When the Hudson Bay Company was counting the ‘Cowichan’ people when they were heading to Fort Langley in 1828, they counted 565 canoes on the beach at Tl’uqtinus. The Future: Unity and Land Division Looking ahead, the Cowichan Nation Alliance aims to secure justice and land. An agreement forged in 2014 stipulates that chiefs will meet to equitably divide the land among the five bands, with population being a key factor. The settlement encompasses 1829 acres of land, with a portion owned by the Port Authority. The ongoing court case seeks the return of public land and negotiation for private land. A First Nation or band by-law is a local law that is passed by a First Nation council, similar to a municipality, to regulate affairs within its community. A by-law governs the activity in question on all reserve lands under the control of the band council that passed the by-law. The by-law applies to everyone present on the reserve, regardless of whether they are a band member or whether they actually live on the reserve.
