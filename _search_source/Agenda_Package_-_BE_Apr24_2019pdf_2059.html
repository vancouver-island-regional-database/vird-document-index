Page 31 of 91 The purpose of the BC Human Rights Code is to foster a society where there are no impediments to free and full participation in the economic, social, political and cultural life of our province. It is also intended to promote a climate of understanding, mutual respect, and equity in dignity and rights, to protect people from discrimination, to provide those people with a means of redress, and to identify and ​eliminate persistent patterns of inequality associated with discrimination prohibited by the Code. Discrimination is an action or a decision that treats a person or a group badly for reasons such as their race, gender identity or expression, or disability. It’s important to note that absence of intention to discriminate is not a legal defence. The BC Human Rights Code is guided by the Canadian Human Rights Act, and Canada’s Act is informed by the United Nations Human Rights Council. Around the world, inclusive education is a goal and a pursuit and our country is signatory to agreements that say we will do this. Catalina Devandas, the UN Special Rapporteur on the rights of persons with disabilities made the following comments on April 12, 2019 after looking at inclusive education in Canada: “However, I am concerned that most provincial and territorial policies are yet to implement fully inclusive education systems and that students with disabilities in other parts of Canada may receive considerably different levels of support. I was informed that many children with disabilities are still being taught in segregated classrooms or in special education schools, and I received worrisome reports that children with disabilities can be put on partial school days or temporarily removed from school, for periods of up to six months without access to education. I also noted a disconnection between the State’s commitment to inclusion in legislation and policies, and everyday implementation in practice, reflected in long waiting time and lack of services for students with disabilities and their families, putting them under significant emotional and financial pressure. I was also informed that children with disabilities in segregated classes or those that have followed some kind of individualized education plan may receive a different certification or diploma than other children, which limits their opportunities for enrolling in education at higher levels.” We know that school districts around BC believe in inclusive education and strive to work towards it in a collaborative manner. Page 32 of 91 Unfortunately, discrimination is still a regular feature of education in BC. It’s difficult to move from an ideal to a reality without a plan. We hope that every district’s strategic plan has a specific component that will effectively move them along the continuum to a fully inclusive education. This component should echo a similar component that should be embedded in the BC Education Plan. A School Board budget is an excellent place to start implementing changes to move towards an inclusive model, to align with Canada’s agreement​ to the United Nations’ Convention on the Rights of Persons with Disabilities and its Optional Protocol. Here are some suggestions we have for you to consider. This is not an exhaustive list and if you connect with other school boards around the province and across Canada, you will find that many are implementing effective strategies. 1. Make your current EAs full time (a minimum of 35 hours per week) 2. Properly equipped sensory rooms creating sensory sensitive spaces. Allocate extra funding for each school in each school budget that carries a directive for the Administrator to implement. 3. Make assistive technology a priority and provide training and resources for teachers to implement them. 4. Stop deploying specialist teachers as replacements for absent classroom teachers - hire the teachers that your district needs. 5. Hire more school psychologists and conduct more assessments. 6. Provide up to date training in current educational practices and put a call to action for educators and educational assistants to be in line with the prescribed training. 7. Provide training for all levels of management on inclusion and support for students with disabilities. 8. Ensure that all secondary students with disabilities have access to all academic courses to ensure that they have the opportunity to graduate with a Dogwood. This means that all the sports academies, academic and fine arts specialty programs, and even French Immersion, are all programs of choice. As such they should be funded AFTER assuring that adequate services and supports have been provided to ensure equitable access to education for ​all​ students, including students with disabilities. Page 40 of 91 Trustee Stanley – Zone Report 1 School and Community Mental Health Conference (2019) Report Submitted by: Jessica Stanley to the NLPS Board of Education Core Themes: Systems Thinking and Systems Leadership Systems Theory: An understanding of the individual as related to and influenced by the whole. A perspective that behaviour, thinking, and reactions are influenced by all the interconnected dynamics in a whole system. This can be an individual in a family, a student in a class, a principal in a school district, a person in society. All individual behaviour is viewed as being influenced by the system dynamics and also as influencing the system. Each factor in a system influences the system. Sometimes behaviours are outcomes of the systems. Context and Application: Mental Health in Education. This includes students, teachers, staff, principals, administration and Trustees. Systems perspective views all as related and influencing on another. For example, we cannot have a positive well-being in a school without considering the well-being of the principal. We must view classroom well-being as connected to the well-being of the school district as well as the student’s families. Systems leadership is rooted in self-awareness, social connectedness, interpersonal skills and holistic decision making. Systems leadership requires an awareness of our interconnectedness and an interaction approach that honours and builds upon generative relationships. Healthy systems and learning environments foster individual resilience and well-being, positive working environments and support positive mental health. Mental health literacy and Social/Emotional Learning are important skills in healthy systems. Report Submitted to BCSTA Mental Health Working Group A basic understanding of mental illness and systems of well-being is necessary for Boards of education to understand and create policy that supports mental health. Mental illness is understood from a diathesis-stress model meaning that both a vulnerability to mental illness (e.g., genetics, trauma background, epigenetics) and stress factors (e.g., poverty, traumatic events, job loss, relationship demise) lead to the manifestation of mental illness. From a systems perspective, the symptoms of mental illness (to the extent that they are not biologically determined) are understood in the context of the greater system; individual symptoms (e.g., behavioural problems) are often reflections of dysfunction in the system. To view mental illness and well-being from a systems perspective, we must consider the environment, community, and family life of the children in the K-12 system. Not taking care of Page 41 of 91 Trustee Stanley – Zone Report 2 aspects in our system will add stress into the system. Hence the statement: if we have stressed Principals, we will have stressed teachers and stressed students. Further, we must understand ourselves as interconnected not only in our relationships with others but also to our environment. Nanaimo-Ladysmith Public Schools have recently embraced a framework that acknowledges our interconnectedness with one another and with our environment which may be useful for consideration at a provincial level. https://www.sd68.bc.ca/wpcontent/uploads/Syeyutsus_Policy.pdf Incorporating a systems perspective in our work to improve mental health and well-being in our schools requires us to step back and look at the factors influencing the system, practically determine our priorities, and accept that individual and system well-being is not achieved by waiving a magic wand, techniques or quick fix-it solutions. Rather, a change in perspective is required. There needs to be a shift to a preventative model and away from a reactive model. Our primary organizational mechanisms must foster collaboration and our leaders need to understand and embrace that leadership exists at all levels. Healthy systems need to become an intention with collaboration and awareness of interconnectedness at the forefront. These practices are best determined at local levels through engagement with partner groups, families and communities. However, Trustees can promote these values in their strategic plans and budgeting decisions. It must be acknowledged that public education is a system that constantly makes do with insufficient funding. We deny needs because we do not have sufficient resources to meet those needs. This is an inherently unhealthy system that is inconsistent with established knowledge of well-being. To ignore this fact is the equivalent of denying a neglected child’s complaints of unmet needs. Systems perspective requires honestly acknowledging the problems and strengths of a system. The message of “we are doing the best we can with what we’ve got” is more accurate. As is, the public education system has much division with many of these differences in perspective related to funding levels. In this context, we seem to have forgotten that we are all on the same side. Chronic underfunding and constant stress cannot be ignored. However, we must also celebrate our considerable achievements. Openly embracing challenges and successes communicates that we are working together to best meet needs – this encourages collaboration. Further, honest acknowledgement of challenges is a necessary step towards well-being (e.g., it is a necessary first step in any therapeutic endeavor). To address the question of how we, in the public education sector, can work toward improving mental health, we need to shift from “doing” mental health actions to “being” mentally healthy. Mental health strategies should be embedded into daily routines rather than just teaching strategies. As well, greater information about mental health and psychological Page 42 of 91 Trustee Stanley – Zone Report 3 disorders could be included in our curriculum not only as a way to support well-being but also as a way to support inclusion. How do we foster well-being and healthy systems from a systems perspective? Some ideas: 1) Best practices of engagement and collaboration should be encouraged both within our school districts and with the external community. 2) Human resources priorities need to be rooted in knowledge of a systems perspective (i.e., that individual issues are usually reflection of the greater system). For example, dropping attendance rates are a symptom of something in the system (e.g., school culture) 3) As leaders in our schools and pivotal determinants of school culture, Principals should be well-versed in issues of mental health, good communication, and collaborative working styles. School districts need to support our school-based administration with clear processes about mental health related issues (e.g., reporting standards about sexually intrusive behaviour) and with well-being supports (e.g., established consultation structures when facing challenging situations and in bearing the emotional burden of those situations). 4) School Counselors and School Psychologists vary considerably in their training on the epidemiology of psychological disorders potentially leaving them ill-prepared to understand, diagnose, support and/or treatment mental illness in our schools. Their training could be adjusted to include these topics or we should consider hiring Clinical Psychologists to work in our school districts. Further, Trustees and senior administration need a better understanding of the differing expertise of School Psychologists and Clinical Psychologists to allow for better decisions making. 5) There needs to be clarity about the role of schools in treatment of mental health. As is, communities are increasingly relying upon and expecting services from our schools. If this is a role that the provincial government expects the schools to play, the appropriate services and necessary funding need to be in place. BCSTA should clarify this with the provincial government and this role should be clarified with the community. If schools are expected to play a role in treatment of mental illness, then the appropriate service providers (e.g., Clinical Psychologists) should be present in the schools and school districts funded accordingly. Introduction B.C.’s Ministry of Education defines the purpose of the public school system this way: “to enable students to develop their individual potential and to acquire the knowledge, skills and abilities needed to contribute to a healthy society and a prosperous and sustainable economy.” Student outcomes are the joint responsibility of Boards of Education and the Ministry of Education. BCSTA member boards have long recognized through their advocacy that we must support both staff and student well-being in order to improve system wide outcomes. Boards of Education work to ensure their school districts are meeting the needs of their communities and therefore have been advocates for policy, funding and programming to support student and staff wellness but we have yet to be invited to partner in the policy and programming work currently underway by government in this area. C. The role for Boards of Education in supporting student mental wellness and improving student outcomes Currently, all school districts all engage in the four pillars of comprehensive school health: Page 46 of 91 Trustee Stanley – Zone Report 7 Relationships and Environments: This pillar is about: 1) the social environment, such as the quality of relationships and emotional well-being; and 2) the physical spaces in the school, such as building, equipment and outdoor areas. Teaching and Learning: This pillar is not about “teachers” and “students” but about teaching and learning opportunities, both inside the classroom and out, that help to build knowledge and skills to improve health and well being. Community Partnerships: Referring to connections between the school and the community, including parents, other schools, community organizations and health professionals. This pillar can also refer to partnerships within the school such as between-class partnerships. Our School Policies: This pillar refers to provincial, district, school or classroom policies, rules, procedures and/or codes of conducts at all levels that help to shape a caring and safe environment and promote student health and well being. (see more: https://healthyschoolsbc.ca/key-focus-areas/comprehensive-school-health/ Each of our 60 member Boards of Education are progressing at different rates on these four pillars, with different capacities amongst us to provide program resources, infrastructure and in-service training for staff. Perhaps the most significant disparity is the wide variety of community based relationships and supports for our student which differ region by region, health authority by health authority. Most concerning is the extreme variability of treatment and intervention options for students which Boards of Education and our community partners have been unable to overcome. This inequity requires a policy and funding response from government. Some young people are just not receiving the timely support they need in order to be ready to learn, and inevitably student success is impacted. We know that an individual student’s future potential is impacted by this lack of support, but collectively we are all negatively impacted as a result of poor public policy and insufficient resources.
