Background: In 2020 the provincial government amended the School Act with sections 85.1-85.4 regarding the establishment of policies governing child care facilities on board of education property. The Minister of Education also issued a prescriptive Order (M326) which includes contents required in board policy. As a result of these amendments and this Order, boards of education are required to review its policies and administrative procedures on childcare within the District. Currently, the District has Administrative Procedure 518 – Out of School Care (AP 518), that governs childcare on our sites and Administrative Procedure 220 – Early Years Program (AP 220) that addresses early learning. This memo outlines a new Administrative Procedure (new AP) that combines the current AP 518 and AP 220 to more reflect the proactive expansion of child care on our sites. It also recommends that the Board create a standalone Board policy. Finally, the new Administrative Procedure ensures that requirements of the School Act and M326 with respect to childcare policies and procedures are met in the interim. Discussion: Policy Creation Staff is recommending that the Board create a child care policy. While the Board has supported early childhood education and child care on District sites for many years, staff believe that a Board policy that outlines its commitment to child care is important for the community as well as to provide a basis for the recommended child care strategy in the Long Range Facilities Plan. Highlights of the new AP The following are the key highlights to the new AP: 1. As noted above, the AP combines the current AP 518 and AP 220 into a comprehensive document. We have not highlighted the format changes or minor edits; Page 16 of 22 Action Sheet: Child Care and Early Years Programs Policy Page 2 2. A requirement for qualified early childhood educators to deliver early learning programming utilizing the B.C. Early Learning Framework; 3. A description of the child care program fees if operated by the board or a licensee is included; 4. A requirement to ensure child care programs are operated in a manner that fosters indigenous reconciliation, promotes the acquisition and use of Hul’q’umi’num language, and is inclusive and consistent with the principles of non-discrimination set out in the British Columbia Human Rights Code; 5. A list of conditions to be included in the contracts with licensees, as well as a requirement to review the child care programs on an annual basis; 6. A requirement for the district, on an ongoing basis to assess community need for child care programs on board property, through a process of engagement with employee groups, parents and guardians, Indigenous community representatives, Indigenous rightsholders, Indigenous service providers, municipalities and regional districts, and existing child care operators; and 7. A requirement for ongoing consideration to be made whether child care programs are best provided by licensees other than the board, the board, or a combination of both; Timing of the AP We note that the AP is coming ahead of the anticipated policy. This is for two reasons. First, we want to make sure our AP aligns with requirements of the School Act and M326, which we understand that it does. Second, as noted in our presentation on March 3, 2021 at the Business Committee meeting, we intend to engage external providers for 2021-22. We want the providers to be aware of the expectations of the District with respect the alignment of child care and schools. Further, it also telegraphs to our existing providers the direction of the District. In the event that the AP does not align with the Policy staff will make the required changes. The purpose of this procedure is to provide guidance with respect to how the district will promote the use of board property for the provision of child care programs at a minimum between the hours of 7 a.m. and 6 p.m. on business days by either the board or third-party licensees. Capital replacement costs Guiding Principles 1. Developmentally appropriate early learning programming reflects a belief in the uniqueness of childhood. AP XXX – Child Care Services and Early Years Programs Page 20 of 22 Page 3 of 3 NANAIMO LADYSMITH PUBLIC SCHOOLS EDUCATION COMMITTEE PUBLIC MEETING INFORMATION SHEET DATE: TO: FROM: SUBJECT: May 5, 2021 Education Committee Superintendent, Scott Saywell Secondary Quarter System for 2021/2022 Background During the 2019-2020 school year, our secondary schools were organized in a Semester System. Students would take 4 courses from September through January, then another 4 courses from February through June. In March of 2020 the Ministry of Education moved all students to remote learning. With the exception of children of essential services workers, students in NLPS worked remotely through April and May. In June, students returned part time to school, with the remaining learning continuing remotely. The Ministry of Education developed a 5-Stage Restart Plan. We finished the school year in Stage 2, with students attending school for part time in-class instruction and part time remote learning. In late July of 2020, the MOE updated their 5-Stage Restart Plan, with changes to Stage 2, including the requirement to restrict the number of students within the newly termed “Learning Group.” At the secondary level, the maximum Learning Group size was set at 120. This was a significant challenge for schools on a Semester or Linear system throughout the province. To reduce Learning Group size to 120 or fewer, most schools across the province moved to a Quarter System. In NLPS, this required a completely new build of secondary timetables in late August. Building timetables within a very short timeline, for a system that students and staff were not familiar with, along with the restrictions the come with Learning Groups, was challenging. In best efforts to work within the intent of the newly formed Learning Groups, secondary schools in NLPS organized students within Learning Groups of small numbers wherever possible. Many Learning Group sizes in grades 8, 9, or 10 were limited to 30 or less students. Although this organization aligns with the health and safety guidelines for contacttracing purposes in the event of a COVID-19 exposure at school, the smaller Learning Groups sizes limited the ability for students to be placed in all of the courses they had requested. Page 21 of 22 Information Sheet: Secondary Quarter System for 2021/2022 Page 2 Quarter System 2021-2022 Planning for the 2021-2022 school year typically occurs from February to May of each year. As the planning phase proceeds, the ability to change course becomes more and more difficult. Schools have to make decisions based on the current Ministry of Education Guidelines as they cannot predict what Stage of the Restart Plan will be in effect, or if the Ministry will make changes to the plan over the summer. For these reasons, schools are presently organizing classes in a Quarter System for 2021-2022. This does not mean that schools will be unable to change course if more information concerning the threat of COVID-19 becomes available. Schools will continue to engage their staff, students, and broader community this school year as the province continues to address the challenges of COVID-19.
