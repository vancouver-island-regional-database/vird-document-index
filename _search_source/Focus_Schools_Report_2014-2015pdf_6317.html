Focus Schools Initiative: A Year In Review The following report provides a high-level summary review of the 2014-2015 Focus Schools Initiative within NLPS. The district is in its third year of implementation of its Response to Intervention (RtI) framework and is seeking to create a sustainable and scalable tiered system of supports for all students in the area of reading. An important aspect of this RtI framework is to increase the use of evidence-based practices within regular instruction. When a significant population of students is at risk it is important to focus on improving the intensity of instruction to improve outcomes. The successful implementation of the district’s RtI framework is key to this Focus School Initiative. Since the implementation of the district’s Response to Intervention (RtI) framework in 2011, principals have been asked to maintain their managerial role while at the same time, become instructional leaders, change agents, capacity builders and facilitators in order to lead the implementation of RtI in their schools. Within NLPS, like most districts, there are schools that experience more challenges and vulnerabilities than others. These challenges often present as social and/or economic challenges. In NLPS we have a variety of socio-economic areas. However, we have a distinct group of schools that experience greater than average challenges, in this regard. In addition, many of these schools also have a higher than average Aboriginal student population. We know that currently Aboriginal students neither participate nor succeed at the same rate as their non-Aboriginal counterparts, in school. The history of colonization in Canada and in particular, residential schools continues to have a huge impact on Canada’s Aboriginal people. Colonization sought to forcibly assimilate Aboriginal people into ‘mainstream’ society. This forced assimilation has left a country of people disconnected from family, culture, language and pride in who they are as Aboriginal people. These aspects of disconnection affect the functioning and/or success in every aspect of society. We also know that reading rates are a critical determinant in school success as well as a clear indicator for life chances. Reading has an impact on every part of Canadian life. Our democracy, our economy, and the quality of our daily lives are all enhanced by reading well and critically. Literacy has become an essential part of the fabric of modern societies, a thread that links all aspects of life and living in our contemporary world. Its reach is extensive and complex, influencing how fully and effectively a person is able to engage in the social and economic life of his or her community. 2014-2015 Focus Schools Report, Department of Learning Services 1 CCL – Canadian Council on Learning http://www.cclcca.ca/CCL/Topic/Literacy/WhatisLiteracy.html#why Provincial assessment and report card data from 2013 indicate that the percent of students underperforming in grade 4 reading in each of these schools ranged from 70% - 33%. Needless to say, these outcomes have resulted in a sense of urgency to develop and maintain effective reading instruction at these schools. NLPS Department of Learning Services (DLS) considered these factors and reviewed our own academic data. The data identified a number of schools that struggle with the factors mentioned above. DLS wanted to support these schools in an explicit, effective way that directly impacted student learning. Vulnerability factors considered, five schools were invited to participate in this initiative. Why we embarked on the Focus Schools project? The Department of Learning Services brought the Focus Schools together to participate in a planning session around the development of a primary level (K-3), early intervention focus school initiative aimed to provide ‘21st Century teaching’ models that are centred around a belief that all students can learn at high levels – including those schools within our district that have a higher population of vulnerable students. Principals at the Focus Schools agreed to the initiative, short term as a measure to increase the capacity of the educators in these schools and assure that all students K – 3 are confident successful readers. The criteria to be considered a ‘focus school’ participant included those schools that hold a higher vulnerability index and a higher Aboriginal student population. A Focus Schools project team was identified, comprising of school staff (principals, itinerate staff, teachers, Supervisor of Instruction and Director of Instruction) who worked together to develop the following project Goals2: 1. Develop and expand current school level initiatives that are evidenced-based 2. Establish a clearly defined RTI process (Tiers 1,2 & 3) for reading and communicate those practices to all district schools 3. Promote principles of self-regulated learning to positively impact all areas of student learning 4. Increase the achievement of Aboriginal students; close the discrepancy of achievement and prevent the overrepresentation of Aboriginal students in special needs categories To support the project’s goal number 4 pertaining to Aboriginal students, the project team identified that all good teaching and reading needs to be the same – what needs to be different is the knowledge of history, perspective, and creating a sense of belonging in schools. In support of this goal, the team identified a specialized Aboriginal PLC that would meet regularly to discuss specifically Aboriginal student data throughout the 20142015 year. How the Project was implemented What was occurring in the district in a parallel process was the creation of a reading assessment, which eventually evolved into creating the NLPS Reading Assessment – a formative assessment. Although we were looking for summative data to make informed decisions at the district level, what evolved out of this assessment process was a formative assessment that was far more useful to teachers in classrooms. The district made a commitment to not collect reading data at the district level however, Focus Schools principals worked together around the collection and analysis of their own specific data for the purposes of the Focus Schools project. Principals agreed that the focus school model is meant to be a shorter term measure to increase the capacity of the educators in these schools and assure that all students k – 3 are confident successful readers. The Focus Schools Initiative is focused on increasing the capacity of the educators onsite. Four students moved away, leaving only 29 to track from the beginning of the year (we did not include students who arrived part way through the year as we did not have the original data to consider). Of the 29 students, 25 students are now fully meeting or exceeding expectations in concepts of print and 24 for letter sound correspondence! For our Aboriginal leaners, 70% are meeting expectations now for concept of print and 60% do so for letter sound correspondence. This is something truly amazing to celebrate indeed!” 2014-2015 Focus Schools Report, Department of Learning Services 6 Georgia “We are very excited to be reflecting on our journey as a Focus School. The goals/focus’ of the initiative will remain the same for the 2015/16 school year: K-3 reading, SRL and Aboriginal student achievement. District supports will remain the same, with the exception of the 0.5 FTE – District Aboriginal Education Enhancement Coordinator/District Coordinator of Self-Regulated Learning. These types of support will still be available, however no specific FTE will be allotted to this initiative. We found that these resources were under-utilized, therefore we have pulled this 0.5FTE mandate out of the initiative During the project team year-end debrief the team decided to amend goal #3: Promote principles of self-regulated learning to positively impact all areas of student learning to better reflect the key components of SRL. The goal has been amended as follows: Promote the principles of SRL by: integrating social/emotional learning throughout the day; promoting meta-cognition through planning, self-monitoring and reflecting on process. The team wants to ensure SRL becomes more tangible with a stronger emphasis placed on promoting the principals of SRL, to positively impact all areas of student learning. It has been decided that North Oyster Elementary will not be participant in the 2015/16 school year. North Oyster has undergone significant changes this past year, as a newly French Immersion/English Dual Track School. These changes have the school community engaged and focused on this transition. The Focus Schools Initiative will potentially be an option for this school at another time. 2014-2015 Focus Schools Report, Department of Learning Services 8 The project lead, Director of Instruction will lead the project team through a collaborative process to develop the 2015-2016 Focus Schools plan early in September, building on key successes gained this year.
