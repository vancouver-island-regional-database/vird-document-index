Print Story - canada.com networkMonday » March 11 » 2013 New science labs part of Wellington upgrade package Robert Barron Daily News Tuesday, February 05, 2013 Wellington Secondary School will get new science labs when its $22.6-million seismic upgrade is completed in 2016, but the majority of the renovations will be geared toward dealing with safety issues. School board chairman Jamie Brennan said Wellington's upgrades include plans to tear down and completely replace a block of the school where the labs are currently located and will allow for some new lab equipment to be purchased. But he said there isn't enough money in the budget for the school's seismic upgrade, which was announced by Education Minister Don McRae last week, for the entire school to be upgraded with state-of-the-art equipment and facilities. A brand new $50-million state-of-the-art high school was to be built in the Nanaimo-Ladysmith school district as part of a facilities plan that was approved by the Ministry of Education in 2008. As part of ongoing efforts to reduce excess space in the district due to declining enrolment, the plan called for the closure of both the aging Nanaimo District and Woodlands secondary schools in 2012, and the construction of the new high school on the Woodlands site that would have been up and running this year. The plan also included the much-needed seismic upgrades at Wellington school, but the old school board rejected the plan in 2009. The upgrades at Wellington were only resurrected from the ashes of that plan after a ministry-sponsored seismic survey of all the province's schools determined it to be a high safety risk in the event of a major earthquake. But Brennan said it's likely many aspects of the old plan will soon be back on the table, including the possible closure of NDSS and Woodlands that could be replaced with one new school, as well as the closure of many smaller schools in the district. Dr. Doug Player, a former superintendent of the West Vancouver school district, was hired by the district last month to coordinate the process in which the school board is expected to make a number of longanticipated decisions regarding its schools and other facilities later this year. The district has recently completed reports on its French Immersion program, transportation services and its overall strategic plan to assist in the decision-making process regarding facilities renewal. "I expect his recommendations will be pretty far-reaching and give us plenty of options to choose from," Brennan said. "The ministry asked us for a comprehensive plan for facilities renewal that looks at the needs of the whole district and I think we've done that. We should know where we should be focusing our attention on by this spring and decisions could be made during the next school year."
