CUPE LOCAL 606 (Mid-Island School Employees) PRESENTATION TO BOARD OF EDUCATION SCHOOL DISTRICT #68 (NANAIMO-LADYSMITH) REGARDING PROPOSED 2015-2016 BUDGET STRATEGIES APRIL 15, 2015 By Jeff Virtanen, Maintenance Service Caretaker Good evening Trustees, Senior Management and members of the audience, my name is Jeff Virtanen, and I am the maintenance service caretaker at Dover Bay Secondary School. I am here today to talk to you about the value myself, the other maintenance service caretakers, the emergency caretaker, the project crew, and the afternoon shift custodians all bring to the classroom. It’s not easy to quantify in dollars and cents what our value is to School District 68, but I will try. While most of our value is intangible, we also do have a large part in one of the revenue streams the school district has. Let’s start with the intangible pieces. In short, our students need somewhere clean to learn in. The sweeping of floors, taking out of garbage, the vacuuming of carpets, the cleaning and disinfecting of bathrooms; all this and so much more is needed in our schools. While I realize the cleaning of schools isn’t as sexy and shiny as fancy new learning programs or the newest technologies, the cleanliness of our schools cannot be underestimated in importance. CUPE Local 606 Jeff Virtanen, Maintenance Service Caretaker - Presentation to Board of Education April 15, 2015 – “Proposed 2015-16 Budget Strategies” 2 This is what makes senior management’s vision so confusing. We keep hearing about making cuts “outside the classroom”. I understand that concept, but guess who isn’t “outside the classroom?” The custodians! They are INSIDE the classroom, washing floors, cleaning countertops and desks, taking out the garbage, etc. None of this happens by itself. Our custodians are making sure our students and teachers have a clean and healthy environment to learn in. The cleaning of schools is only one level of our duties, though. Our custodians are the first line of security in our upward of 40 buildings. Our emergency caretaker rushes out to each and every one of the schools for glass clean up, vomit clean up, and a myriad of other duties that crop up on a daily basis. Our project crew shampoos carpets, change gym lights, upkeep our older floors and prep and varnish our well used gym floors. Our maintenance service caretakers do their best to hold down the fort in our high schools. Do you know how much havoc a thousand teenagers can cause in a building in an average day? As someone CUPE Local 606 Jeff Virtanen, Maintenance Service Caretaker - Presentation to Board of Education April 15, 2015 – “Proposed 2015-16 Budget Strategies” 3 who spends five days a week inside of one, I can speak on this with some authority. Believe me, the everyday wear and tear on a building housing over a 1,000 students and teachers is a ton. Another one of our many intangibles is that we are the eyes and ears of the over 40 buildings in our district. Who knows how many untold dollars have been saved because these employees are seeing and reporting issues before they become major? Without us being in every nook and cranny of our buildings, finding water leaks, unwanted rodents, catching building problems in their early stages, etc., the costs incurred would be astronomical. We don’t just save money, though, but we also help the district make money, too. One of the revenue streams the district uses is building and gym rentals when school is not in session. Guess who’s there for these rentals to let them in, to give them assistance if needed, to be the security for the school when these renters inevitably wander the hallways. Our custodians! The report showed that the district needed to hire four more custodians just to hit the provincial average. We are not naïve; we knew management would never hire four more caretakers. We did feel though, that this report showed the caretaking department was as lean as could be. We are staffed below the average level, but your proud employees do their best to keep the schools at an acceptable level of cleanliness for our students and teachers. It is disheartening when senior management feels a need to follow one consultant’s review word for word and bloat their own ranks within the board office, yet completely ignore another review simply because it doesn’t fit in with their “vision”. Worse than ignoring it, they even want to cut more. This way of thinking defies all common sense. Come this June, I will have been with School District 68 for over 21 years. This district has changed a lot since then.
