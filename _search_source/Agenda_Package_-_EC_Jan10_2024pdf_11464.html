Objective: • Increasing awareness and capacity in addressing mental health and wellness. Background In June 2019, the provincial government announced A Pathway to Hope, which laid out a 10-year vision for mental health and substance use care that provides people with the services they need in order to tackle problems early and support their wellbeing. It identifies the priority actions government will take to help people immediately and reduce demand for services down the road. The Ministry of Mental Health and Addictions (MMHA) worked in partnership with the Ministries of Children and Family Development (MCFD), Health and Education and Child Care, as well as with First Nations, Métis, and Inuit partner organizations to develop a suite of initiatives that target existing gaps in child, youth, and young adult mental health and substance use service delivery. One of the initiatives detailed in A Pathway to Hope is Integrated Child and Youth (ICY) Teams. The province is currently working to establish ICY teams in 20 school districts. These multidisciplinary teams will deliver wraparound mental health and substance use services and supports for children, youth, and their families/caregivers. In 2021, 5 districts began to implement ICY teams, with 7 additional districts added in 2023. Nanaimo-Ladysmith Public Schools is part of this second wave of implementation. Aside from Richmond, which was in the first wave, our community has received the largest allocation of ICY teams thus far, with 4 teams allocated. ICY teams serve children and youth 0-19 years of age in the Nanaimo Ladysmith area (this includes non-NLPS children and youth) who have identified mild, moderate, or severe mental health and/or substance use needs as defined by impairment or disruption in key areas of human development, and for whom existing community or school-based supports are not adequate. For youth who are already receiving youth mental health and/or substance use services from an ICY team, services may be available to age 21, based on unique needs, developmental considerations, transition plan, and best fit. 1|Page Page 6 of 25 ICY in NLPS Targeted funding for ICY was provided for the 2023-24 school year. An initial gathering of local community organizations who support children and youth, including our 5 Indigenous partner groups, took place in the spring of 2023 to consider ICY in Nanaimo-Ladysmith. On an ongoing basis, involved ministries meet at local employers’ table (LET) meetings, as well as provincial meetings that include all districts in the second wave of implementation. Kirstin Funke Robinson, Director of Instruction-Inclusive Education and Lynn Brown, Assistant Director-Complex Mental Health, represent NLPS at the LET and provincial ICY tables. 2|Page Page 7 of 25 NLPS has hired 4 Registered Clinical Counsellors (RCC) who will serve as part of the ICY teams. Clinical counsellors are distinct from school counsellors in that they provide direct clinical counselling support and therapy through an outreach/outbound service delivery model to clients. The NLPS RCCs are excluded staff who work 12 months per year at variable hours and supervise a Peer Support Worker (CUPE). Early in January 2024, we will be hiring 4 Peer Support Workers. These staff will have lived experience as a young person with mental health and/or substance use challenges. They will be responsible for non-clinical work to connect with children, youth, and their families/caregivers. Although the service delivery model is flexible and outbound, a central office space for ICY team members, including those from multiple ministries, is being created at the Dufferin Crescent site. At the present time, the referral pathway via multiple ministries is being created between LET members. In NLPS, initial student referrals are being collected centrally, through the Learning Services Support Team, as well as the Assistant Director of Complex Mental Health. ICY teams are expected to begin intake for referrals in mid-late January, 2024. The Program provide learners with disabilities and diverse abilities an opportunity to develop the skills and confidence needed for functional living. Life Skills is for learners who: • • • • • Are complex in multiple functional skills domains Require highly individualized support with functional skills Require 1:1 support from a support person at all times May require augmentative or alternative communication support Have complex behaviours or other needs which cannot be safely met in a neighbourhood school The decision for a student to attend the Life Skills program is made collaboratively between the parent, the team at the neighborhood school and the Director of Instruction – Inclusive Education. As schools work to be as inclusive and supportive as possible, children will not be directly enrolled in the Life Skills Program unless it has been determined that they require an exceptional level of support that their neighborhood school is unable to provide. This program is an educational option for those students who may struggle with learning in a regular classroom setting, as well as students who wish to take courses in addition to the scheduled timetable available at their enrolling high school. As well, community adults who have not yet graduated but are working towards their dogwood diploma may take courses that lead to graduation, under Ministry of Education and Child Care guidelines. Student enrolled with Island Connect Ed work towards the same curricular and core competencies as those in traditional schools, however, the distributed learning environment requires a partnership between students, teachers and parents where a continued cycle of participation, feedback and assessment ensures the students successfully navigate and complete the required course work. At Island Connect Ed, “connections” are a fundamental component of student success. Connection to a particular course, a teacher or learning assistant, another student, or an arranged activity ensures there is a positive link between the student and the school; staff at Island Connect Ed are focused on these connections in order to enhance student outcomes. Funding for Island Connect Ed comes from the Ministry of Education and Child Care via the Operating Grant, under Distributed Learning per pupil rates. These rates are marginally lower than traditional schools based per pupil rates due to the online (distributed/distant) learning platform where less staffing is required to support this type of educational delivery. Students in Kindergarten to Grade 9 are funded at 1.0 FTE, whereas students in grades 10 to 12 are funded for the number of courses taken at Island Connect Ed during the school year. Students may be cross enrolled and taking courses at one of the districts high schools, course funding applicable to a school is directed there to support the associated staffing. 12 Page 21 of 25 Island Connect Ed – Funding, Resource and Financial Summary Important to note that many students who take courses with Island Connect Ed are also enrolled and taking courses at their local high school. It is a program aimed at supporting students experiencing long-term school refusal, or who have not returned to school following the global pandemic. The team works in collaboration with school teams and is an extension of the services provided by schools. The goal is to create a continuum of wrap around support for students and families of learners with diverse needs. This includes students who require highly individualized supportive planning due to a specific diagnosis or developmental need, (e.g., students with a diagnosis of autism who are non-verbal and have complex behaviours in the school environment), or students who are experiencing significant barriers in attending school. There are three main areas of focus for this team: supporting students with complex learning needs, supporting students with complex behaviours, and system-wide capacity building and resource development. Services are accessed through referrals from school-based teams. The outreach services provided through this team are available for students in grades K-7, the capacity building and school-based consultive services are provided K-12. In addition to the intervention and capacity building the team provides for individual students, they are also engaged in broader work across the system. Our Inclusion Outreach Coordinators serve as district partners for the provincial outreach programs for autism (POPARD); fetal alcohol syndrome disorder (POPFASD) and inclusion outreach (PIOP). They provide site and student specific Non-violent Crisis Intervention Training in collaboration with the trainers who provide the larger-group opportunities, and they assist with the creation and curation of resources for supporting our learners with diverse abilities and disabilities. They also provide support and capacity building for staff in both of the District Life Skills programs. In 2022/23 the team processed referrals for approximately 192 students, spanning all 28 of our elementary schools, and 4/6 secondary schools. For 2023/24 there are currently 108 students receiving support from the Team. The Inclusion Outreach Team is funded from within the District Learning Services Budget. Ministry funding for the 108 participating students may flow to an enrolling school or enrolling program as applicable or consolidate into centralized district resources for allocation to a department, such as District Learning Services, based on departmental needs and priorities. 15 Page 24 of 25 Inclusion Outreach Team – Funding, Resource and Financial Summary 16 Page 25 of 25
