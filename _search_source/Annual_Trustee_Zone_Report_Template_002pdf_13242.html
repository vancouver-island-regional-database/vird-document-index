ANNUAL TRUSTEE ZONE REPORT Trustee Tom Rokeby Date: 2025/11/20 School Names/Zone: Cedar Secondary School, Cedar Elementary School, Cinnabar Elementary School Career Technical Centre, Island Connect Ed Annual Highlights: The zone of schools I was assigned for 2025 is a diverse family of schools. Schools in this zone are located across the district, as far north as the old Mount Benson Elementary site and as far south as Cedar Elementary School. Three schools share students in a traditional sense, with students from Cinnabar Elementary and Cedar Elementary moving after grade 7 to Cedar Secondary. This zone has also experienced significant organizational change roughly a decade ago with the closing of Woodbank and South Wellington elementary schools. Although numbers of students in these three schools are relatively low, the community has experienced a number of years of stability since the changes allowing healthy relationships and a sense of belonging to emerge. Cedar Secondary School keeps in contact with their community through Weekly Family Memos emailed home. Along with keeping parents abreast of significant academic and extra-curricular events, it also provides a wealth of information to families and students navigating supports in the larger community, and the district Wellness Van is a regular feature in the parking lot. In November, the Cedar community was invited to the school to hear a presentation from the White Hatter about raising kids that are savvy when it comes to artificial intelligence. Despite being school district 68's smallest secondary school, it still provides an array of athletic teams and clubs for students to develop their whole self. Volleyball wrapped up this year with Grade 8 girls placing third in the district and senior boys and girls earning 3rd and 4th at Islands. A group of fine arts students are raising funds to go to London England, and in the south end Country Grocer receipts are the go-to fundraising mechanism for PAC, as well as regular suppliers of fresh produce to our kids. In 2025, the Career Technical Centre (CTC) in School District 68 has solidified its reputation as a premier hub for hands-on, future-focused education. Building on its successful model, the CTC has continued to expand its program offerings, introducing new specialized streams in Health Services to directly align with emerging high-demand fields in the regional and global economy. Other program offerings provide students with industry-standard certifications and practical, projectbased learning experiences. Principal Dave Travers announced a Barrista and Cashier Certification program at 8:30 am, and by the time of my visit at 9:30 the program had 25 students signed up. A significant development this year has been the deepening of industry partnerships. The CTC has recently added a staff member to the team who is responsible for arranging work experience and apprenticeship opportunities. This position is paid for through ministry funding from the extra courses students take through the CTC. Furthermore, the centre’s partnership with Vancouver Island University has grown, with an increased number of dual-credit courses now offering direct articulation into corresponding diploma and degree programs. The core mission remains steadfast: to provide every student with a direct, tangible pathway to a rewarding career that reflects their passion and purpose, and in 2025, the CTC is successfully equipping a new generation of skilled, work-ready graduates. Page 2 This year, Island Connect Ed (ICE) in School District 68 has continued to solidify its role as a vital and dynamic hub for distributed learning. As a program of choice, ICE has focused on enhancing flexibility and personalization for its diverse student body, which includes full-time online learners, home-school support students, and brick and mortar students from other district schools seeking specific course options. Pedagogically, the program has continued to refine its blended learning model, combining robust online platforms with meaningful face-to-face opportunities. This year saw a strong return of popular in-person events and workshops, fostering a crucial sense of community and connection among students and staff. Thursdays is club days at the school, which gives online students a regular opportunity to join their peers in extra-curricular pursuits. Teachers have worked diligently to curate and create engaging digital content, while also providing targeted, one-on-one support to ensure student success in the virtual environment. Embracing the district's goals, ICE has also strengthened its integration of Indigenous perspectives and worldviews across its curriculum. Ultimately, Island Connect Ed's ongoing mission this year has been to provide a high-quality, responsive, and inclusive educational pathway that empowers students to thrive in a non-traditional setting. While this zone of schools has numerous pathways to graduation, be it brick and mortar at Cedar Secondary, distributed learning at Island Connect Ed or a trades-oriented graduation plan through the Career Technical Centre, it only has two elementary schools. Cedar Elementary serves a mix of rural and residential families and is located on a massive 17 acre lot, The outdoor facilities at Cedar elementary feature prominently in a students day, including scheduled classes for all students in the adjoining Addison Way Park. At Cedar Elementary School, Timberwolves strive to be peaceful problem solvers, aware and responsible, cooperative, and kind. Each month the staff selects a different value on which to focus. During my recent visit, the students had just completed an amazing “haunted hallway” to celebrate Hallowe'en, and were building gingerbread houses and candy canes to decorate candy cane lane, leading up to the Winter Concert on December 4th. All of these activities are successsful because of the great staff spirit in the school. I witnessed numerous classes teaching and learning together, and a very supportive and collegial team approach to caring for students and the school. Cinnabar Valley Elementary continues to be a vibrant and engaged learning community within School District 68. This year, the school has deepened its commitment to place-based and outdoor learning, frequently utilizing its natural surroundings to enrich curriculum. Every classroom at Cinnabar has an exterior door to encourage outdoor time. A key focus has been on strengthening foundational literacy and numeracy skills through targeted interventions and collaborative teaching strategies, ensuring all students build a strong academic base. On a recent visit, I met with a coteacher who is able to help individual students with targeted interventions, and the two Kindergarten teachers have their students working together from day 1. They taught me about boomerang lunches...not as messy as it sounds. The school has also been actively promoting community and inclusiveness. Recent events like the Terry Fox Run and literacy nights have strengthened the partnership between home and school. Furthermore, Cinnabar Valley has embraced the district's dedication to Truth and Reconciliation, with students and staff participating in meaningful projects and lessons that integrate Indigenous knowledge and worldviews. Extra-curricular student leadership initiatives and clubs, such as coding and garden club, remain popular, fostering a strong sense of belonging. Staff have also initiated a well-attended book club for teachers. As a small, community-oriented school, Cinnabar Valley prides itself on knowing every child well, ensuring a supportive and personalized educational experience where each student is empowered to thrive academically and socially.
