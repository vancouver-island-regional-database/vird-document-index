INTRODUCTION The purpose of this study is to assess the feasibility of developing a new FAC facility on the 505 Howard Avenue Property, currently occupied by the Harewood Elementary School gym, the school’s classroom wing having been demolished. The principal issue addressed in this study is site fit – whether there is potential on the site to develop the facility program identified for proposed District Administration Centre (DAC) replacement in a viable way. The site is 1.17 acres in size and is triangular in shape. See Figure 1 below. Figure 1: Aerial View of Study Site 7.2 Page 31 of 69 CORNERSTONE architecture SD68 DAC Site Analysis – Howard Avenue Property 2. FACILITY PROGRAM The table opposite summarizes the current facility program for the proposed new DAC facility (Cornerstone Planning Group, January 2014). In overview, the program is for a district office building of approximately 2,550 gsm plus parking for 80 vehicles and a service vehicle loading bay. Figure 2: Facility Program Summary: The diagram opposite illustrates the relative size of the DAC program components and high-level spatial relationships among them. The diagram does not specifically consider the distribution of functions between floors in a multi-storey building; this would be the determined ultimately through a schematic design process. INITIAL OBSERVATIONS The current site zoning designation is CS1: Community Service One that would appear to allow the DAC, not necessarily as an outright, but almost certainly as a site specific use. The outright required setbacks are 6m for the front yard, 4m for side yards, and 7.5m for the rear yard. These could be increased subject to the classification of the flanking roads. Lot coverage is limited to 40% and height to 14m. The site has little slope and from the point of view of traffic, it appears that Howard Avenue would present the best site frontage for the main entrance to DAC for both vehicles and pedestrians, although secondary access from the other sides is likely to be advantageous. The site would provide good community visibility for the DAC. The existing building on site is a one-storey gym, the 3-story classroom wing of the Harewood School having been demolished earlier in 2016. An initial idea to be studied is whether a viable strategy could entail retaining the gym wing and upgrading it to accommodate part of the DAC program. From a very basic analysis, we conclude that this would not be an optimum development approach for reasons that include the following (also see figure 4 following): • • • The gym facility is approximately 430 gsm with most of the area over-height. The proposed DAC Boardroom, that could benefit from this large volume space, is less than half this area, so, while it would be possible to insert infill floors, there is not an obvious fit between what is required and what is afforded in retaining the existing gym structure. Our experience suggests there is rarely a clear cost-benefit advantage to retaining an existing structure of this kind (fifty year old wood-frame) unless: there is significant heritage value; there are planning approval or site constraint issues favouring retention; or there is a very close functional fit and a relatively straight forward upgrade scope of work. None of these conditions appear to apply here, and it is likely that the result would be a development characterized by unequal quality of facilities and compromised functionality. Finally, if any cost saving on the gross facility space itself could be realized, in the best of circumstances it is unlikely to be more than, say, one-third of the cost of new construction (and this is optimistic given current building code requirements for seismic, accessibility, building envelope and environmental performance, etc.). For illustration, if we assume new development project costs are in the order of $3,000/gsm, then the maximum/optimistic benefit to retaining/upgrading the gym might be $430,000, In the analysis illustrated in figure 4, it appears that even if the new development was in a three-storey form, the site size and shape of new development would dictate the need for underground parking – likely in the order of 30-35 of the 80 spaces. At $35,000 per underground space, this is likely to increase development costs by over a $million. Therefore, we conclude that keeping the gym wing could be expected to increase the project cost and deliver a less functional development, and is not advisable. The question that follows is whether there is a viable form of (new) development on the site that meets the program and avoids the requirements for underground parking. This is the subject of the final report section. 7.2 Page 33 of 69 CORNERSTONE architecture SD68 DAC Site Analysis – Howard Avenue Property Figure 4: Simplified Diagrammatic Illustration of Site Fit Challenge - Superimposition of DAC and Parking Development Program onto Howard Avenue Site with Retention of Gym Structure  7.2 Page 34 of 69 CORNERSTONE architecture SD68 DAC Site Analysis – Howard Avenue Property 4. SITE FIT – POTENTIAL NEW DEVELOPMENT This section illustrates a possible form of new development on the site. Note that this is essentially a preliminary concept massing diagram; some character has been added to give a sense of how a building would “feel” on the site, but this should not be interpreted as an actual architectural design. In this test concept, the DAC building is located in the north corner of the site in a two-storey, roughly triangular form; the parking, entirely surface parking, is configured on the south side of the site. The main access is situated on the Howard Avenue side, although such a development would also have prominent visibility from Harewood Road. The illustrated Main floor has a footprint of 1,100 m2. Assuming a Basement level, that could vary in size depending on opportunity and project budget, but is likely to be at least 200 gsm, the Upper floor would need to be at least 1,200 to 1,250 gsm. As illustrated, this is reflected in the upper level extending over a small area of surface parking and proving a covered arrival area. Through an actual architectural design process, the specifics of this massing might be modified/simplified, for example, by assuming: a larger basement area or a small third level, by reducing the site setbacks, or by tightening up the parking configuration, etc. The DAC will be a more functional building if developed on no more than two floors (plus a basement). In the illustrated concept, the Boardroom, Finance and Human Resources departments, and Reception are located on the Main level, with the Executive Group, Learning Services, and Info Services (less certain functions that are assumed to be in the Basement) are located on the Upper level. Common facilities, such as meeting rooms, are distributed. This arrangement balances the area requirements within the massing concept, but other options could be investigated through an iterative design process. The potential for future expansion has not specifically been assessed; however, options would be available to construct the DAC with provisions to support a third storey addition, and/or to plan such that the parking area could be developed, at which time structured parking would be required. As noted the above, the detail of the massing could be modified, however, a viable development will require a site footprint approximately of the size illustrated. The feasibility of establishing this development potential will depend on City approval for the use, the overall form of development, and particularly the site setbacks – the illustrated concept does encroach somewhat on the zoning bylaw minimums. Assuming City approval could be obtained, the conclusion reached in this study is that the Howard Avenue property is a viable site to consider for developing a new DAC. Final Note: This study does not include a cost analysis. A cost analysis was prepared for the studies undertaken in 2014 and, in updated form, could be applied to this site option – there is nothing in the analysis to this point to suggest there would be any unusual cost factors associated with developing the Howard Avenue site compared to any other likely site. Although as a note of caution, to our knowledge neither a geotechnical nor a site contamination study has been undertaken.
