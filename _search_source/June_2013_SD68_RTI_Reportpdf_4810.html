1 THE BOARD OF EDUCATION OF SCHOOL DISTRICT 68 TO: Board of Education FROM: Bob Esliger, Assistant Superintendent – Learning Services DATE: June 28, 2013 RE: Update on Response to intervention (RTI) Introduction Response to Intervention (RTI) is a district-wide approach to teaching and learning that combines high quality instruction with a tiered system of interventions for children who need additional help in their learning. The main reason Nanaimo-Ladysmith Public Schools is focusing its attention on RTI at this time is its promise to provide an extraordinary education for all students regardless of which school they attend in the district. RTI is not a “thing” or a “program” that can be purchased; rather, it is a method or way of doing things. RTI provides a common structured mechanism for special and regular education staff to work together in ways that increases the accuracy of special education designations and the number of students who respond positively to regular education curriculum (Witt, 2006). Despite the anticipated positive results of our RTI model, it is no surprise that implementation has posed a number of challenges. This report will provide trustees with an update regarding our RTI initiatives, suggest changes required in policy and practice as we move forward, and offer suggestions regarding how our school leaders can manage the changes that RTI brings to the different levels of our system and the many stakeholders involved. Attributes of RTI The phrase Response to Intervention (RTI) stems from the premise that successful teaching and learning depends on close observation of the student’s response to the tiered interventions integral to RTI, and the subsequent development of additional interventions as required for those students who are not responding as expected. Formative assessments (also known as progress monitoring) serve as the foundation of our RTI model because they enable educators to carefully measure a student’s success in mastering the BC curriculum. Knowing whether a child is on track towards mastering essential grade level outcomes is a key attribute of RTI. This proactive monitoring empowers educators to develop effective, personalized, instructional, enrichment, and remedial interventions. RTI requires collaboration between all classroom teachers, administration and support staff. In RTI, this collaboration requires everyone to adopt new ways of thinking, collaborating, and acting together as a team to support all learners. Additionally, it may mean that educators need to assume new identities that are not based on their role descriptions or funding source, but rather how their professional expertise can best help students meet the learning outcomes. Many aspects of the work that is included in the RTI framework were also included in the previous work of Project Success. Teachers who have been engaged in previous innovation and leadership activities in their schools may now be reluctant to engage in the RTI process as it may be viewed that all previous work is not recognized as having been important or successful. It is unfortunate that the district wasn’t more celebratory 2 in acknowledging the hard work and successes of Project Success and building upon those initiatives. An apology is extended to those educators involved in Project Success and a sincere thank you is also extended for the work accomplished. In moving forward, it is hoped that a great deal of that work that will continue and become more widespread in the district as critical elements of our Tier 1 instructional practices. The point being made here is that the RTI framework requires that basic structures and procedures be established in all district schools and not simply left to the willing. Every one of our learners, in every single classroom, deserves the opportunity to learn in innovative, collaborative environments. We must continue the important work of scaling up our most effective practices on a more district wide basis. Many schools have reported that they have teacher book clubs going for the past few years where staff (teachers and principals) study and discuss RTI, poverty, a teaching intervention, or an aspect of literacy. This is to be celebrated and continued while at the same time extended to include teacher team time to review student data and school-wide interventions for students who are not responding to instruction. These teams have been referred to as Professional Learning Communities (PLCs) and the district is discussing various means through which to provide this necessary teacher team time. Add a few sentences highlighting opportunities for staff including on-going in-service for principals, SST’s, in-service presentations at requested schools, etc. Successes and Challenges Reported As one would expect, most staff believe that the philosophy behind the RTI model is solid. The move to RTI as a district has already provided for greater sharing of professional skills and a wider exploration of possible techniques, strategies and interventions for students. The addition of the Instructional Coordinator role on the Learning Support Services Teams (LSSTs) has made it possible for more in-depth conversations about instruction and student program observations in addition to multidisciplinary collaborative planning for students who are struggling in their learning. In addition, School-Based Teams (S-BTs) are developing an intervention model and moving away from the assessment model with a growing focus on all team members being in classrooms in order to work directly with students and educators. Conversations about “the data” are also beginning to drive the S-BT decision-making process contributing to more timely next steps for students. Schools that have previously adopted the Positive Behaviour Interventions and Supports Model (PBIS) reflected that they feel as though they have had a jump-start on the concept of a tiered approach to interventions and are more fully aware of the need to “teach to the green” first and then go beyond the universal tier as identified by student needs. An example of positive change is when the district’s self-regulated learning consultant worked with a classroom teacher and LSST members to transform the classroom into a self-regulated learning space. This was done through close collaboration with the teacher and resulted in reducing her stress and fatigue, creating a safe space for a highly anxious student by providing him with a suitable place to stay in the classroom, and has helped students identify what they need to be “calm, alert and learning”. The teacher felt as though her concerns were acknowledged and validated and is now pleased to be moving forward with some new ideas and insight and ongoing collegial support when required. Another elementary principal reported that she has witnessed a change in student behaviour when the kindergarten teacher addressed the universal needs of the class first and then worked on behavioural interventions with specific students. Student behaviour problems, such as difficulty paying attention and following directions, difficulties with completing a task, and problems cooperating with peers could lead to deficits in academic success if interventions are not implemented in a timely manner. This principal noted that early assessment and intervention in kindergarten must occur at both the class-wide instructional level and individual student level in order to plan and implement interventions early and then to monitor and respond accordingly. To understand the challenges involved in implementing this RTI initiative it is helpful to review the areas affected by RTI at both the district and school levels. Regardless of where staff work in Nanaimo-Ladysmith Public Schools, they will become involved in this change. As superintendent, Mr. Dave Hutchinson stated, the strategic plan will bring about continuous system-wide improvement of academic achievement through the supportive structure of RTI as we strive to meet each student’s unique needs. Therefore, staff are required to learn new skills and knowledge, and confront new values; hence, this change begins to take on second-order implications as identified in the Table 1. As anticipated, some staff are hesitant to make any quick changes while those who have embraced the RTI framework are happy with the outcomes. Increasingly, school staff are inviting the ICs for conversations or inviting them for a classroom consultation. Other educators have agreed to incorporate the new LEXIA reading program as an intervention and are considering or utilizing different assessment strategies with their 4 students. More and more educators are reaching out to the S-BT and the LSST for both individual and classroom support for their most vulnerable students. However slowly or rapidly this change is taking place, there has been a noted positive change for those teachers and their students. In addition, some educators have picked up a few new ideas after visiting the demonstration classroom at Seaview Elementary and discussing possible interventions for their classrooms. Upon close examination of these challenges it becomes clear that more communication and more inservice and professional development are required. LSSTs were created to assist schools in building the internal capacity of all staff to meet the unique learning needs of all students. Therefore, they cannot become the individuals who are responsible for providing the interventions, but instead, they can co-teach, co-plan and co-monitor so that educators don’t have to feel alone in the process and can feel supported in implementing strategies and interventions that are new to them. Implementation Year 2012-13 Perhaps the term “year zero” wasn’t the best choice in naming this planning and preparation year, but it was truly meant to signify a “readiness year”. As referenced by Witt (2006), the implementation of RTI will take us through four phases: (1) readiness, (2) training, (3) resources, and (4) policy and procedures. In our readiness year (phase 1) we considered the knowledge level of all management staff on RTI, assessment, balanced literacy etc. Efforts have focused on building principal/vice-principal awareness about the district’s RTI implementation plan. On February 1st, 2012 the district provided initial preparation to all 1800 staff on RTI Overview, the importance of maintaining a respectful workplace, and self-regulated learning. Having a “readiness year” meant that the district could work toward putting the initial structures in place so that they model had a strong foundation to be built upon. Many teachers have had promising experiences with various components of the RTI process while others can honestly state that they know little about and have not been impacted by it. This reaction, eight months into our RTI model, should be seen as ok and not a sign that RTI is not working. A challenge that became apparent in the fall was that the district was not getting information to classroom teachers in a regular manner. This remains a challenge. However, a Learning Services portal was created for RTI where forms, documents, research and a FAQ is located. This portal is updated on a regular basis. Initially RTI became synonymous with self-regulation, the demonstration classroom, Instructional Coordinators, classroom furniture, wall colour, and LSSTs. These types of misunderstandings can be viewed as a normal response and indicative of systems change. However, this is also a signal that the district needs to provide further communication to educators in schools; this is a scheduled part of the implementation plan and will begin in August 2013. Assistant superintendents and members of their LSSTs visited their schools and met with the full staff to deliver an RTI presentation and have a discussion. The superintendent sent out a memo to all educators explaining RTI and the processes involved; all principals received a similar memorandum. February 1, 2013 was termed the “Success for All” day and was a very successful endeavour in terms of providing a common language all 1800 employees in the district. In the 2012-13 school year the district hired a consultant with expert knowledge in self-regulated learning to assist all educators and support staff with infusing current research on learning theory into their lessons and classrooms. There are many success stories developing from this important work including classroom makeovers where learning environments have been drastically adapted/modified to reflect current theory in learning environments in relation to student learning. However, suggesting changes to practice to this degree does not come without inherent challenges due to misconceptions and/or miscommunication. However, self- 5 regulation continues to be seen by many as an important component of student learning which includes a greater emphasis on metacognitive processes. It is a given that RTI will require everyone to develop new understandings and incorporate new strategies. During the training phase (phase 2) (scheduled to begin in August 2013 with the Summer Institute), the district will provide awareness training for educators and then follow-up, job-embedded training for an extended period of time that will allow educators to understand RTI and all of its components at a deep level. The goal is for educators to move past the defensive stage when discussing students who are not responding to instruction and develop a stronger link between assessment and instruction; thereby, examining their practice alone or with a colleague. Although this phase of RTI is not scheduled to begin until August 2013 many schools have taken it upon themselves to continue with the school initiatives that they had underway that fit with RTI and have requested inservice on the aspects of RTI that are new to them. Therefore, during this implementation year many schools have accessed school district expertise to initiate or continue with staff development on school-based initiatives that include, but not limited to, inquiry, assessment, community of learners, DMA, and DART. To ensure implementation of RTI, in phase 3 the district will provide resources that will assist schools with student screening, monitoring of progress, selecting and implementing interventions, and managing data. When implementation began, the district had been recently reorganized and the LSSTs developed. This new structure caused some initial confusion which the district continues to work through. School-based staff and School-Based Team members were initially unclear as to how to access the district level team (LSST) and some still remain unclear. Feedback from schools indicates that some of the new processes, in the fall, took too long and that scheduling the necessary parties to come together was barrier to service delivery. As such, there was an initial reluctance to refer students to the LSST level of intervention as the perception was that “it will take too long for something to happen.” As we progressed through the fall and into 2013 the LSSTs have worked through many of these process and procedural issues. The “old way” had a form for everything and a lengthy paperwork trail. The “new way” has reduced the paperwork and provided schools with the ability to phone or email members of their LSST for consultation and/or support. In addition, no forms are necessary to activate initial classroom support. There is a determination regarding paperwork as the process unfolds; e.g. if an itinerant staff member needs to remain involved for an extended period of time. Schools are reporting that processes are becoming clearer. Another principal reported that their S-BT discussions this year are more productive than ever and are now more student focused than ever before; they feel that the district is moving in the right direction. In phase 4, as implementation progresses and final processes and procedures are developed, the district will develop and/or revise policies that provide further guidance about assessment practices, selection of interventions, fidelity of implementation, movement through tiers of intervention, special education eligibility determination, and the timeline and sequence of procedures. Nanaimo-Ladysmith Public Schools has a history of merging funding from special education, general education and special purpose funds to best meet the needs of all students. This school year the district has spent time during this “readiness year” to reorganize roles, create a Department of Learning Services, and promote closer working relationships between itinerant staff (SLPs, school psychologists, CYFSWs, elementary counselors etc.) in classrooms. As evidenced in the feedback received from schools, there are definite growing-pains as we work through these structural changes, but the benefits to student learning clearly outweigh the systemic challenges. Traditional special education labels and categories should become less important in schools as staff internalize the reality that services will materialize based upon student need. There will always be a small 6 number of students who are severely disabled and excluded from formal assessments; however, all students, regardless of their disability status, will be held to the high learning standard. Under the RTI model, the specific reasons that students are not responding to instruction and/or interventions is of interest primarily as a lens for considering differentiated instructional strategies. Consequently, the success of RTI (and, ultimately, of all students) depends on a collaborative approach to teaching and learning supported by everyone in the school. Classroom teachers are held responsible for the learning of all students, not just those regarded as normal or typical. Student who have special needs are the responsibility of the classroom teacher to whom they are assigned, and school support teachers (SSTs) must use their skills to assist their colleagues by helping to adapt instruction in order to help students meet the learning outcomes of the regular education curriculum. The most often cited challenge from the field at present is the apparent lack of communication from the district to teachers in schools – on many levels. School staff have questioned how the LSST can discuss a referral when LSST members don’t know anything about the student referred. Once again, this type of statement is flawed because before a student can be referred to the LSST one or more LSST members (itinerant staff) need to have been involved with the school-team and student in a problem-solving capacity. If no one on the LSST knows anything about the student being referred then it means that the S-BT has not connected with their itinerant staff and asked for assistance. This type of assistance from LSST members can be accessed via a phone call or email. This simplified process is to make itinerant staff more accessible to schools. As stated earlier, when all resources and expertise have been exhausted or it is determined that an LSST member needs to spend some concentrated time at a particular school, a referral is made to the LSST and a plan is developed to provide the necessary support(s) to the school team and/or classroom. Under RTI, there is a much greater emphasis on finding the right pathways for students to learn. Principals and teachers will need to find ways to redefine roles and develop collaborative methods for analyzing problems and finding solutions. As was stated at the beginning of this report, RTI is not a “thing” or a “program” that can be purchased and implemented; rather, it is a method, a belief structure and/or way of doing things. RTI provides a common structured mechanism for all staff to work together to provide a high quality education for all students. This is what we have professed to believe since the beginning of time; however, Response To Intervention challenges us to use a consistent framework, district-wide, to address the important work that we do with all children. Conclusion Some schools have reported that there does not appear to be 100% buy in from staff and this makes it difficult to move forward with this initiative. This is not a surprising comment to hear, from a district perspective, as we have only just begun. In fact, the implementation of RTI has become more widespread than the district’s implementation team thought possible in this “year zero”. Our work now is to focus on our school leaders and classroom teachers and strengthen their understanding of RTI and how the process can support all learners. RTI is not a “top down” driven initiative by the district – it is a lens through which we conduct our professional practice as we work to support student learning at a high level. Finding the time to collaborate or will become increasingly more important as we move forward with our RTI process and reflect on student learning. In summary, RTI is hard work and it requires everyone to change, sometimes in significant ways. The only agenda with Response To Intervention (RTI) is to support student learning at high levels.
