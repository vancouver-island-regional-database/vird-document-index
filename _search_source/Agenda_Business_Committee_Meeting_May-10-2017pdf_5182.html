Background: By Board motion R16/03/30-16 (motion included at the end, for reference), staff was requested to provide the cost, including associated travel and accommodation costs, and benefits of membership in the BC School Trustees Association. The Board also asked for alternative options for Trustees to consider. The Cost: The cost of NLPS being a member of the BC School Trustees Association is made up of a number of components. First of all is the membership fee itself, which for 2015-2016 was approximately $46,600. In 2016-2017, the membership fee was some $46,900. The cost of attending the BCSTA Annual General Meeting and professional development events must be factored into the total cost of membership. Those events vary by year. For example in early 2015, the focus was training for newly elected school trustees. In 2015 the cost of trustees and senior staff attending BCSTA functions was approximately $16,700; the amount for 2016 was $10,000. The final cost of being a member of the BCSTA is the cost of participating in Vancouver Island School Trustees Association (VISTA) events. VISTA is a branch organization of BCSTA. In 2015, the cost of attending VISTA events was $1,300 while in 2016 the cost was $2,000. In summary, the cost of participating in the BC School Trustees Association was $64,600 in 2015 and $58,900 in 2016. The Benefits: BCSTA’s mission is to provide support to member boards of education and to advocate on behalf of those boards. Page 9 of 32 10.1 BCSTA is recognized as the voice of local boards of education and is influential in shaping Government’s education policy. The executive has direct access to the Minister of Education and senior Ministry staff. Examples of recent advocacy efforts are:  response to Government’s plans to change the structure of BCPSEA, resulting in the BCSTA providing the vehicle for local boards of education to have input into the negotiations during teacher and support staff bargaining. BCSTA developed plans to provide the day-to-day services BCPSEA currently provides to individual boards of education in the event Government disbanded BCPSEA;  response to the Ministry’s changes to how capital and seismic projects are funded, resulting in increasing the level of understanding of district surpluses and retaining local decision making control;  response to the Supreme Court ruling on class size and composition, developed the Stability for Students Action Plan, advocating for stability for students, for reaching a bargained resolution to the class size and composition issue and for provincial funding for the implementation of the decision;  along with partner groups, participating in Ministry of Education and Government committees regarding a variety of initiatives affecting K-12 education, as the only voice of local boards of education; and,  ongoing dialogue with the Minister of Education and senior Ministry staff regarding matters affecting K-12 education. Services provided to member school districts include:  regular communication with member boards regarding new and developing matters affecting K-12 education;  Board Chairs’ discussion forum;  access to the joint BCSTA/BCTF group life insurance program;  access to BCSTA developed legal and policy-related publications which are used by districts to develop their own policies;  access to BCSTA’s in-house legal counsel for advice on legal, policy and governance matters at no additional cost to member districts;  professional development services for trustees of member districts; and,  maintains database of individual district policies, accessible to member districts. Alternatives: 1. Cancel NLPS membership in the BC School Trustees Association. The district would be able to reallocate the funds currently spent on membership and related expenses. The District would no longer have access to the benefits of the association unless they were purchased elsewhere or undertaken by district staff. (Note – March 31 is the deadline for a board to notify BCSTA of its decision to withdraw from the association for it to be effective on July 1 in the same year.) Page 10 of 32 10.1 2. Renew NLPS membership in BCSTA and reduce associated costs by: a. not attending any BCSTA or VISTA events; b. reducing the number of trustees and staff who attend BCSTA and VISTA events; or, c. reducing the cost of those attending BCSTA and VISTA events by using shared accommodation, carpooling or walking on the ferry and using public transit. 3. Renew NLPS membership in BCSTA and continue the current practice of staff and trustees attending BCSTA and VISTA events. Page 11 of 32 10.1 BOARD OF EDUCATION Nanaimo Ladysmith Public Schools BUSINESS COMMITTEE MEETING ACTION SHEET DATE: April 12, 2017 TO: Business Committee FROM: Secretary-Treasurer, Carrie McVeigh SUBJECT: Reducing the Number of Trustees and Potential Ward System Recommendation: That the Education Committee recommends the Board of Education of School District No. 68 (Nanaimo-Ladysmith) receive the report dated April 12, 2017, on reducing the number of trustees and potential ward system. Background: By Board motion R16/03/30-13 (motion included at the end, for reference), staff was requested to report on: a. the necessary steps to reduce the Board from nine to seven trustees; b. estimate the savings by reducing the number of trustees from nine to seven; c. trustee stipends; and, d. options for a ward system. Steps to Reduce the Number of Trustees on the Board The Act stipulates that 3, 5, 7 or 9 trustees will make up a board of education. The Minister, by order, determines the number of trustees for each board. The ministry has published “Trustee Variation Guidelines” which sets out how a board requests a change to the number of trustees and what information must be included with such a request. In summary, the Board would need to provide a rationale for the change in the number of trustees and then hold some sort of public consultation to seek the views of the public on the proposed change. As well, the Board would need to reach out to municipal governments, the regional district, First Nations and special interest groups to seek their opinions on the proposed change to the number of trustees. Once that is completed, the Board would need to write to the Minister requesting the change, outlining the rationale and a summary of consultations held. While there is no legislated date by which the proposal would need to be submitted; upon enquiry, Ministry staff have advised that the Board would need to receive the letter by October 1, 2017. Page 12 of 32 10.1 Cost Savings of Reducing the Number of Trustees Each trustee is currently paid an annual stipend of $16,342 per year. As well as the stipend paid to trustees, other expenses are incurred by the district for each trustee. For the last two years, those costs have averaged out to $6,950 per year per trustee. So in total, the cost savings by reducing the number of trustees from nine to seven would be approximately $46,600 per year. Trustee Stipends The subject of trustee stipends was also the subject of a separate resolution passed on August 31, 2016. Response to the trustee stipends request will be presented on an Action Sheet for that resolution rather than duplicating it in this document. Options for Ward System One option for establishing a ward system is particularly applicable if it is felt that a geographic area has historically been under-represented on the Board. This option would entail identifying the geographic area and have one trustee to be elected from that area alone. The remainder of the trustees would be elected from the remainder of the district. One pitfall with this approach is that it may create a disproportional representation. For example, if the under-represented area had a population of 5,000 people, there would be one trustee representing them and eight trustees representing the remaining 121,000 people living in the rest of NLPS boundaries, which works out to about 15,000 per trustee. If the number of trustees was reduced to six, it would be about 20,000 people per trustee in the remaining area. Another option is to divide the district into nine separate wards with about the same number of residents in each ward. Using current populations that would be about 14,000 people per ward and about 18,000 per ward if the number of trustees was reduced to seven. In order to keep the population numbers about the same in each ward, the district should review the ward boundaries periodically to determine whether or not they need to be adjusted. A third option is to divide the district into fewer than nine wards (or fewer than seven wards if the number of trustees is reduced) and have two trustees elected from wards with the greatest population. A fourth option is a hybrid between at-large and ward elections for trustees. The district would be divided into fewer than nine wards (or fewer than seven wards if the number of trustees is reduced) and have one trustee elected from each ward. That would mean that one or more trustees would be elected at-large. The final option is to leave trustees to be elected at-large, as is the current practice. Process for changing to a ward system The first step to implement a ward system would be for the Board to articulate the rationale for such a change. In other words, identify the problem and how the proposed changes will address the concerns. Following that, the Board would need to identify the ward or wards proposed and the number of trustees to represent each ward. A professional surveyor would need to prepare maps and metes and bounds descriptions of the new wards. The Board would also have to estimate the population in each proposed new ward. Page 13 of 32 10.1 Following that, the Board would need to undertake public consultations and reach out to other organizations in the same way as described above for reducing the number of trustees. Logically, the consultations for reducing the number of trustees to be elected and establishing new wards would be combined into one process. Changing the number of trustees and creating or altering wards are both covered in the “Trustee Variation Guidelines.” Finally, the Board would need to write to the Minister requesting the change, outlining the rationale and a summary of the nature of the consultations held. Comparison to Other Districts Attached to this Action Sheet is a table showing the remuneration data for four other school districts. All of the districts have student populations that are comparable to the number of students enrolled in NLPS. In addition to the remuneration given to Chairs, Vice-Chairs and Trustees, the table shows the other support provided to elected officials as well the method each district uses to review or increase the remuneration paid. None of the districts listed have a different rate of pay for committee chairs. It is interesting to note that two districts (three including NLPS) base increases on the Consumer Price Index. One district bases increases on a weighted average of increases given to their employee groups in the previous year. One district reviews trustee remuneration and, if an increase is approved, the amount of the increase is prescribed in policy. That district has not increased trustee remuneration since the December 2014 inaugural meeting. Page 18 of 32 10.1 Discussion It is awkward for a board to increase its own remuneration. Critics will accuse a board who grants themselves an increase in pay of being self-serving and diverting funds from the classroom. Other critics will maintain that candidates run for office out of a sense of community service and should not be paid at all. Supporters will maintain that there needs to be fair compensation for those individuals who are willing to serve in public office. Some locally elected boards resolve this conflict by setting the rate of pay for the next board in advance of the election process. Other boards look to retired trustees or elected officials in other organizations, municipalities for example, for recommendations on trustee remuneration. Still other organizations base the remuneration for their own elected officials on what their peer organizations pay their elected officials. One thing that creates a problem is to not address the issue of remuneration for years on end. It is very difficult for a board to then justify a “catch-up” increase in pay. Review of Existing Policy Policy 2.6 Governance and Management contains the existing NLPS policy dealing with trustee remuneration. Section 7 of the policy states: 7.1 Under Section 71 of the School Act, a Board may authorize the payment of remuneration to be paid to Trustees. The Income Tax Act allows part of this remuneration to be declared as a tax-free expense allowance. 7.1.1 Trustees remuneration is indexed to the cost of living each September. There are no Procedures accompanying this policy. New Policy To amend this policy so that the remuneration of trustees is reviewed annually based on comparable districts, the following wording change would meet the intent of the Board’s motion: Replace existing 7.1.1 with “Trustee remuneration is to be reviewed by the Board in September in each year; such review is to be based on remuneration paid to trustees of comparable British Columbia School Districts.” An Administrative Procedure would not be required to implement this policy.
