There have been a number of notable improvements in Nanaimo Ladysmith Public Schools’ achievement data that deserve acknowledgement and recognition. Our Six-Year Completion Rate for “All Students” is maintaining an upward trend in the number of students graduating from our school system. Maintaining this trend in improved student achievement is indicative of the high expectations that we have set for our district related to the Board of Education’s vision of Success for All. In the last decade, the district has specifically focussed its attention on advancing the learning of students who have traditionally underperformed in our schools as compared to the provincial norms. Currently, more than 15 percent of our student population is of Aboriginal ancestry and 10 percent of our population includes students with special needs. Our data indicates that there has been significant student achievement growth within both of these populations again this year. Challenging Areas What trends and evidence in student achievement are of concern to you? Although there have been some significant gains that should be acknowledged, there are specific areas that need greater attention. The Early Years Data from the district’s Early Development Indicators (EDI) instrument from the UBC initiative, the Human Early Learning Partnership, has demonstrated that a significant number of our students entering Kindergarten are vulnerable in one or more categories of the assessment. Overall, 34% of Kindergarten students in 2011 were vulnerable on one or more of the following measures: physical, social, emotional, language, and communications. Looking closely at our different neighbourhoods, we see our highest vulnerability rates in neighbourhoods where there is socioeconomic disadvantage. And yet, despite the much lower vulnerability rate in more populous north Nanaimo, there are actually a greater number of developmentally-vulnerable children than in our most vulnerable neighbourhoods. We recognize a critical need to both provide universal access to supports and services, and to reduce barriers to more marginalized families and children in high-risk communities. Response to Intervention (RTI) Framework Nanaimo Ladysmith Public Schools values the passionate commitment to student learning that is demonstrated by our staff on a daily basis. We also realize that as our students’ learning needs become more complex, we need a structure that unites us in our efforts, facilitates our ability to share innovative, effective practices, and increases our capacity to collectively support student learning. RTI is a framework for organizing schools so that systems exist to ensure that every student receives the time and support they need to be successful. RTI is a process for ensuring higher levels of academic and behavioural success for all students (Hierck & Weber, 2014). It combines extensive effective schools research that identifies teacher collaboration, sharing of effective instructional practices, and problem solving as critical factors for improving student learning. While many initiatives in education have focused on implementing new “programs,” RTI is a framework that empowers educators to align essential learning outcomes, use reflective inquiry to identify the most effective instructional practices, develop a culture of collaboration, and provide proactive interventions to prevent students from falling behind, academically and behaviourally. RTI supports the belief that all students can learn at optimal levels. Terms Belonging to the RTI Framework  Tier 1: We refer to this as Universal Practices. It refers to the high quality instruction provided to all students. Examples of instructional strategies being utilized by our teachers to meet student needs include balanced literacy, real-world learning (inquiry/project-based, trades, apprenticeships), Aboriginal Understandings, Assessment “for,” “as,” and “of” Learning, and Universal Design for Learning (UDL). The district will continue to promote these instructional practices through professional learning experiences and collaboration.  Tier 2: We refer to this as Targeted Support. Despite our best efforts, we will have students who come to us without prerequisite skills or need more time and support to master essential learning outcomes, academically, socially, and behaviourally. Often, Tier 2 intervention (supplemental instruction) is provided in the classroom and possibly by the classroom teacher in small groups. Tier 2 interventions target specific student needs. We monitor students’ response to Tier 2 intervention (approximately every 3-4 weeks as appropriate) to see if the intervention is working or if it needs to be adjusted. It is critical that interventions be connected to Tier 1 classroom practices, and are identified through collaboration with the classroom teacher.  Tier 3: We refer to this as Intensive Intervention. There will be a very small group of students (generally less than 5%) who need intensive support to master essential learning outcomes, academically, socially, and behaviourally. These students may be significantly below grade expectations in basic skills and/or self-regulation. Due to the intensive nature of Tier 3, we may monitor students’ progress every one or two weeks as appropriate, and adjust interventions as necessary to meet the unique needs of the student. 4 Superintendent’s Annual Report on Student Achievement (SD68) 3. Self-Regulated Learning (SRL) Self-regulation is the ability for a learner to perform at an optimal level physically, emotionally, behaviourally, socially and academically. In the school environment, these areas of self-regulation are addressed in order to provide the foundation for optimal academic learning. While addressing each area of self-regulation is critical to optimal learning, it is also imperative that assessment and intervention of these areas be viewed through a tiered Response to Intervention lens. At the school and classroom level, both assessment and intervention begins at the Tier One universal level. Tier Two and Tier Three supports follow, based on the progress within Tier One. In NLPS we are currently growing the awareness, knowledge and practice of self-regulated learning at a practitioner level. Our NLPS SRL Coordinator works within a team approach to build common understandings around the five domains of self-regulated learning. The SRL Coordinator is working towards a cohesive approach with all of our Learning Support Services Teams. 4. District-Wide Assessment Nanaimo Ladysmith Public Schools recognizes that rich, varied assessment processes are crucial to student learning. As a district, it is our collective responsibility to ensure that all students learn at optimal levels. As our district explores the most effective options for the identification of crucial data sets at the district, school, classroom and individual levels; professional dialogue will be key. Formative assessment serves as the foundation of our RTI model monitoring because it enables educators to carefully measure a student’s success in mastering the BC curriculum. Knowing whether a child is on track towards mastering essential grade level outcomes is a key attribute of RTI. This proactive approach empowers educators to develop effective, personalized, instructional, enrichment, and remedial interventions. Fall 2014 marked the implementation of our formative assessment pilot in which five assessment instruments were piloted K-9, district wide. Staff continue to be surveyed to inform recommendations from a district assessment committee. With respect to district-wide literacy assessments, an assessment pilot was completed in the spring of 2014 resulting with the District creating and implementing its’ own K-3 formative reading assessment. Currently, the district is working on creating a Grades 4-9 formative reading assessment, including French Immersion K-3 and 4-9. For this school year, schools will use either the RAD or DART assessments for Grades 49 while awaiting the creation of the district’s own 4-9 reading assessment. We experienced a late start to the school year due to the teachers’ strike but the fall formative assessment process proceeded as planned. Finally, NLPS is working with EDUDATA at UBC to create a versatile database for the formative assessment data so teachers can access specific reports to inform their instruction. 5. Professional Learning Community: A Collaborative Framework RTI requires collaboration between all classroom teachers, administration and support staff. In RTI, this collaboration requires everyone to adopt new ways of thinking, collaborating, and working together as a team to support all learners. This year, NLPS established a Professional Learning Community model (PLC). It has marked the beginning of a new culture of staff collaboration that provides a school-wide focus on student performance while providing time for embedded professional development. The PLC teams meet every other week at each school for 60 minutes. The collective dialogue includes the following:  Focus on ensuring all students learn at a high level  Provide a systematic response to student needs for intervention and enrichment (personalized learning)  Partner together and have a collective responsibility for student learning The RTI framework requires that basic structures and procedures be established in all district schools. Educational teams will have the opportunity to learn and share innovative instructional practices as well as focus on instruction, intervention, and enrichment as we work to meet the needs of our learners. 7. Early Years to Grade 3 (E23) Reading Initiative Nanaimo Ladysmith Public Schools is continuing with a district-wide literacy initiative focused on improving the reading results of our primary students. In the 2012-13 school-year, Nanaimo Ladysmith Public Schools participated in the provincial initiative Changing Results for Young Readers (CR4YR). As a result, we recognized that our efforts had to become more systemic across our school district. We have, therefore, focussed our efforts to improve the reading levels for students in the primary years through targeted professional learning for teachers. Our goal is to ensure that all students will be reading at grade level as they enter Grade 4 In the 2013-14 school year, E23 targets two major areas:   Consistent use of evidence-based practices, and Building a collaborative culture. In Year Two, 2014-15, we have continued to focus on improving the reading levels of our primary students initially identified by the district-wide assessment. More specifically, the sessions will concentrate on Defining what is high quality tier one literacy instruction; Designing our learning environment to promote effective, inclusive literacy structures; and Using assessment to inform our instruction and monitor student achievement. The district will continue to build capacity related to the promotion of evidence-based practices within a collaborative inquiry process. A common assessment tool will be used by all schools in the district during the fall of 2014. This formative assessment tool will provide the evidence necessary to determine where each student needs specific reading instruction. Once the results have been collected and an analysis has been determined, the Early Years to Grade 3 (E23) professional development model will continue to address the specific needs of our learners. Moreover, the district will continue to promote deep understandings related to reading instruction, assessment (for, as, and of learning), differentiated instruction, and the collaborative inquiry process. 6 Superintendent’s Annual Report on Student Achievement (SD68) 8. Learn@Home NLPS continues to support and promote its two distributed learning schools; Learn@Home K-7 and Learn@Home 8-12. These schools continue to serve a growing desire in the district/province for choice and flexibility for students and parents. Learn@Home schools offer a full, comprehensive K-12 selection of courses and are “open” year-round which allows students to enrol, study and gain credit throughout the year. While both schools have continued to gradually increase their student population over the last few years, this year, Learn@Home K-7 has experienced a dramatic 50 percent increase in enrolment. This development brings a variety of new challenges concerning the classroom space and allocation of resources. Learn@Home 8-12 is also experiencing a dramatic change in the 2014-15 school year. Learn@Home 8-12 has changed their delivery model for cross-enrolled students to an “embedded DL teacher model” whereby students can access their DL teacher at their neighbourhood school. DL teachers rotate through each secondary school’s DL support blocks helping students on an “as needs” basis. We are confident this model will improve success rates. 7 Superintendent’s Annual Report on Student Achievement (SD68) 10. International Student Education (ISE) and English Language Learners As the number of International Students and English Language Learners continues to grow in NLPS, we are dedicated to using a variety of teaching techniques to increase achievement of these students. Recently, we have observed a change in the needs of our learners with an increase of International Students and new immigrant families from Mainland China, and are currently developing strategies to support the district initiatives of Balanced Literacy, Universal Design for Learning, and Cultural Understanding. A three-tiered model of RTI for English Learners incorporates “SIOP” (Sheltered Instruction Observation Protocol), a way of teaching that uses strategies and techniques to make instruction comprehensible while at the same time promoting academic language development in the curricular courses. SIOP allows for differentiated instruction based on assessment that identifies the student’s unique language and academic strengths and needs. D. Early Learning In NLPS we recognize that a strong beginning is crucial for every learner, and that we must facilitate a good start by addressing learning long before, and continuing on through that exciting first day of Kindergarten. Our efforts are focused in four areas this year: StrongStart Centres StrongStart facilitators are participating in the second year of the E23 (Early Years to Grade 3) district-wide literacy initiative. Guided by a team of educators that includes Kindergarten teachers, an instructional coordinator, and an administrator, the facilitators are deepening their understanding of early literacy development. In particular, they are exploring shared reading, shared writing and parental involvement in early literacy with an emphasis on literacy through play. This year NLPS strengthened our involvement with GNEYP—Greater Nanaimo Early Years Partnership—by engaging all the StrongStart Facilitators in community working groups designed to address early years issues such as Barrier Reduction, Cultural Engagement, Communication and Early Years Policy. With this alliance we hope to broaden our support to vulnerable families across the city. Ready Set Learn/Healthy Start to Learning We are continuing the Bedtime Shenanigans Ready Set Learn initiative introduced last year. Families of 3 and 4 year olds are welcomed into their neighbourhood schools to promote early learning, physical literacy and connection to our schools. In addition, plans are underway to host our annual Healthy Start to Learning days in both Ladysmith and Nanaimo. Because we know that healthy children are better learners, we organize these Saturday drop-in events to provide opportunities for families to connect with both community health care providers and recreational/fitness groups. These events align with the Comprehensive School Health Initiative. Kindergarten Extending the E23 initiative begun last year, K Teachers, Grade 1 Teachers, School Support Teachers and Principals are joining together for three professional learning sessions this year. Last year we began to build common understandings and promote horizontal alignment around teaching reading and stimulating literacy in a play-based curriculum. This year we are delving into the structures that support balanced literacy in primary classrooms. 11 Superintendent’s Annual Report on Student Achievement (SD68) Qeq College Qeq in Hul’qumi’num means baby.
