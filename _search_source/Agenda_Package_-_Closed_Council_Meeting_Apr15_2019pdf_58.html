INTRODUCTION/BACKGROUND: Page 5 of 35 The Town has received a request from the Stz’uminus First Nation to reconstruct an old logging road in order to access unit D of Woodlot W1713. The old logging road is identified as DR 100 (see Figure 1, attached) and passes through a portion of Town property that has been dedicated as park. The length of road through the Town property is approximately 340 metres. It is not known how long ago the logging road was active but based on trees growing on the fringes of the road it is likely 50 plus years. Stz’uminus’ Woodlot W1713 covers a large area (See Figure 2, attached) and utilizes a number of access points when logging operations are active. The main access road for Unit D is identified as DR 101 and is accessed off Thetis Drive. DR101 is also the main road that the Town uses to access the Stocking Lake Pressure Reducing Valve station as well as the Stocking Lake balancing reservoir. Utilizing DR101 as the access point for logging trucks is not desirable as they will travel through a residential area of the Town. In addition, the alignment of the access on Thetis Drive has insufficient room to allow trucks to swing in and out of the entrance. The SFN estimate that construction of the road would take two to three months to complete. During this time, there would be no public access to the road in the park. Active use of the road for hauling during the logging season would be for a further two to three months in 2019, although this will not require a closure of the trail or the park. Current plans are that the SFN would maintain the road indefinitely for long-term woodlot and recreation access. Vehicle access would be restricted by gate or barricade. In addition, regulations required that public access be restricted for falling of any trees within two trees’ lengths of a park or public space. While not directly related to the road request, this does mean that the Stirling Park and the Stocking Creek Trail would be closed temporarily while active logging is taking place in Woodlot W1713. Town staff received the same application request from Stz’uminus in December of 2013 and although there was discussion between the applicant and Town staff at that time, the applicant did not move forward. Apparently some nests were found in one of the logging areas that required further study, this work has now been done and the application is again moving forward. Park Area The park area was acquired by the Town in 2008 and was dedicated during subdivision of the Stirling Road area (see Figure 3). There is a Town-owned hydro and gas right-of-way through the park that is not dedicated as park. The park area is undeveloped with a number of unofficial trails. The hydro/gas right-of-way has a gravel access road along its length that is utilized by the public for walking, biking and motorized ATV’s. Its actual intended purpose is to provide maintenance access for hydro and gas services. The old logging road alignment crosses through the park area approximately Page 6 of 35 parallel to and west of the hydro/gas right-of-way. The old road alignment is also currently used as a recreation trail. This park is defined in the 2016 Parks, Recreation and Culture Master Plan as an Open Space, which is defined as a site for the protection and stewardship of wildlife, habitat and other natural systems. These spaces are accessible for low impact use with minimal infrastructure on the site in order to not adversely affect the local habitat or natural systems. Development Permit Area The trail is located within DPA 6 – Riparian which will require the issuance of a development permit prior to the commencement of the work. Econ is aware of this requirement. Council has delegated to staff the issuance of this type of development permit. If Council is willing to consider the logging road to be constructed on the 340m section of DR100 within the Park, a Park Use Permit would be the means to do so. Staff recommend that legal counsel assist in the preparation of the permit. The purpose of the permit would be to allow road construction and logging truck access to Unit D of Woodlot W1713, subject to terms and conditions. The Parks Use Permit could be limited to a one-year period and it could be renewed on request of Stz’uminus. Further terms and conditions could be added. Indemnification  Obtain insurance satisfactory to the Town and indemnify the Town in the event of any injury, loss or damage ALTERNATIVES: Council can choose to: a) Direct staff to work with legal counsel to prepare a one-year, renewable, Parks Use Permit authorizing the construction and use of a logging road with conditions as generally outlined in this staff report, subject to receipt of a Band Council resolution from Stz’uminus First Nation that it will actively support the Town to obtain an agreement for the long-term use of existing roads in Woodlot W1713 for access to the Stz’uminus Woodlot and the Town’s infrastructure and for public recreation purposes. b) Direct staff to bring the draft Parks Use permit back to Council for consideration prior to considering approval; c) Refer the request to the Parks, Recreation and Culture Advisory Committee for comment prior to approving or not approving; d) Request Stz’uminus to provide more information about the alternative route. e) Not approve this request. FINANCIAL IMPLICATIONS; N/A LEGAL IMPLICATIONS; Staff have reviewed the matter with legal counsel. While there doesn’t seem to be a case that exactly matches this request, a case was reviewed that has some similarities. The view of legal Page 9 of 35 counsel is that the Town can likely issue a parks use permit, but the Town could get challenged in court, if members of the community (such as the people currently using the road as trail) are opposed and feel strongly about the issue to commence a legal challenge. Where the question is whether a specific activity or use should be permitted within a park on a temporary or intermittent basis (i.e. there is no intention to sell or otherwise dispose of the land, or to remove the dedication of the land as park), electoral approval is not a requirement, but Council would need to be satisfied that the use is compatible with the park purpose for which land is held. If this proposal was to go ahead, Council would definitely want a formal agreement with the First Nation that places limits on the use of the road, in terms of days or times, number of trucks or loads permitted, requirements for advance notice, the First Nation’s obligation to obtain insurance and to indemnify the Town in the event of any injury, loss or damage, and so on, and at the same time the Town would need to consider how to keep pedestrians and other users off the road and out of harm’s way when the road was being used for logging purposes. CITIZEN/PUBLIC RELATIONS IMPLICATIONS: Council has worked hard to establish a positive, productive relationship with the Stz’uminus First Nation. Not approving the request could be viewed in a negative light by the SFN. Having an active logging road through dedicated park land could create concern and opposition among users to the park who value the wilderness area. In discussions with legal counsel, we have been advised that a formal electoral approval process is not required; however, Council could consider inviting input from the community, or through a Council advisory body such as the Parks, Recreation and Culture Advisory Committee. INTERDEPARTMENTAL INVOLVEMENT/IMPLICATIONS: Both the Parks Department and Public Works have a role in this project.
