Response to Intervention (RTI) Framework Nanaimo Ladysmith Public Schools values the passionate commitment to student learning that is demonstrated by our staff on a daily basis. We also realize that as our students’ learning needs become more complex, we need a structure that unites us in our efforts, facilitates our ability to share innovative, effective practices, and increases our capacity to collectively support student learning. RTI is a framework for organizing schools so that systems exist to ensure that every student receives the time and support they need to be successful. RTI is a process for ensuring higher levels of academic and behavioural success for all students (Hierck & Weber, 2014). It combines extensive effective schools research that identifies teacher collaboration, sharing of effective instructional practices, and problem solving as critical factors for improving student learning. While many initiatives in education have focused on implementing new “programs,” RTI is a framework that empowers educators to align essential learning outcomes, use reflective inquiry to identify the most effective instructional practices, develop a culture of collaboration, and provide proactive interventions to prevent students from falling behind, academically and behaviourally. RTI supports the belief that all students can learn at optimal levels. Terms Belonging to the RTI Framework  Tier 1: We refer to this as Universal Practices. It refers to the high quality instruction provided to all students. Examples of instructional strategies being utilized by our teachers to meet student needs include balanced literacy, real-world learning (inquiry/project-based, trades, apprenticeships), Aboriginal Understandings, Assessment “for,” “as,” and “of” Learning, and Universal Design for Learning (UDL). The district will continue to promote these instructional practices through professional learning experiences and collaboration.  Tier 2: We refer to this as Targeted Support. Despite our best efforts, we will have students who come to us without prerequisite skills or need more time and support to master essential learning outcomes, academically, socially, and behaviourally. Often, Tier 2 intervention (supplemental instruction) is provided in the classroom and possibly by the classroom teacher in small groups. Tier 2 interventions target specific student needs. We monitor students’ response to Tier 2 intervention (approximately every 3-4 weeks as appropriate) to see if the intervention is working or if it needs to be adjusted. It is critical that interventions be connected to Tier 1 classroom practices, and are identified through collaboration with the classroom teacher.  Tier 3: We refer to this as Intensive Intervention. There will be a very small group of students (generally less than 5%) who need intensive support to master essential learning outcomes, academically, socially, and behaviourally. These students may be significantly below grade expectations in basic skills and/or self-regulation. Due to the intensive nature of Tier 3, we may monitor students’ progress every one or two weeks as appropriate, and adjust interventions as necessary to meet the unique needs of the student. 4 Superintendent’s Annual Report on Student Achievement (SD68) Page 27 of 65 12.1 3. Self-Regulated Learning (SRL) Self-regulation is the ability for a learner to perform at an optimal level physically, emotionally, behaviourally, socially and academically. In the school environment, these areas of self-regulation are addressed in order to provide the foundation for optimal academic learning. While addressing each area of self-regulation is critical to optimal learning, it is also imperative that assessment and intervention of these areas be viewed through a tiered Response to Intervention lens. At the school and classroom level, both assessment and intervention begins at the Tier One universal level. Tier Two and Tier Three supports follow, based on the progress within Tier One. In NLPS we are currently growing the awareness, knowledge and practice of self-regulated learning at a practitioner level. Our NLPS SRL Coordinator works within a team approach to build common understandings around the five domains of self-regulated learning. The SRL Coordinator is working towards a cohesive approach with all of our Learning Support Services Teams. 4. District-Wide Assessment Nanaimo Ladysmith Public Schools recognizes that rich, varied assessment processes are crucial to student learning. As a district, it is our collective responsibility to ensure that all students learn at optimal levels. As our district explores the most effective options for the identification of crucial data sets at the district, school, classroom and individual levels; professional dialogue will be key. Formative assessment serves as the foundation of our RTI model monitoring because it enables educators to carefully measure a student’s success in mastering the BC curriculum. Knowing whether a child is on track towards mastering essential grade level outcomes is a key attribute of RTI. This proactive approach empowers educators to develop effective, personalized, instructional, enrichment, and remedial interventions. Fall 2014 marked the implementation of our formative assessment pilot in which five assessment instruments were piloted K-9, district wide. Staff continue to be surveyed to inform recommendations from a district assessment committee. With respect to district-wide literacy assessments, an assessment pilot was completed in the spring of 2014 resulting with the District creating and implementing its’ own K-3 formative reading assessment.
