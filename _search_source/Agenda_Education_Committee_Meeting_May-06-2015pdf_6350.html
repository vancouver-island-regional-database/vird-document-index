Page 11 of 40 7.2 NLPS International Baccalaureate (IB) Primary Years Programme (PYP) The International Baccalaureate: The International Baccalaureate (IB) was founded in 1968 as a non-profit organization which currently works with over 3,500 schools in 144 countries to develop and offer challenging programmes to over one million students worldwide. “The IB aspires to help schools develop well-rounded students with character who respond to challenges with optimism and an open mind, are confident in their own identities, make ethical decisions, join with others in celebrating our common humanity and are prepared to apply what they learn in real-world, complex and unpredictable situations. The IB offers high-quality programmes of international education that share a powerful vision. Informed by the values described in the learner profile, an IB education:  Focuses on learners - the IB’s student-centred programmes promote healthy relationships, ethical responsibility and personal challenge.  Develops effective approaches to teaching and learning – IB programmes help students to develop the attitudes and skills they need for both academic and personal success.  Works within global contexts - IB programmes increase understanding of languages and cultures, and explore globally significant ideas and issues.  Explores significant content - IB programmes offer a curriculum that is broad and balanced, conceptual and connected. The aim of all IB programmes is to develop internationally minded people who, recognizing their common humanity and shared guardianship of the planet, help to create a better and more peaceful world. IB learners strive to become:  inquirers  open-minded  thinkers  principled  communicators  caring  risk-takers  reflective  knowledgeable  balanced These attributes represent a broad range of human capacities and responsibilities that go beyond intellectual development and academic success.” (Source: Making the PYP Happen) Page 12 of 40 7.2 What is the Primary Years Programme? The Primary Years Programme (PYP) is the first of four programmes designed for students aged 5 to 12. As a curriculum framework, it focusses on the development of the whole child as an inquirer, both in the classroom and in the world outside. Knowledge in the curriculum is defined by six transdisciplinary themes of global significance. These themes incorporate six subject areas (language, mathematics, science, social studies, arts, and personal, social and physical education) that are explored through an inquiry-based pedagogy. To engage and challenge students’ with each transdisciplinary theme, concepts are explored to promote an in-depth understanding of each theme within a local and global context. The use of each conceptual lens requires the development of various approaches to learning such as thinking, social, communication, self-management and research skills. These elements contribute towards fostering positive student attitudes towards people, their environment and most importantly learning. The building of knowledge, concepts, skills, and attitudes result is an explicit expectation of the PYP which is that successful inquiry leads to responsible action, initiated by the student as a result of the learning process. “What then is a PYP school? It is a school that, regardless of location, size or composition, strives towards developing an internationally minded person” (MTPYPH, p.3). Page 13 of 40 7.2 What qualities of our current NLPS program does the PYP enhance? NLPS offers excellent teaching and learning across the district. The PYP however, brings together some common qualities that concentrate these evidence based practices into one distinct framework. The aim of the IB is to offer pedagogy that models best practices, yet remains flexible enough to fit with most school systems. Student programming would look rather different from our existing model. First, the curriculum is more clearly articulated to parents and students; it is clearly understood by all staff and revolves around five essential elements. These elements (also listed above) are knowledge, concepts, approaches to learning, and attitudes and action. These essential elements all fall under the umbrella of the IB Learner Profile which ties together the definition of what makes an internationally minded person. These attributes represent a broad range of human capacities and responsibilities that go beyond intellectual development and academic success. Despite the programming differences, the BC Curriculum coordinates well with the Units of Inquiry. Furthermore, IB requires a standard of collaborative planning across all grades. This promotes a strong understanding of the student programme by all staff and a system wide understanding of the importance of assessment and evaluation that must be articulated in a school assessment policy as a requirement of the IB. One major difference in student programming is in the final year of the PYP programme when students complete the PYP Exhibition. This showcase of learning serves as the ultimate culminating task in which students conduct their own inquiry and present their learning of not only their inquiries but their understanding of the PYP to the school community. Other qualities of the PYP curriculum are:         Global perspectives Globally relevant content Inquiry-based learning Concept-based learning Focus on development of responsible citizenship Collaborative approach to planning of instruction Development of “International-Mindedness” through the IB Learner Profile A global architecture for professional development which includes online, regional and global workshop models How will it benefit NLPS? The immediate benefit of implementing the PYP will be in enrollment. Statistics have shown that Independent School enrollment has climbed steadily by approximately 5% per year while public school enrollment across most of B.C. is in decline. In addition, offering such a programme will create other options for parents and provide some relief for the rapidly growing demands for French immersion programs. Thirdly, the IB is a highly recognized name worldwide by both parents and universities. How much will it cost? The costs related to the programme are two-fold. First are fees that are paid directly to the IB on a yearly basis. Considerations  MDI participation would further tax both Grade 4 and Grade 7 classrooms where learning time is already lost to Ministry Satisfaction Survey participation.  MDI participation would further tax both Grade 4 and Grade 7 classrooms where learning time is already lost to FSA participation.  MDI participation requires a teacher or principal to supervise the distribution and completion of the survey. All of this data is utilized in different ways by a number of our staff and schools. Some of the MDI survey items are of interest to the Department of Learning Services. As a district, we are able to add questions to the Ministry Satisfaction Survey, therefore, we could potentially identify a few add-on questions for each grade level gleaned from the MDI. The one-time cost of adding these questions would allow us to track students and schools over time on issues related to our goals and the strategic plan. Recommendation On behalf of the Department of Learning Services, we recommend to the Board that NLPS does not participate in the MDI at this time. Participation could be revisited in the future when a more thorough comprehensive plan is developed which emphasizes a need for the MDI data. They recognize a significant overhaul is required to ensure leading practices are followed. The overhaul may include: changing the name of MRRC to reflect current language; potentially splitting Multicultural from Race Relations, include anti-bullying & other such topics; and, re-envisioning the role of the committee to reflect a new need.
