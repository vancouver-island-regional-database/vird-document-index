BETWEEN: THE BOARD OF EDUCATION OF SCHOOL DISTRICT NO. 68 (NANAIMO-LADYSMITH) (Hereinafter called the “Board”) AND: NANAIMO-LADYSMITH SCHOOLS FOUNDATION (Hereinafter called the “NLSF”) Background: Since 1982, the Board has enjoyed a mutually supportive relationship with the Nanaimo Schools Financial Awards Society that, in 2008, became the Nanaimo-Ladysmith Schools Foundation (NLSF). The NLSF is a legally incorporated, non-profit entity. The NLSF, with the single purpose of supporting students served by the Board, is led by a representative board of community members and is governed by a constitution and bylaws. This Memorandum of Agreement formalizes a mutually beneficial relationship that has existed between the Board and the NLSF since the foundation’s inception. It details the principles and commitments that each organization makes to the other, and will be reviewed every 3 years or at the request of either party. Guiding Principles: 1. 2. 3. 4. To work positively together to achieve a student-centered mission. In other words, both parties will share a vision where all students enrolled with the Board experience programs and support aimed at maximizing their potential. To operate as collaborative partners whereby communication is open, regular, and respectful. The results of this regular consultation will be shared openly with the public in order to ensure that our communities are kept informed of joint efforts programs, and student successes. To share expertise and advice with one another in order to maximize the goals of both parties and to carry out the respective commitment identified under this agreement. To support a wide range of programming and to engage in public relations efforts to support student success and to strengthen the mission of both parties. Under the new policy, schools may provide a maximum of $600 for each full time public or independent DL student in Kindergarten through Grade 12. Exceptions to this cap are: a. supporting students with special needs in levels 1, 2 and 3; and categories K,P,Q,R, in achieving the goals in their Individual Education Plans; b. career technical centre program courses; c. post secondary transition program courses and; d. a portion of Internet access costs if required to participate in a DL program or course. Many distributed learning (DL) schools around the province provide resource/service allocation funds so that DL students have access to educational services that are not easily delivered through DL. For example, a brick and mortar Physical Education curriculum might include swimming, while a Fine Arts curriculum could include a music program. Third-party services enable a DL student to access similar learning, e.g. swimming or music lessons. In practice, access to third-party services has led to competition between schools for DL students based on the amount of money provided for third-party resources/services. The amount DL schools provide for third party services has traditionally not been set by the Ministry of Education in either public or independent schools. While DL schools do not pay parents directly, many DL schools (public and independent) offer up to $1,200 in “resource/service credits” for enrolled students in grades K—9. These credits support schoolapproved third-party services and resources that assist students in meeting Ministry curricular outcomes. We also realize that as our students’ learning needs become more complex, we need a structure that unites us in our efforts, facilitates our ability to share innovative, effective practices, and increases our capacity to collectively support students’ learning. Response to Intervention is a framework that focuses on collaborative problem solving to improve all students’ learning and to close achievement gaps for struggling learners. It combines extensive effective schools research that identifies teacher collaboration, sharing of effective instructional practices, and problem solving as critical factors for improving student learning. While many initiatives in education have focused on implementing new “programs,” RTI is a framework that empowers educators to align essential learning outcomes, use reflective inquiry to identify the most effective instruction practices, develop a culture of collaboration, and provide proactive interventions to prevent students from falling behind, academically and behaviourally. RTI supports the belief that all students can learn at optimal levels. Terms Belonging to the RTI Framework • Tier 1: We refer to this as Universal Practices. It refers to the high quality instruction provided to all students. Examples of instructional strategies being utilized by our teachers to meet student needs include balanced literacy, real-world learning (inquiry/project-based, trades, apprenticeships), Aboriginal Understandings, Assessment “for,” “as,” and “of” Learning, and Universal Design for Learning (UDL). The district will continue to promote these instructional practices through professional learning experiences and collaboration. • Tier 2: We refer to this as Targeted Support. Despite our best efforts, we will have students who come to us without prerequisite skills or need more time and support to master essential learning outcomes, academically, socially, and behaviourally. Often, Tier 2 intervention (supplemental instruction) is provided in the classroom and possibly by the classroom teacher in small groups. Tier 2 interventions target specific student needs. We monitor students’ response to Tier 2 intervention (approximately every 3-4 weeks as appropriate) to see if the intervention is working or if it needs to be adjusted. It is critical that interventions be connected to Tier 1 classroom practices, and are identified through collaboration with the classroom teacher. • Tier 3: We refer to this as Intensive Intervention. There will be a very small group of students (generally less than 5%) who need intensive support to master essential learning outcomes, academically, socially, and behaviourally. These students may be significantly below grade expectations in basic skills and/or self-regulation. Due to the intensive nature of Tier 3, we may monitor students’ progress every one or two weeks as appropriate, and adjust interventions as necessary to meet the unique needs of the student. Time to Collaborate RTI requires collaboration between all classroom teachers, administration and support staff. In RTI, this collaboration requires everyone to adopt new ways of thinking, collaborating, and working together as a team to support all learners. 5 Superintendent’s Annual Report on Student Achievement (SD68) Page 175 of 208 13.1 Additionally, it may mean that educators need to assume new identities that are not based on their role descriptions or funding source, but rather how their professional expertise can best help students meet the learning outcomes. The RTI framework requires that basic structures and procedures be established in all district schools and not simply left to the willing. Every one of our learners, in every single classroom, deserves the opportunity to learn in innovative, collaborative environments. Self-Regulated Learning Self-regulation is the ability for a learner to perform at an optimal level physically, emotionally, behaviourally, socially and academically. In the school environment, these areas of self-regulation are addressed in order to provide the foundation for optimal academic learning. While addressing each area of self-regulation is critical to optimal learning, it is also imperative that assessment and intervention of these areas be viewed through a tiered Response to Intervention lens. At the school and classroom level, both assessment and intervention begins at the Tier One universal level. Tier Two and Tier Three supports follow, based on the progress within Tier One.
